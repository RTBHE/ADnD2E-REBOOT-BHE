# Player Character Races
After creating your character's ability scores, you must select a player character race. This is not a race in the true sense of the word, It is a fantasy species for your character - human, elf, dwarf, gnome, half-elf, halfling, etc. Each have their own advantages and hindrances and they determine what classes you may choose from.

Many races and sub-races are described in this chapter. In many cases, broad descriptive statements are made concerning the races. Players are not bound by these generalities. For example, a statement that "dwarves tend to be dour and taciturn" does not mean that your character cannot be a jolly dwarf. It means that the 'average' dwarf you will encounter is dour and taciturn. If player characters were just like everyone else, they wouldn't be adventurers. Make your character unique and he will be more fun to play.
## List of Playable Races
The following list gives the published races and sub-races - your DM may offer additional choices, or limit this list to fit their campaign.
### Humans and Demi-Humans
#### Dwarves
- **Hill Dwarf** – The most commonly encountered dwarves, adaptable surface-and-underground kin known for hardy builds, practical craft, and close ties to human lands.
- **Deep Dwarf** – Reclusive underworld dwellers shaped by extreme depths, pragmatic and conservative guardians of dwarven tradition adapted to the deep earth.
- **Gray Dwarf (Duergar)** – Harsh subterranean dwarves of the Underdark, defined by ruthless survival, dark magic, and deep enmity with their kin.
- **Gully Dwarf** – Marginalized scavenger-dwarves of ruins and refuse, surviving through opportunism, ignorance, and chaotic adaptability.
- **Mountain Dwarf** – Isolated deep-mountain dwarves of strong tradition and pride, valuing privacy, lineage, and ancient subterranean strongholds.
- **Sundered Dwarf** – Surface-dwelling refugees of lost underground realms, carrying the trauma of displacement while maintaining fractured dwarven traditions.
#### Elves
- **High Elf** – The most commonly encountered elves, balancing tradition with engagement among other peoples and often serving as intermediaries between cultures.
- **Dark Elf (Drow)** – Subterranean elves of the Underdark, defined by ruthless hierarchy, intrigue, and mastery of shadowed magic.
- **Grey Elf** – The most aloof and tradition-bound elves, defined by rigid hierarchy, immense scholarship, and cultural superiority.
- **Sea Elf** – Aquatic elves of coral cities and open seas, serving as guardians of the oceans and its living balance.
- **Sylvan Elf** – Instinct-driven forest elves who live in deep harmony with nature, favoring immediacy and territorial guardianship over formal structure.
#### Gnomes
- **Rock Gnome** — Curious artisan-explorers who study the material world through craft, invention, and subtle experimentation.
- **Deep Gnome (Svirfneblin)** — Hardened subterranean gnomes whose curiosity is expressed through disciplined survival science in the hostile Underdark.
- **Forest Gnome** — Reclusive woodland dwellers who rely on concealment, environmental attunement, and subtle manipulation of natural systems.
- **Tinker Gnome** — Relentless inventors driven by elaborate, unpredictable engineering and a philosophy that nothing is ever truly finished.
#### Half-Elves
#### Halflings
- **Hairfoot** – The most common halflings, adaptable pastoral folk who integrate easily into human society and rural trade networks.
- **Furchin** – Cold-climate nomads who survive through endurance, hunting, and migration across arctic and highland regions.
- **Hardfell** – Insular survival-focused halflings of harsh terrain who prioritise group continuity and environmental mastery over comfort.
- **Stout** – Dense, industrious halflings closely allied with dwarves, known for resilience, craftsmanship, and subterranean communities.
- **Tallfellow** – Elven-aligned woodland halflings noted for height, agility, forestry skills, and spear-based light cavalry traditions.
- **Kender** – Curious, fearless wanderers whose instinctive mischief and disregard for ownership set them apart from all other halflings.

## Class Restrictions and Advancement Thresholds
Humans possess a unique advantage in AD&D: they may pursue any character class and advance without restriction. Demihuman races, by contrast, have more limited class choices and progress more slowly beyond certain advancement thresholds.

These class restrictions reflect the natural tendencies of the races (dwarves favor warfare and craftsmanship over magic, for example), while advancement thresholds exist primarily for game balance. 

A demihuman character advances normally according to the experience requirements of their class until reaching the advancement threshold listed for their race and class on the table below.

Exceptional aptitude may increase this threshold. If a character possesses a score above 15 in every prime requisite ability of their class, their advancement threshold increases by 1 for each point by which their _lowest_ prime requisite exceeds 15.

Once a character advances beyond their threshold, all further level advancement in that class requires twice the normal experience cost, in addition to any racial or other experience penalties.

| Race     | Bard | Cleric | Druid | Fighter | Mage | Paladin | Ranger | Thief |
| -------- | ---- | ------ | ----- | ------- | ---- | ------- | ------ | ----- |
| Human    | U    | U      | U     | U       | U    | U       | U      | U     |
| Dwarf    | -    | 10     | -     | 15      | -    | -       | -      | 12    |
| Elf      | -    | 12     | -     | 12      | 15   | -       | 15     | 12    |
| Gnome    | -    | 9      | -     | 11      | -    | -       | -      | 13    |
| Half-Elf | U    | 14     | 9     | 14      | 12   | -       | 16     | 12    |
| Halfling | -    | 8      | -     | 9       | -    | -       | -      | 15    |

**Note:** U - Unlimited Levels in this class
#### Example
A human fighter normally attains 12th level at 1,000,000 XP and reaches 13th level at 1,250,000 XP.

A deep gnome fighter with Strength 16 has an advancement threshold of 11, as shown on the table above. Since Strength is the fighter’s only prime requisite, the character’s Strength increases this threshold to 12.

Deep gnomes also suffer a 10% racial experience penalty. As a result, the character attains 12th level after earning 1,100,000 XP.

Advancing beyond the threshold requires double the normal experience cost. Instead of the usual 250,000 XP needed to progress from 12th to 13th level, the deep gnome must earn 500,000 XP, which is then increased to 550,000 XP after applying the racial penalty.

The character therefore reaches 13th level at a total of 1,650,000 XP.
## Languages
Regardless of race all PCs begin the game knowing 'Common', a trade language or *lingua franca*, which allows for some basic level of communication with at least some NPCs across races and regions. The complexity of communication that can be conducted in common depends on the individuals involved - individuals who speak different, but broadly similar languages, will likely be able to carry on moderately complex discussions in common, while speakers of dissimilar languages may struggle through a very rudimentary exchange. Among PC party members it is assumed that high complexity discussions can usually be conducted.

In addition to Common, all PCs begin the game knowing a native language - the one they grew up with; for humans this is likely, though not always, a language tied to the region or country they are from, while for non-humans it may be a language shared by members of that race or subrace across regions . Work with your DM to decide the native language that makes sense for your PC - perhaps they grew up in a religious community or ethnic enclave that speaks a language not tied to the region. While a shared language often allows for highly complex communication bear in mind it does not always mean speakers will understand each other perfectly as one may speak a dialect that is vastly different from the other (consider Cajun French vs. Quebecois French vs. Parisian French).

Additional languages may be learned using proficiency slots (see [[Chapter 5 - Proficiencies]]) at character creation and as you progress in level. At character creation the DM may limit the choice and/or number of languages your PC plausibly had an opportunity to learn - for non-human characters additional racial languages should be limited to a) those given in the race description, typically the languages of allied races or sworn enemies, or b) a regional language, unless discussed with the DM.

## Race Descriptions
### Dwarves
Dwarves encompass several distinct subraces, yet share enough traits to be readily recognized as kin. Physically, they are uniformly short and broad of build, and—barring misadventure—long-lived, often enduring for several centuries. In temperament, dwarves tend toward a dour and taciturn disposition, even when enjoying the strong brews for which they are renowned.

Dwarves favor the solidity of stone and earth over the uncertainty of the sea; while not all dwell beneath the mountains, few are comfortable far from firm ground. They possess a deep affinity for the treasures of the earth—stone, gems, and especially precious metals such as gold—and are tireless in their pursuit and mastery of these materials.

Dwarven societies are most commonly organized along clan lines—extended kinship groups bound by ancestry, oath, and shared craft. These clans form the backbone of dwarven culture, preserving traditions, regulating conduct, and maintaining complex hierarchies of duty and authority. Loyalty to clan and kin is paramount, and it is within this structure that most dwarves find purpose and identity. This strong cultural emphasis on order, obligation, and continuity contributes to the tendency of many dwarves toward lawful alignments.

Though the long-standing frictions between grumpy dwarves and aloof elves are widely known, it is orcs and goblinoids whom dwarves most fiercely oppose. They also share a particular enmity with giant-kin, whose incursions into dwarven mines and strongholds are met with relentless resistance.

Dwarves are generally ill-disposed toward arcane magic and show little aptitude for its practice; whether by nature or long tradition, this aversion is matched by a notable resistance to magic and to poison alike. Instead, dwarves excel in the practical and scientific arts—engineering, metallurgy, and craftsmanship—and, through discipline and industry, their works rival the wonders wrought by magic in other lands. To dwarves, even warfare is a craft, pursued with the same discipline and rigor as their forges and workshops.

#### Common Characteristics of Dwarves
Unless otherwise noted in a subrace description, all dwarves share the following characteristics:
##### Available Classes
Dwarven characters may be clerics, fighters, or thieves, and may multi-class as fighter/clerics or fighter/thieves.

Rarely, a dwarf may be a bard or fighter/bard; however, such characters must select a kit from a restricted list and do not gain access to arcane spellcasting as other bards do.

A dwarven fighter/cleric may elect to function as a demi-paladin, following the rules presented after the paladin class description.
##### Languages
Each dwarven subrace speaks its own dialect, all derived from a single ancient Dwarven tongue. While differences between these dialects may cause some misunderstanding, dwarves of different subraces are generally intelligible to one another, **with the notable exception of the Gray Dwarves (Duergar)**.

Though the Duergar language shares the same roots, it has become a corrupted and heavily altered form. Like the Duergar themselves, it has diverged so far from its origins that even those familiar with the ancient Dwarven language would recognize little more than a handful of words.

Dwarves also find it useful to learn the languages of both allies and enemies. Each subrace description lists commonly known languages that are appropriate for a character to select using proficiency slots during character creation.
##### Dwarven Resistance
As already stated, dwarves are by nature nonmagical (despite some subraces having *some* innate magic) and never use arcane spells and also have exceptional resistance to toxic substances. This gives a bonus to dwarves' saving throws against attacks from magical wands, staves, rods, and spells and saving throws against poison. This bonus is +1 for every 3.5 points of Constitution score. Thus, for example, if a dwarf has a Constitution score of 7 he gains +2 on these saving throws. These bonuses are summarized on the following table:

| Constitution Score | Saving Throw Bonus |
| ------------------ | ------------------ |
| 4-6                | +1                 |
| 7-10               | +2                 |
| 11-13              | +3                 |
| 14-17              | +4                 |
| 18-19              | +5                 |

##### Magical Malfunction
Also because of their nonmagical nature, however, dwarves have trouble using magical items. All magical items that are not specifically for use by the character's class have a 20% chance to malfunction - often dramatically, not simply fails to function - when used by a dwarf. This check is made each time a dwarf uses a magical item. A malfunction affects only the current use; the item may work properly next time. For devices that are continually in operation, the check is made the first time the device is used during an encounter. If the check is passed, the device functions normally until the item is removed or until the next encounter begins. Thus, a dwarf would have to check upon donning a _robe of blending_ but would not check again until he had taken the robe off and then put it on again. If a cursed item malfunctions, the character recognizes its cursed nature and can dispose of the item. Malfunctions apply to rods, staves, wands, rings, amulets, potions, horns, jewels, and all other magical items **except** weapons, shields, armor, gauntlets, and girdles. This penalty does not apply to dwarven clerics using priest items.
##### Racial Enmities
In melee, dwarves add 1 to their dice rolls to hit the hated orcs and goblinoids.  When giant-kin (including ogres, and trolls) or titans attack dwarves, these monsters must subtract 4 from their attack rolls because of the dwarves' small size and combat ability against these much bigger creatures. The DM may substitute other racial enemies as appropriate to the campaign setting.
##### Infravision
Dwarven infravision enables them to see up to 60 feet in the dark.
##### Dwarven Detection
Dwarves are miners of great skill. While underground, they can detect the following information when within 10 feet of the particular phenomenon (and can determine their approximate depth below the surface at any time).

|Description|Roll 1d6|
|-|-|
|Detect grade or slope in passage| 1-5|
|Detect new tunnel/passage construction| 1-5|
|Detect sliding/shifting walls or rooms| 1-4|
|Detect stonework traps, pits, and deadfalls| 1-3|
|Determine approximate depth underground| 1-3|

Note that the dwarf must deliberately try to make these determinations; the information does not simply spring to mind unbidden.

### Dwarven Subraces
#### Hill Dwarf
Most humans, when they think of dwarves, envision the hill dwarf. So named due to their preference for living in areas of rolling hills, they are the most commonly encountered dwarves in most human lands. Though their strongholds are primarily located underground, they frequently have outposts on the surface.

A typical hill dwarf stands 4 feet tall and weighs about 150 pounds. He is stocky and muscular. His skin is a deep tan or light brown in colour and he has ruddy cheeks and bright eyes. His hair is typically black, grey, or brown. He favours dark, sombre, earth-toned clothes, and wears little jewellery.

Hill dwarves have adapted well to life both above and below ground. They claim that they have always lived in the hills, but they may have migrated there either by travelling above ground, or via underground passages. If by surface travel, they are probably descended (literally) from mountain dwarves.

The alignment of hill dwarves tends towards lawful good, but strongholds of chaotic, neutral, or rarely, evil dwarves are known to exist.

**Ability Score Adjustment**: +1 Constitution, -1 Charisma <br/>
**Frequently Known Languages**: Dwarven (Hill), Common, Gnomish, Goblin, Kobold, Orc

#### Deep Dwarf
Deep dwarves live far beneath the surface of the earth. They may always have lived there, or they may have gone deep underground to escape a dreadful cataclysm, marauding monsters, or perhaps were driven downward by their mountain or hill dwarf cousins.

Deep dwarves are large boned, but leaner than other dwarves. Their skin varies from pale brown to light tan, and often carries a reddish tinge. Their eyes are large, but without the sheen of their cousins nearer the surface; in color, a washed-out blue. Hair color ranges from flame red to straw blond. The females wear their beards long, unlike other dwarf women (who are typically clean-shaven).

Deep dwarves have little contact with the surface. The journey is long, and they have no desire to be influenced by surface cultures. Their relations with hill and mountain dwarves vary—some maintain cautious alliances, while others harbor long-standing grudges, viewing their kin as tainted by outside contact.

While tending towards neutral alignment, deep dwarves may also be lawful neutral and sometimes lawful good. They are just as conservative as hill or mountain dwarves, and consider themselves to be the sole repositories of dwarven culture.

**Ability Score Adjustment**: +2 Constitution, -2 Charisma <br/>
**Frequently Known Languages**: Dwarven (Deep), Duergar, Drow, Illithid, Kuo-toa, Troll, Troglodyte, Svirfneblin, Undercommon, Sign Language <br/>
**Infravision**: 120 feet <br/>
**Dwarven Resistance**: gains an additional +1 bonus as deep dwarves are especially resistant to magic and poisons. <br/>
**Light Sensitivity**: Deep dwarves suffer a -1 penalty to all rolls in bright sunlight or within the radius of a *continual light* spell. A standard *light* spell does not however have this effect. <br/>
**Experience Penalty** Requires +10% experience points to gain a new level.

#### Gray Dwarf (Duergar)
Gray dwarves, also known as Duergar, dwell at depths equal to or even greater than those of the deep dwarves. They rarely venture to the surface, finding bright light painful, and then only under cover of night or heavy cloud. Though light does not harm them directly, it significantly impairs their vision.

Duergar frequently compete with deep dwarves for territory and resources, and—largely due to internal strife—seldom prevail in such conflicts. As a result, their strongholds are often impoverished, established in regions other dwarves have deemed unsuitable. In rare cases, however, this has proven advantageous, as the Duergar have uncovered obscure or unrecognized resources which they are able to exploit in secrecy.

Widespread hardship has left many Duergar gaunt and emaciated, their pallid skin and white or dull gray beards lending them a sickly appearance. Both males and females may be naturally bald; those who are not commonly shave their heads.

Most Duergar are of lawful evil alignment, though some exhibit neutral tendencies. Other dwarves find their ways deeply repugnant. Duergar frequently make war upon their kindred and have been known to ally with orcs and other evil races in raids against dwarven strongholds.

Some believe that the Duergar one time lived among other dwarves but were driven into the deep for their worship of evil gods. Others believe they may have been created by the evil gods as a check on the other dwarves who are rarely evil. If that is the case, they may have a divine mission to eradicate or enslave all dwarves of good alignment.

Although their society is evil, they still retain many traditional dwarven structures; they remain clan based, though their crafts are usually inferior to those of other dwarves

**Ability Score Adjustment**: +1 Constitution, -2 Charisma <br/>
**Frequently Known Languages**: Duergar, Dwarven (Deep), Drow, Illithid, Kuo-toa, Troll, Troglodyte, Ghoul, Undercommon, Sign Language <br/>
**Infravision**: 120 feet <br/>
**Duergar Stealth**: When at least 90 feet away from non-Duergar allies, Gray Dwarves impose a -2 penalty on opponents’ surprise rolls. This benefit is lost if the Duergar must open a door or otherwise reveal their presence. They also gain a +2 bonus to their own surprise rolls. <br/>
**Duergar Resistance:** In addition to normal Dwarven Resistance, Gray Dwarves are immune to paralysis and to _illusion_ and _phantasm_ spells, and are also unaffected by magical or alchemical poisons (but natural poisons may still affect them). <br/>
**Innate Magic**: Once per day each, a Gray Dwarf may cast _enlarge_ and _invisibility_ as a wizard of twice their level. These abilities affect only the caster and their gear. <br/>
**Light Sensitivity**: While a Duergar is in bright light, such as sunlight or a *continual light* spell, they lose the benefits of Duergar Stealth, and suffer a -2 penalty to both their dexterity ability score and to attack rolls. If the Duergar is in darkness, but an opponent is in bright light they  suffer only a -1 to penalty to attacks. Dimmer light sources such as torches and lanterns, the standard *light* spell, or *Faerie Fire* do not however have this effect. <br/>
**Racial Enmities**: Gray Dwarves **do not gain** the normal +1 bonus to attacks against orcs and goblinoids. <br/>
**Experience Penalty** Requires +20% experience points to gain a new level.

#### Gully Dwarf
Gully dwarves are widely regarded as the lowest and most degraded of the dwarven peoples. Outcast from the strongholds and traditions of their kin, they have adapted to lives of scarcity on the margins of other societies. They are most often found in abandoned ruins, derelict mines, sewers, refuse heaps, or the poorest districts of large settlements—places most avoid and few contest.

A typical gully dwarf stands about 4 feet tall and weighs roughly 100 pounds. Their skin ranges from olive-brown to a pale yellowish hue, though it is often obscured by grime, scar tissue, boils, and scabs. Hair and beards tend toward dull, dirty shades of blond or brown; females grow coarse facial hair along the cheeks but lack full beards. Their eyes are often described as dull and lifeless, in shades of blue, green, or hazel.

They are the most slenderly built of the dwarven races, with thin fingers well-suited to rummaging and salvage. Among them, a distended belly is often seen as a mark of success—a visible sign of one who has secured regular food in an environment where such things are uncertain.

Gully dwarves have a notorious reputation among other races for ignorance, filth, and unreliability. Whether this reputation is entirely deserved or exaggerated by prejudice, their behavior is undeniably shaped by harsh conditions: they are opportunistic scavengers, quick to lie or steal when it ensures survival, and quicker still to flee from open conflict. When forced to fight, they do so poorly and without discipline, preferring trickery, evasion, or submission over direct confrontation.

Despite this, gully dwarves possess a high opinion of themselves, while viewing other dwarves as arrogant or out of touch. Their societies are loose and chaotic, lacking the rigid clan structures of other dwarven cultures.

Because they inhabit places even monstrous races avoid, gully dwarves have few consistent racial enemies. Their possessions are typically of little value, scavenged from refuse or discarded goods, and thus rarely worth taking. Indeed, it is said that even a Carrion Crawler would choose starvation over eating a gully dwarf.

**Ability Score Adjustment**: +1 Strength, +1 Dexterity, -2 Charisma <br/>
**Frequently Known Languages**: Dwarven (Gully), Common, Gnome, Orc, Goblin <br/>
**Grovelling**:  When faced with immediate danger and not already engaged in melee, a gully dwarf may throw himself on the mercy of his enemies—begging, grovelling, or feigning unconsciousness. This requires one round.

All opponents who witness this must make a saving throw vs. spell (modified by the gully dwarf’s level, see table below). Those who fail may not attack the gully dwarf for 1d6 rounds, though they may restrain or bind him. Those who succeed are unaffected and immune to that gully dwarf’s grovelling for the remainder of the encounter. If the gully dwarf makes an attack, casts a spell, or takes any overtly hostile action, the effects of grovelling immediately end for any creature that can clearly see that action.

| Level | Modifier |
| ----- | -------- |
| 1-4   | 0        |
| 5-8   | -2       |
| 9-12  | -3       |
| 13+   | -5       |

**Ignorance**: Gully dwarves are not known for careful thought or planning. While player character gully dwarves are exceptional individuals, the DM may call for an Intelligence check when the character attempts complex reasoning, detailed planning, or subtle problem-solving. Failure indicates the character overlooks a key detail, misunderstands the situation, or proposes a flawed approach. <br/>
**Magical Malfunction**: The chance of magical items malfunctioning in the hands of a gully dwarf increases from 20% to 40%.

#### Mountain Dwarf
These dwarves live beneath the mountains. Their strongholds are usually isolated and they often have little contact with other races. Mountain dwarves tend to value their privacy and actively discourage visitors to their strongholds.

A typical mountain dwarf is, on average, 4-1/2 feet tall and weighs about 170 pounds. His hair is a lighter shade than his hill cousin's, and his skin is slightly more red in colour.

Mountain dwarves claim that they are the original dwarves and that all other dwarves are descended from them. They are wary of hill dwarves because of their closer dealings with humans, elves, and other races. They are clannish and keep to themselves. The vast majority are of lawful good alignment.

**Ability Score Adjustment**: +1 Constitution, -1 Charisma <br/>
**Frequently Known Languages**: Dwarven (Mountain), Common, Gnomish, Goblin, Kobold, Orc, Ogre, Troll

#### Sundered Dwarf
Sundered Dwarves are so named because they have been violently severed from their traditional subterranean way of life. Where other dwarves withdrew deeper beneath the earth, growing more insular, the Sundered were driven to the surface, forced to live among other races.

Whether displaced by natural catastrophe—volcanic upheaval or earthquakes that shattered their strongholds—or by relentless foes rising from the depths, all Sundered Dwarves share a history of loss. Their ancestral halls are gone, and with them the security and certainty that defined dwarven life below ground.

Though they have adapted as best they can to the surface world, it remains an ill fit. Many carry lingering trauma from the events that drove them out, sometimes manifesting as aversions to darkness or enclosed spaces. Even when the opportunity arises, few willingly return below, whether from fear, unease, or the weight of memory; this reluctance is often a source of quiet shame.

In human cities, Sundered Dwarves are often found in poorer districts or enclaves, though many live and work alongside other races. They tend to be slightly taller and leaner than their subterranean kin, and in dim light might be mistaken for short-statured humans, were it not for the faint blue cast often seen in their hair.

Despite their displacement, they retain many dwarven traditions. Clan structures persist, though often in diminished or altered form, and many continue in customary crafts such as smithing, brewing, and stonework. Others turn their skills to mercenary service, where dwarven discipline and resilience remain highly valued.

Sundered clans differ markedly from those of other dwarves. With shared wealth and security harder to maintain, identity is often expressed through outward signs—ritual, custom, and the visible trappings of tradition—rather than the tightly interwoven obligations of kin. As a result, Sundered Dwarves most commonly tend toward lawful neutral alignments, valuing order and structure, but tempered by moral pragmatism born of hardship.

**Ability Score Adjustment**: +1 Strength, +1 Constitution, -1 Charisma <br/>
**Frequently Known Languages**: Common, Dwarven (Any), Elven, Halfling, Orc, Kobold, any Goblinoid <br/>
**Infravision**: 30 feet <br/>
**Claustrophobic**: Sundered dwarves have a strong aversion to enclosed underground spaces. When attempting to enter a confined subterranean area (such as a dungeon, cave, or tomb), the character must make a successful saving throw vs. paralysis. If the save fails, the dwarf hesitates and refuses to enter for 1 hour, after which they may attempt the save again.

Once underground, the dwarf suffers a -2 penalty to attack rolls and saving throws due to mounting anxiety. Each day spent below ground, the dwarf must make another saving throw vs. paralysis. On a failed save, the penalty worsens by -1 (to a maximum of -5), and the dwarf becomes increasingly determined to return to the surface by the most direct route.

A successful saving throw does not remove accumulated penalties, but prevents them from worsening further that day. Once the dwarf returns to open air, all penalties are removed after 8 hours.

### Elves
Elves comprise several distinct peoples, some differing markedly in appearance, though all share certain underlying traits that mark them as elven. They are typically of near-human height but are more slender of build, with sharp yet delicate features and an effortless grace of movement. Elves are extraordinarily long-lived—often enduring for a millennium or more—and this longevity grants them a patience and perspective that shorter-lived races rarely comprehend.

Perhaps because such long lives would become intolerably stagnant without change, most elven cultures place greater value on the ephemeral than on material accumulation. A song is never performed the same way twice, a poem is reinterpreted with each telling, and even the changing light of dawn is regarded as something to be observed and appreciated anew each day. This attentiveness to variation fosters a deep appreciation for beauty, refinement, and subtlety, and is reflected in their close affinity with the natural world—particularly forests, rivers, and other places where change unfolds gradually and without artifice.

Elves are thoughtful and deliberate in both word and deed. Their humor tends toward the subtle and ironic, their speech measured, and their actions guided more often by awareness than impulse. They are brave, but seldom reckless; cautious where others might be hasty, and patient where others would press forward. They eat lightly, drink in moderation, and take pleasure in finely wrought objects, though they are rarely driven by wealth or possession for its own sake.

They are deeply drawn to disciplines that reward precision and mastery. Magic, particularly arcane magic, is regarded not as a tool but as an art—an extension of thought, memory, and will. Likewise, archery and swordplay are studied as refined practices, valued as much for their form and elegance as for their utility in war. This pursuit of mastery can, at times, become a flaw; elves may lose themselves in study, artistry, or even conflict long after others would have judged the matter settled.

Except for the Drow, elven societies tend to be fluid and decentralized. While some form lasting realms or kingdoms, many prefer smaller communities bound by shared purpose, artistry, or tradition rather than rigid hierarchy. Where authority exists, it is usually earned through wisdom, skill, or insight, rather than imposed by rank or lineage. This balance between communal identity and individual expression contributes to a tendency among elves toward chaotic or neutral alignments, though lawful societies are not unknown.

Elves are frequently opposed to orcish and goblinoid peoples, whose destructive tendencies they regard as a violation of natural balance. Relations with dwarves are often strained, shaped by deep cultural differences: where dwarves value permanence, endurance, and material mastery, elves favor transience, adaptation, and refinement. Despite this, alliances between the two are not uncommon in the face of greater threats.

Above all, elves tend to view the world not as something to be dominated, but as something to be experienced, understood, and, where possible, preserved.

#### Common Characteristics of Elves
Unless otherwise noted in a subrace description, all Elves share the following characteristics:
##### Available Classes
A player character elf can be a cleric, fighter, wizard, thief, or ranger, and may multi-class as fighter/mage, fighter/thief, fighter/mage/thief, or mage/thief.

Rarely, an elf may be a bard, mage/bard, or thief/bard; however, such characters must select a kit from a restricted list.

An Elf may also multiclass as a fighter/cleric, but only for the purpose of playing as a demi-paladin, following the rules presented after the paladin class description.
##### Languages
Each elven subrace speaks its own distinct language, all of which share deep and ancient roots—sometimes said to predate the elves’ arrival in the mortal world. Over long ages, and shaped by the elven fondness for change and variation, this ancestral tongue has fragmented into forms that are no longer mutually intelligible, though as echoes of their shared origin persist in song, poetry, and magic, some rudimentary communication may be possible.

Elves often learn languages of the forest's children, both friends and foes. Each subrace description lists commonly known languages that are appropriate for a character to select using proficiency slots during character creation.
##### Resistance to Charm/Sleep
Elven characters have 90% magic resistance to _sleep_ and all _charm_-related spells. This is in addition to the normal saving throw allowed against a _charm_ spell.
##### Martial Studies
During childhood (e.g. before their 75th birthday) an Elf is expected to become familiar with the forms and traditions of archery and swordplay. While this does not grant proficiency in any weapons, it does impart a +1 bonus to attacks with bows (not crossbows), short-swords, and long-swords.
##### Natural Stealth
Elves can move through forests and other natural terrain almost invisibly. When they move carefully (half movement rate), they appear only as shifting shadows among the foliage. This is of immense value to elves, who can gather information about enemies who often do not even suspect their presence.

An elf who is not wearing metal armor and is not engaged in combat gains a +15% bonus to Hide in Shadows rolls made in natural terrain. An elf who does not otherwise possess the Hide in Shadows ability may still attempt to use it in such environments, with a base chance of 25%, modified by Dexterity as appropriate.

In addition, regardless of terrain, an elf who is a) not wearing metal armour, and b) alone **or** accompanied only by elves and halflings not in metal armor **or** at least 90 feet away from their party, imposes a -4 penalty to opponents surprise rolls. If the elf must open a door or otherwise break concealment immediately prior to an attack, this penalty is reduced to -2.
##### Infravision
Elven infravision enables them to see up to 60 feet in the dark.
##### Elven Detection
Elves’ attentiveness to variation allows them to notice inconsistencies that reveal secret doors (those constructed to be hidden) and concealed doors (those hidden from view by screens, curtains, or similar coverings). An elf merely passing within 10 feet of a concealed door has a one-in-six chance (1 on 1d6) to notice it.

If actively searching for a hidden door, an elf will locate a secret door on a roll of 1–2 on 1d6, and a concealed one on a roll of 1–3 on 1d6.
### Elven Subraces
To the eye of outsiders, the differences between the branches of the elven race are mostly cosmetic, but most elves maintain that there are important cultural differences between them. Aquatic elves obviously spend their lives beneath the waves and have adapted to these conditions. Grey elves are considered the most noble and serious-minded, High elves the most common. Sylvan elves are often regarded by outsiders as wild and temperamental. And all the other subraces agree that the subterranean dark elves are corrupt and evil, the undesirable part of the elven diaspora.

#### High Elf
High elves are the most commonly encountered of the elven peoples and the most likely to interact with non-elven races. Of all elves, they are generally the most outward-facing, and are therefore more often found in adventuring parties.

They are typically more accustomed to travel beyond elven lands than other subraces, though they remain cautious in their dealings with shorter-lived peoples. While often appearing aloof at first, high elves value friendship and alliance, particularly with other good-aligned races, though trust is usually earned rather than freely given.

Physically, high elves are very pale and do not tan easily, even after long exposure to sunlight. Their hair varies between fair (blond with blue eyes) and dark (ranging from brown to black, often paired with green eyes), with both types occurring in roughly equal numbers and no known functional difference between them.

High elves prefer light, natural colours and often favor green cloaks when moving through forested terrain. Their equipment reflects both practicality and refinement: they favour bows and blades such as long swords and short swords, and commonly wear lighter armours that allow ease of movement in natural environments.

High elf communities are typically stable, long-established, and closely tied to the natural world, maintaining a careful balance with their surroundings. Unlike many other elven subraces, they are neither wholly insular nor wholly detached, often acting as intermediaries between elvenkind and the wider world.

Though outsiders sometimes regard them as the “typical” elves, high elves themselves place little importance on such distinctions. Their societies tend to favour consensus over hierarchy, and authority—where it exists—is informal, resting on reputation, wisdom, and accomplishment rather than lineage or title.

**Ability Score Adjustment**: +1 Dexterity, -1 Constitution <br/>
**Frequently Known Languages**: High Elvish, Any other Elvish, Dwarven (any), Gnomish, Halfling, Common, Orcish, Hobgoblin, Goblin

#### Dark Elf (Drow)
Dark elves, commonly known as drow, are the subterranean kin of the elven race. Long ago driven beneath the surface after a devastating war with other elves, they now dwell in the vast caverns of the Underdark, where they have established powerful and far-reaching domains. They are widely feared and almost universally reviled by surface-dwelling peoples.

Drow are physically similar to other elves but are typically shorter and more slight. Their skin ranges from deep charcoal to jet black, their hair is most often stark white, and their eyes glow red. These features, along with their adaptation to lightless environments, mark them clearly apart from their surface kin.

Drow society is rigid, hierarchical, and most often matriarchal. Power rests primarily with priestesses devoted to their dark goddess, Lolth, who wield both religious and political authority. Advancement within drow society is ruthless and frequently achieved through intrigue, betrayal, or assassination. Though individually cunning and ambitious, drow live within strict social structures where status is closely guarded.

Unlike other elves, drow embrace domination rather than harmony. They value power, cunning, and control, and regard other races—including other elves—as either tools or enemies. Their hatred of surface elves is particularly intense, and they will rarely pass up an opportunity to undermine or destroy them.

Drow craftsmanship is intricate and often imbued with potent magic, though many such items degrade when removed from the magical environment of the Underdark. Their affinity for magic is strong, particularly in forms that manipulate darkness, illusion, and fear.

Drow are highly sensitive to bright light, which they avoid whenever possible. As a result, they operate primarily in darkness or dim conditions, where their senses and training give them a significant advantage.

Drow tend strongly toward chaotic evil alignments, driven by ambition, cruelty, and devotion to their harsh and unforgiving society.

**Ability Score Adjustment**: +2 Dexterity, -1 Constitution, +1 Intelligence, -2 Charisma <br/>
**Frequently Known Languages**: Dark Elvish, Other Elvish, Dwarven (Grey), Svirfneblin, Dwarven (Deep), Illithid, Undercommon, Sign language, Kuo-toa, Bugbear, Orcish. <br/>
**Infravision**: 90 feet <br/>
**Innate Spellcasting**: Drow may cast spells through force of will, requiring no material components. All drow may cast _dancing lights_, _faerie fire_, and _darkness_ once per day each. At 4th level, all drow gain _levitate_, _know alignment_, and _detect magic_, each usable once per day. At this level, drow priests also gain _clairvoyance_, _detect lie_, _suggestion_, and _dispel magic_ once per day each. <br/>
**Magic Resistance**: Drow have 50% magic resistance at 1st level, increasing by 2% per level to a maximum of 80%. Multiclass characters use their highest class level to determine this value.  
In addition, drow gain a +2 bonus to all saving throws against magical effects, including those from magic items. <br/>
**Light Sensitivity**: In light brighter than a torch, drow suffer a –2 penalty to Dexterity and attack rolls, and opponents gain a +2 bonus on saving throws against the drow’s spells (including innate spellcasting). If the drow is in darkness while their opponent is in bright light, only the –2 attack penalty applies. <br/>
**Underdark Dependency**: Drow physiology and magic are sustained by the energies of the Underdark. After two weeks away, their innate powers begin to fade. For each additional day spent outside the Underdark, the drow loses the use of one innate spell (in the reverse order they are listed under Innate Spellcasting), and their magic resistance decreases by 10%. These effects are cumulative.

Upon returning to the Underdark, the drow recovers all lost abilities after spending one day in the Underdark for each week spent away (rounded up). <br/>
**Elven Enmity**: Other elves react to drow with hostility, imposing a –4 penalty to reaction rolls. This penalty is cumulative with other modifiers. <br/>
**Experience Penalty** Requires +20% experience points to gain a new level.

#### Grey Elves
Grey elves are both the most reclusive and self-assured of the elven peoples. They regard themselves as the purest expression of elvenkind, and as the custodians of its highest ideals. While they believe it is their role to safeguard the world from great evils, they seldom involve themselves in its affairs unless the threat is significant and unavoidable.

They are distant and often haughty in their dealings with others, including other elves. Though not overtly hostile, they are deeply insular and take great care to preserve both their traditions and what they see as the integrity of the elven line. Their hidden cities—typically located in remote highlands or secluded valleys—are protected by powerful magic, and intruders are rarely welcomed.

Physically, grey elves are taller and more slender than most of their kin. They commonly possess silver hair and amber eyes, though rarer individuals with pale golden hair and violet eyes are sometimes regarded as especially gifted or significant. Their attire is elegant and formal, favouring pale tones accented with deep blues or purples, and their bearing is often striking enough to seem almost otherworldly.

Grey elf society is highly structured, with clearly defined roles and hierarchies. Authority is formalized and often hereditary, supported by councils of noble houses and scholarly orders. Though not needlessly cruel, they are exacting and place great importance on discipline, duty, and the proper fulfillment of one’s station. Other elves often view this rigid order as excessive, and regard grey elves as aloof, joyless, or overly concerned with status.

Of all elves, grey elves place the greatest emphasis on knowledge and the cultivation of the mind. They produce more mages and scholars than any other subrace, and their long lives allow them to accumulate vast stores of learning. Their libraries are extensive, their magical traditions highly refined, and their craftsmanship—particularly in enchanted items—renowned even among other elves.

Grey elves tend strongly toward lawful alignments, valuing order, tradition, and the preservation of what they believe to be the highest forms of elven culture. While they are not inherently hostile to other races, they are among the least tolerant of them, and maintain distance wherever possible.

**Ability Score Adjustment**: -1 Strength, +1 Dexterity, -2 Constitution, +2 Intelligence <br/>
**Frequently Known Languages**: Grey Elvish, Any language (reflecting their unparalleled access to libraries and accumulated scholarship) <br/>
**Research Assistance**: Grey elves have access to vast and carefully maintained libraries, and may consult them when seeking knowledge, however obscure. When in or near a grey elven settlement, a character may conduct research directly. Otherwise, requests may be sent from any settlement of at least town size, incurring a fee and requiring time as determined by the DM. If the information sought is judged by the Grey Elf Council to be of clear importance to the welfare of elvenkind, the request may be fulfilled more quickly and at reduced cost. This ability does not guarantee success; the availability, accuracy, and completeness of any information remain subject to the DM. <br/>
**Aloof Demeanor**: Grey elves are distant and often perceived as condescending in their dealings with others. They suffer a –3 penalty to reaction rolls when interacting with non-elven races, and a –1 penalty when dealing with other elven subraces. <br/>
**Experience Penalty** Requires +15% experience points to gain a new level.

#### Sea Elf
Sea elves, also known as aquatic elves, are a widespread but seldom-seen branch of elvenkind. They dwell in oceans and large inland seas, ranging through underwater territories and coral cities. To surface dwellers, they are most often glimpsed only briefly among kelp beds or in the company of dolphins near coastal waters.

Sea elves possess gill slits that allow them to breathe underwater, though they can also breathe air. Their skin ranges from silver-green to blue-green, blending naturally with seaweed and open water, and their hair typically matches these tones. In their native environment they are extremely difficult to detect, gaining full benefit of elven natural stealth.

Sea elf society is loosely organized across wide maritime regions rather than fixed kingdoms. Local leaders exist but hold limited authority, and most communities are largely self-governing, uniting only in times of crisis.

Sea elves serve as guardians of the sea, opposing destructive forces such as sahuagin and ixitxachitl. The sahuagin are their most persistent enemies. They also strongly dislike sharks, which they regard as brutal and indiscriminate predators.

They maintain close bonds with dolphins and hippocampi, both of which are treated as allies. Dolphins are valued companions, while hippocampi are often used as mounts.

Despite their isolation, sea elves maintain contact with surface elves and are generally hospitable to elven visitors. Trade is common, though it is usually land elves who travel between realms. Sea elves often accompany elven ships, defending them from underwater threats.

Sea elf settlements are formed from living coral and natural structures, often enhanced with crystal and shellwork. Life beneath the waves is typically ordered and harmonious, with most elves engaged in fishing, farming, or craft.

Sea elves tend toward neutral alignments, concerned primarily with preserving the balance of the seas and the creatures that dwell within them.

**Ability Score Adjustment**: +1 Dexterity, -1 Intelligence <br/>
**Frequently Known Languages**: Sea Elvish, Kuo-toa, Sahuagin, Dolphin, Merman, Undersea Common, High Elvish, Common <br/>
**Infravision**: 360 feet <br/>
**Martial Studies**: As bows are ineffective underwater, a sea elf does not study archery and does not gain the +1 bonus to attack with bows. <br/> 
**Aquatic Dependency:** Sea elves are adapted to life in salt water and suffer when removed from it. While not wholly reliant on their gills and able to breathe air normally, their physiology is ill-suited to prolonged exposure outside the sea.

A sea elf may remain out of salt water for a number of days equal to their initial Constitution score. Thereafter, they suffer cumulative penalties of –1 to Strength, Dexterity, Constitution, and related proficiencies for every two days spent away from the sea. If any of Strength, Dexterity, or Constitution is reduced to 0, the sea elf dies.

Immersion in fresh water halts further deterioration but does not restore lost abilities; full recovery occurs only upon immersion in salt water, at which point all penalties are removed within 15 minutes. Time spent in fresh water does not count against time away from salt water, and a new period of tolerance begins upon re-emergence. Impure or fouled water is difficult for sea elves to breathe and does not halt deterioration.

#### Sylvan Elf
Sylvan elves, often called wood elves, are the most closely bound to the living rhythms of the natural world. Though descended from the same ancient stock as other elves, they have diverged markedly in culture and temperament. Where other elves refine and reinterpret experience, wood elves immerse themselves in it directly, favouring immediacy over abstraction.

Their lives are shaped by the cycles and demands of the forest. Survival is not seen as hardship, but as a defining element of existence—one that sharpens the senses and deepens their connection to the world around them. The sounds of wind, beasts, and birds serve as both music and meaning. What art they create is often transient or embodied, such as tattoos or seasonal adornments, rather than objects meant to endure.

Physically, wood elves are somewhat more robust than their kin, with lightly tanned, often ruddy complexions. Their hair ranges from yellow-blond to coppery red, and their eyes are most often light brown or green, with rarer hues sometimes regarded as omens. Their clothing is practical and subdued, favouring natural tones that allow them to move unseen through their environment.

Often described as wild or temperamental, wood elves are more accurately understood as intensely intuitive. They rely on instinct and immediate perception rather than careful deliberation, acting swiftly when needed and trusting to experience over analysis. This outlook shapes their societies, which are small, mobile, and loosely structured, with leadership based on skill rather than fixed authority.

Wood elves are highly territorial and maintain constant, unseen watch over their lands. Intruders are typically observed long before they are aware of any presence. Depending on circumstance, they may be ignored, warned, or driven off; those who pose a threat—or linger too long—may be met with sudden and decisive violence. This is not viewed as cruelty, but as a necessary means of preserving the balance of their domain.

Though reclusive, wood elves are not without celebration. At long intervals, they gather to mark seasonal and celestial events with music, fire, and ritual, expressing a communal and exuberant side of their nature.

Wood elves tend strongly toward neutral alignments, concerned primarily with the preservation of their lands and way of life. While not inherently hostile, they are rarely welcoming to outsiders, and form alliances only when necessity demands it.

**Ability Score Adjustment**: +1 Strength, +1 Dexterity, -1 Constitution, -1 Charisma <br/>
**Frequently Known Languages**: Sylvan, High Elvish, Centaur, Pixie, Dryad, Treant. <br/>
**Wild Empathy**: A sylvan elf can improve the reaction of any natural forest creature (i.e., non-monstrous creatures native to woodland environments) that is not already hostile by two categories. This ability is not magical and does not function as a _charm_ effect; it cannot compel service or override a creature’s nature. Creatures made friendly will not accompany the elf unless further persuaded by normal means. <br/>
**Elven Detection**: Sylvan elves are attuned to the natural world rather than to constructed environments, and cannot detect secret doors. They may, however, detect concealed doors as other elves do. <br/>
**Child of the Wild**: Sylvan elves are most at ease in forested environments and among the creatures that dwell there. They are generally distant and uncooperative toward outsiders, though they tend to show greater tolerance for rangers and druids.

They are willing to travel along rivers or inland waters, and may briefly take shelter in natural underground spaces, but avoid prolonged stays outside woodland environments. They refuse to board sea-going vessels, are uncomfortable underground, and strongly dislike cities and other centres of civilization, which they regard as a corruption of the natural world.

A sylvan elf who consistently disregards these attitudes may incur an experience penalty at the DM’s discretion; this has no other direct mechanical effect.

### Gnomes
Gnomes are a diminutive and long-lived people, typically smaller and more lightly built than other demi-humans, with expressive features and distinctive, prominent noses. Their colouring tends toward earth tones—browns, tans, and muted hues—while their hair often fades to pale shades with age. Though physically unassuming, they possess a quickness of perception and movement that reflects an active and inquisitive mind.

Gnomes are defined less by what they make than by how they understand the world around them. They are keen observers of patterns, processes, and subtle irregularities, with a natural curiosity directed toward the hidden workings of things—whether material, mechanical, or magical. This inclination often manifests as experimentation, careful tinkering, or the study of transformation, and underlies many of the traditions for which gnomes are known.

They possess a dry and often understated sense of humour, favouring wordplay, misdirection, and quiet irony. What may appear to outsiders as frivolity frequently masks deliberate intent, as gnomes take as much interest in how others perceive a thing as in the thing itself. This duality—of curiosity and subtlety—shapes much of their behaviour.

Gnomes favour environments where natural complexity is abundant: wooded hills, rocky uplands, and regions where earth and growth intertwine. Their settlements are often concealed or unobtrusive, blending into their surroundings or extending beneath them. While their communities vary widely in structure and custom, they are typically organised around shared work, inquiry, and mutual reliance rather than rigid hierarchy.

Though cautious in their dealings with larger races, gnomes are not inherently reclusive. Their curiosity frequently draws them beyond their homes, and many spend years or decades among other peoples before returning. Even so, they remain difficult to fully know, combining openness with a tendency toward privacy and a habit of revealing only what they choose to share.
#### Common Characteristics of Gnomes
Unless otherwise noted in a subrace description, all gnomes share the following characteristics:
##### Available Classes
Gnomish characters may be clerics, fighters, thieves, or illusionists, and may multi-class as any two of these classes, but not more.

Rarely, a gnome may be a bard or thief/bard; such characters must select from a restricted list of kits and do not gain access to arcane spellcasting as other bards do.

A gnomish fighter/cleric may elect to function as a demi-paladin, following the rules presented after the paladin class description.
##### Languages
There are only two truly distinct gnomish languages - high gnomish (commonly just known as gnomish ) and svirfneblin (deep Gnomish). The differences in the usage of high gnomish by speakers of the different subraces that speak it might affect the delivery of a punchline or wordplay, but not mutual understanding.
##### Gnomish Resistance
Gnomes possess an innate resilience to magical effects, gaining a +1 bonus to saving throws versus wands, staves, rods, and spells for every 3.5 points of Constitution.

##### Magical Interference

This does not reveal the nature of the substance or effect, only that it is unusual or out of place. At the DM’s discretion, this may include:

- alchemical residues or unstable compounds
- disguised or substituted materials (such as false metals or treated surfaces)
- faint magical contamination or lingering spell effects

This ability requires a moment of attention and does not function in combat or under rushed conditions.

### Gnomish Subraces
#### Rock Gnome
Rock gnomes are the most widely encountered of the gnomish peoples, and are what most outsiders mean when they speak simply of “gnomes.” They are small and lightly built, with expressive features and prominent noses, and are seldom still for long. Their skin ranges from tan to deep brown, and their hair typically fades to pale or white with age. They are long-lived, commonly reaching three and a half centuries or more.

Among gnomes, rock gnomes are the most outwardly engaged with the material world. They are drawn to the properties and behaviour of substances—stone, metal, crystal, and compound alike—and are rarely content to leave anything unexamined. This curiosity often expresses itself through craft, but for rock gnomes the act of making is as much investigation as it is production. Cutting a gem, alloying a metal, or assembling a device is understood as a way of discovering how and why a thing holds together—or fails.

They are inventive and sociable, with a fondness for clever mechanisms, subtle alterations, and controlled misdirection. Practical jokes and illusions are common, though these are as often demonstrations of timing and perception as they are attempts at humour. Rock gnomes are patient when engaged, but easily distracted by new problems, new materials, or new possibilities, and are rarely content to repeat a task once they feel they understand it.

They favour wooded hills, rocky uplands, and other regions where varied materials are readily available. Their settlements are typically a blend of surface and subsurface construction, with burrows, workshops, and shared spaces woven into the surrounding terrain. These communities are active and cooperative, organised into clans that share labour, knowledge, and resources, with leadership falling to those who demonstrate practical skill and sound judgment.

Though cautious around larger folk, rock gnomes are not reclusive. Their curiosity frequently draws them outward, and many spend years or decades among other peoples before returning home. They are generally on good terms with dwarves, though they regard dwarven methods as overly rigid and final, preferring approaches that allow for adjustment, refinement, and occasional failure.

Rock gnomes are often encountered abroad as artisans, traders, or wanderers, but just as often as observers and experimenters, following questions that others might overlook. They are friendly but not always forthcoming, and even long acquaintance does not guarantee full understanding of their intentions.

**Ability Score Adjustment**: +1 Intelligence, -1 Wisdom <br/>
**Frequently Known Languages**: Gnome, Common, Dwarven (any non-evil), Halfling, Kobold, Goblin, and Burrowing Animal (the last is a language of signs, grunts, and snorts that allows minimal communication with moles, badgers, weasels, and similar creatures, including giant versions). <br/>
**Infravision**: 60 feet

#### Deep Gnomes (Svirfneblin)
Svirfneblin, or Deep Gnomes, are the most secretive and hardened expression of gnomish ingenuity. Where surface gnomes experiment for curiosity or delight, Deep Gnomes are driven by necessity—treating survival in the Underdark as a continuous exercise in applied science.

Distant kin to other gnomes, they are leaner and more austere in form, adapted to perpetual darkness with pale or stone-toned skin, sparse hair, and eyes suited to low light. They are long-lived, though few avoid the hazards of their environment indefinitely.

At their core, Svirfneblin remain gnomes: observant, inquisitive, and driven to understand how things work. But their curiosity is disciplined into method. Stone, mineral, pressure, toxin, and light are not just hazards or resources—they are systems to be tested, measured, and controlled. Their culture is one of practical experimentation under extreme conditions.

This focus makes them unmatched subterranean engineers and miners. They extract and refine gemstones and rare ores with exceptional precision, while also developing techniques in structural reinforcement and alchemical adaptation of underground materials. What surface gnomes might explore for novelty, Svirfneblin refine into survival knowledge.

Their settlements are hidden cavern networks, designed as controlled environments of tunnels, workshops, farms, and living spaces. Every community is both home and ongoing experiment in managing hostile geology and ecology.

Leadership is practical and divided between complementary roles—typically one focused on external survival and defense, and another on internal stability and resource management. Authority is earned through competence rather than inheritance.

Isolation has made them highly self-reliant but not entirely withdrawn. They maintain cautious awareness of Underdark powers, especially drow and other predatory species, and have developed extensive practices of concealment and environmental control.

While often perceived as grim by outsiders, Svirfneblin share the same defining trait as all gnomes: relentless curiosity. It is simply expressed in harsher conditions, where every experiment carries real consequence and every discovery is measured in survival as much as understanding.

**Ability Score Adjustment**: +1 Dexterity, -1 Intelligence, +1 Wisdom, -2 Charisma <br/>
**Frequently Known Languages**: Svirfneblin, Gnomish, Underworld Common, Drow; Kuo-toa, Earth Elemental (a curious "language" without words consisting solely of vibrations; each different pitch conveying a different message) <br/>
**Infravision**: 120 feet <br/>
**Magic Resistance**: Deep Gnomes possess an innate resistance to magic that is shaped by prolonged exposure to the Underdark. They have a base magic resistance of 20% and this increases by 10% at 5th, and every 5 levels thereafter (maximum 60%) <br/>
**Gnomish Resistance**: For Svirfneblin, Gnomish Resistance applies to "poison, toxins, gases, fear effects, and illusion or enchantment magic" instead of the normal "wands, staves, rods, and spells". <br/>
**Innate Magic**: All Deep Gnomes radiate _non-detection_. In addition, all have the innate ability to cast *blur* and _change self_ once per day. <br/>
**Unsurprised**: Deep Gnomes are only surprised on a roll of 1 or 2 on 1d10. <br/>
**Svirfneblin Stun**: Svirfneblin can make and wield _stun darts_, (range 40 feet, +2 to hit). Each dart releases a small puff of gas when it strikes; a creature struck by the dart must save versus poison or be stunned for 1 round and _slowed_ for the four following rounds, but take no damage. Crafting a stun dart requires a normal dart and specialized reagents prepared under heat. Materials sufficient for 2 darts cost 1 gp, or may be gathered with 1 additional hour of foraging. Two stun darts can be produced per hour of work. <br/>
**Experience Point Adjustment:** +10% experience required to advance.

#### Forest Gnome
Forest gnomes are the smallest and most reclusive of the gnomish peoples, seldom encountered except in the deepest and least disturbed woodlands. Even more lightly built than other gnomes, they are characterized by expressive features, prominent noses, and an appearance that often seems subtly blended with the forest itself. Their skin tends toward earthy tan tones with a faint greenish cast, their hair is typically dark and may grow long in adulthood, and their eyes are most often brown or blue, with green eyes regarded as especially fortunate. They are long-lived, commonly reaching five centuries or more.

Forest gnomes are quiet, observant, and deeply attuned to the natural world. They prefer subtle action over direct confrontation, relying on patience, concealment, and intimate knowledge of their environment. Though shy with strangers, they are not timid in purpose, and can display remarkable resolve when their homelands are threatened. Their outlook is strongly protective rather than acquisitive, and they form careful, enduring bonds with the living world around them.

They dwell in extensive woodland regions, favoring deep forests with abundant cover and minimal intrusion. Their settlements are deliberately hidden, often dispersed and difficult to detect even by experienced woodsfolk. Unlike other gnomes, they make frequent use of natural structures such as trees, roots, and hollowed trunks, blending habitation into the living forest rather than reshaping it. They avoid excavation into stone and prefer to work with what nature already provides.

Forest gnomes are highly cautious in their dealings with outsiders, particularly humans and dwarves, whom they often regard as careless with the natural world. Elves are their closest allies among the larger races, and halflings are generally regarded with cautious friendliness. While they rarely seek contact, they are capable of forming strong and lasting alliances when trust is established.

Forest gnome communities are small and loosely organized, often resembling extended family groups rather than formal settlements. Leadership is typically informal and based on age, experience, and demonstrated judgment rather than rank or inheritance. Though most are content to remain within their hidden domains, individuals occasionally venture beyond the forest, though few remain away permanently.

Despite their isolation, forest gnomes are far from passive. They are vigilant guardians of their territories and skilled in subtle forms of woodland defense, relying on concealment, misdirection, and intimate environmental knowledge rather than open warfare.

**Ability Score Adjustment**: -1 Strength, +1 Dexterity, -1 Intelligence, +1 Wisdom <br/>
**Frequently Known Languages**: Gnomish, Common, Sylvan Elven, High Elven, the simple “forest speech” of animals (a limited system of sounds and signs allowing basic communication of intent such as danger, food, or presence). <br/>
**Infravision**: 30 feet <br/>
**Racial Enmity**: Forest gnomes gain a +1 bonus to attack rolls against orcs, lizard men, and troglodytes, instead of kobolds and goblins. This bonus also applies against any creature they have directly observed damaging or despoiling woodland environments (such as wanton logging, burning, or destruction of habitat), at the DM’s discretion. <br/>
**Pass Without Trace**: Forest gnomes move through natural woodlands with minimal disturbance. They do not leave obvious tracks or signs of passage in forested terrain under normal conditions, and tracking them suffers a −4 penalty unless the tracker has specialized training or the gnome is moving hastily, carrying heavy loads, or travelling through especially unsuitable ground (mud, snow, etc.). This is a natural ability and not magical. <br/>
**Woodland Obscurity**: In natural woodland environments, forest gnomes are exceptionally difficult to detect. Reduce all normal visibility ranges for spotting, identifying, or observing a forest gnome to one-third their usual distance. This ability does not function in open terrain or heavily altered environments.

#### Tinker Gnomes
Tinker gnomes are an eccentric and highly specialized subrace defined by their relentless drive to invent, refine, and reimagine the workings of the world. While they share the physical traits of other gnomes—slight build, prominent features, and pale hair in adulthood—their manner quickly sets them apart. They are rarely still, often distracted, and almost always in the process of improving something that already functions perfectly well.

To a tinker gnome, no device is ever complete. Every tool, structure, or mechanism is merely an interim step toward a more elaborate and “perfect” design. This philosophy produces creations of remarkable ingenuity, but also of unpredictable reliability. Their devices frequently incorporate excessive complexity—gears, vents, counterweights, and auxiliary systems whose purpose may be theoretical, redundant, or entirely misunderstood.

Tinker gnomes organize their societies around guilds devoted to fields of study and invention. Upon reaching adulthood, each individual undertakes a _life-quest_: a self-defined pursuit to master, perfect, or reinvent a particular concept, device, or principle. These pursuits often span centuries and are rarely completed, as new ideas and complications continually arise.

Their communities are typically located in well-ventilated subterranean or semi-subterranean environments, often near sources of coal, which they prize as a fundamental agent of transformation and industry. Such settlements are dense with workshops, experimental apparatus, and improvised transport systems, forming active and often hazardous environments of constant activity.

Tinker gnomes are sociable but difficult to follow, communicating in rapid, information-dense exchanges that can overwhelm those unaccustomed to their pace. While not intentionally reckless, they display a marked indifference to risk in the pursuit of innovation, accepting mishap as an expected consequence of progress.

Other gnomes often regard them with a mixture of fascination and exasperation, viewing their methods as undisciplined and their results as unnecessarily dangerous. Tinker gnomes, in turn, see such caution as a failure of imagination.

**Ability Score Adjustment**: -1 Strength, +2 Dexterity, -1 Wisdom <br/>
**Frequently Known Languages**: Gnomish, Common, any Human language <br/>
**Mechanical Aptitude**: A tinker gnome gains either _Engineering_ or _Locksmithing_ as a bonus nonweapon proficiency. When working within the chosen proficiency, tasks that would normally require a proficiency check due to moderate complexity or unfamiliar design are instead treated as routine, provided the gnome has sufficient time and materials. If the character later acquires the other proficiency, this benefit applies to both. <br/>
**Experimental Design**: Tinker gnomes may design and construct mechanisms that are extremely unconventional, including:

- hybrid mechanical/alchemical devices
- unusually compact, intricate, or using multi-stage mechanisms
- experimental locking systems or mechanical assemblies

Such work always requires a proficiency check, regardless of conditions. The gnome may attempt to accelerate the process, reducing required time by half; doing so imposes a penalty to the proficiency check at the DM’s discretion.
### Halflings
Halflings are a small and long-lived people, similar in proportion to humans but rarely exceeding three and a half feet in height. They are typically broad-featured and solidly built for their size, with round faces, bright eyes, and a tendency toward a well-fed appearance. Their hair is often thick or curly, and their features soften with age rather than harden. Though physically unassuming, halflings are notably hardy, and commonly live a century and a half or more.

Halfling life is most often rooted in pastoral landscapes—rolling farmland, hedgerows, orchards, and low hills shaped as much by long habitation as by nature itself. Their communities are rarely grand or imposing, but they are carefully maintained and deeply inhabited, the result of generations of incremental work. Paths follow old habits, fields are cultivated with quiet precision, and dwellings—whether burrows, cottages, or a blend of both—are expanded and refined over time rather than rebuilt.

At a glance, halflings appear simple in their desires, concerned primarily with comfort, good food, and familiar surroundings. This impression is not wholly inaccurate, but it is incomplete. Halfling culture is not defined by idleness or lack of ambition, but by a deliberate preference for sufficiency over excess, and stability over upheaval. Where other races pursue expansion, mastery, or transformation, halflings concern themselves with maintaining a life that is balanced, reliable, and enduring.

This outlook shapes both their material and social worlds. Halfling communities are close-knit and cooperative, structured around shared labour, mutual reliance, and a largely unspoken understanding of each individual’s role. Wealth is valued primarily as a means of securing comfort and continuity, and is rarely pursued beyond what is needed to maintain these ends. Change is not rejected, but it is approached cautiously, evaluated for its impact on the whole rather than its novelty or promise.

Halflings are attentive observers of their surroundings, both social and physical. They are rarely forceful in manner, but neither are they unaware; rather, they possess a subtle understanding of how systems—households, communities, even travelling companies—hold together, and how they come apart. As a result, halflings tend to avoid unnecessary conflict, preferring to deflect, accommodate, or outlast disruption. When pressed, however, they are capable of quiet determination, particularly when their homes or kin are threatened.

Though not naturally inclined toward adventure, halflings are by no means incapable of it. Those who leave their communities often do so in response to disruption—loss, displacement, obligation, or a curiosity that cannot be satisfied within the bounds of their accustomed lives. Away from home, they carry with them the same instincts that define their people: a preference for caution over risk, subtlety over display, and persistence over force.

Halflings maintain generally amicable relations with other races. Their unassuming nature and lack of territorial ambition make them easy to accommodate, and they are more readily accepted in mixed communities than most demi-humans. Dwarves tend to view them as soft but dependable, elves as pleasant but unremarkable, and gnomes as kindred spirits of a more grounded sort. Halflings, for their part, are adaptable and sociable, able to find a place for themselves in a wide variety of environments without drawing undue attention.

Above all, halflings are defined not by what they lack, but by what they preserve. In a world often shaped by ambition, conflict, and change, they represent continuity—the quiet, persistent effort to maintain a life that is not grand, but is whole.

### Common Characteristics of Halflings
Unless otherwise noted in a subrace description, all halflings share the following characteristics:
#### Available Classes
Halfling characters may be clerics, fighters, or thieves, and may multi-class as fighter/thieves.

Rarely, a halfling may be a bard or thief/bard; however, such characters must select a kit from a restricted list.

A halfling fighter/cleric may elect to function as a demi-paladin, following the rules presented after the paladin class description.

#### Languages
All halflings share a common tongue, with regional variations reflecting long settlement in particular areas rather than true linguistic divergence. These dialects are mutually intelligible, though differences in idiom, humour, and expression are often pronounced. Note that Kender are not true Halflings and have their own distinct language.

Due to their frequent contact with other races and their tendency to live in mixed or adjacent communities, halflings commonly learn the languages of their neighbours. Each subrace description lists commonly known languages appropriate for selection during character creation.
#### Halfling Resilience
Halflings possess a natural resistance to forces that disrupt the body or mind. This grants a bonus to saving throws against magical wands, staves, rods, and spells, as well as against poison. This bonus is +1 for every 3.5 points of Constitution score.

| Constitution Score | Saving Throw Bonus |
| ------------------ | ------------------ |
| 4–6                | +1                 |
| 7–10               | +2                 |
| 11–13              | +3                 |
| 14–17              | +4                 |
| 18–19              | +5                 |

Unlike dwarves, whose resistance reflects a fundamental aversion to magic, or gnomes, whose interaction with magic is often unpredictable, halfling resilience represents a tendency toward equilibrium. External forces—whether magical or toxic—are less likely to produce extreme or lasting effects.
#### Unassuming Presence
Halflings move through the world with minimal disturbance, both physically and socially. Their manner is unthreatening, their expectations modest, and their presence rarely provokes immediate hostility. In most non-hostile interactions, halflings gain a +1 bonus to reaction rolls. This bonus does not apply in situations where hostility is already established, or where the halfling’s actions are overtly disruptive, aggressive, or socially inappropriate.

In addition, when not wearing metal armor, and when alone, accompanied only by halflings or elves not in metal armor, or at least 90 feet distant from their party, a halfling imposes a –4 penalty on opponents’ surprise rolls.

If the halfling must open a door or otherwise reveal their presence immediately prior to an encounter, this penalty is reduced to –2.
#### Missile Familiarity
Halflings are adept with thrown weapons and slings, which serve practical roles in daily life as much as in combat. In pastoral communities, such tools are commonly used to drive off vermin, protect crops, herd small animals, or discourage predators without damaging valuable land or livestock. From an early age, halflings develop a casual but reliable accuracy born of constant, low-stakes use.

They gain a +1 bonus to attack rolls when using thrown weapons or slings.
#### Halfling Perception
Halflings possess a practical awareness of their immediate surroundings, particularly in settled or semi-settled environments. When within 10 feet of concealed doors (those hidden by screens, furnishings, or similar means), a halfling has a base 1-in-6 chance of noticing the irregularity.

If actively searching, this improves to a 1–2 on 1d6.

This ability does not apply to secret doors constructed to be hidden within stone or similar materials unless otherwise noted.
#### Small Stature
Halflings are difficult targets for larger opponents, not through specialized training, but due to their size, low center of gravity, and instinctive evasiveness. When attacked by creatures larger than man-sized (including ogres, trolls, and similar beings), such opponents suffer a –2 penalty to their attack rolls against halflings.

The DM may adjust this modifier where size or circumstance makes it inappropriate.
### Halfling Subraces
#### Hairfoot Halfling
Hairfoot halflings are the most widespread and commonly encountered of their kind, particularly in lands settled by humans. They are the most adaptable of halfling subraces, and the most comfortable living alongside other peoples, often forming stable communities within or adjacent to human villages and towns.

Physically, Hairfeet are slightly shorter and more compact than other halflings, with a sturdy build and broad, expressive features. Their colouring varies widely, reflecting long association with human populations. They favour practical but well-made clothing, often in bright or warm tones, and place a high value on comfort in both dress and dwelling.

Hairfoot society is strongly rooted in pastoral life and small-scale craft. They are farmers, millers, brewers, tailors, and tradespeople, favouring work that produces immediate, tangible results. While not driven by expansion or accumulation, they display a quiet efficiency: tasks are approached with an eye toward balance and sustainability rather than maximum output. A Hairfoot holding may be modest in size, but it is typically well-managed and consistently productive.

Unlike many halflings, Hairfeet are at ease in mixed communities and frequently interact with human economies. They hire and employ freely, trade widely, and are often found in roles that bridge rural and urban life, particularly as merchants, innkeepers, and provisioners. Though they can and do live in human-style structures, they prefer dwellings adapted to their own scale—most often a combination of surface construction and burrowed space, designed for both openness and shelter.

Hairfeet are sociable and accommodating, with a natural ease in conversation and a talent for maintaining amicable relations. They are rarely forceful, but are skilled at navigating social expectations and finding their place within larger systems. Among halflings, they are generally regarded as the most outward-facing, and among other races, they are often taken as representative of halflingkind as a whole.

**Ability Score Adjustment**: -1 Strength, +1 Dexterity <br/>
**Frequently Known Languages**: Halfling, Common, any regional human language <br/>
**Human Familiarity**: Hairfoot halflings are deeply integrated into human societies and are particularly adept at navigating their customs, expectations, and hierarchies. They gain an additional +2 to their _Unassuming Presence_ bonus when dealing with human NPCs. <br/>

#### Furchin Halflings
Where most halflings cultivate comfort and settlement, Furchin cultivate survival through constant movement across cold, open, and resource-scarce landscapes. They are among the most wide-ranging of halflings, following migrating herds, shifting resources, and seasonal routes across tundra, glacier, and high mountain steppe.

They are stockier than most halflings, with dense builds suited to conserving heat rather than speed over short distances. Skin and hair tones vary widely but tend toward pale or muted shades, often darkening with exposure. Adults commonly wear layered furs and insulated garments tailored for mobility in extreme cold. Among mature males, full beards are common and culturally significant, often carefully maintained as markers of age, skill, and status.

Furchin society is organized into small, highly mobile kin-clans. These groups prioritize shared survival knowledge, cooperative hunting, and mutual protection while travelling vast distances. Permanent settlements are rare; instead, they make use of seasonal camps, temporary shelters, and portable structures adapted to wind, snow, and ice.

Despite their harsh environment, Furchin culture is notably warm in temperament. Humor, storytelling, and practical jokes are valued social tools for maintaining morale during long journeys and difficult seasons. Hospitality toward strangers is a strong tradition, particularly when visitors bring news, trade goods, or useful knowledge.

Furchin survival practices emphasize tracking, hunting, trapping, and efficient use of animal resources. They are especially skilled in working leather, bone, ivory, and hide, and have developed highly reliable cold-weather travel techniques, including small, stable personal watercraft used in thaw seasons.

In conflict, Furchin prefer ambush and precision over direct confrontation. They are highly effective at exploiting terrain, weather, and visibility conditions to avoid disadvantageous engagements. Though not a warlike people, they are capable hunters of dangerous predators and are known to coordinate carefully planned strikes against threats far larger than themselves.

Unlike most halflings, Furchin place less emphasis on domestic permanence and more on **continuity of movement**—the ability of the clan to persist across distance, season, and hardship.

**Ability Score Adjustment**: -1 Strength, +1 Dexterity, +1 Constitution  
**Frequently Known Languages**: Furchin, Dwarven, Regional Human Languages <br/>
**Arctic Adaptation.** Furchin ignore movement penalties from snow, ice, and non-magical cold terrain, and gain a +2 bonus to checks involving navigation, tracking, or survival in arctic or high-altitude environments. <br/>
**Nomadic Survivalist.** Furchin gain **Survival (Arctic)** as a bonus nonweapon proficiency, and one of the following: **Tracking, Hunting, or Animal Handling**. <br/>
**Fieldcraft of Hide and Bone.** Furchin gain a +2 bonus to proficiency checks involving leatherworking, tanning, hide preparation, or crafting utilitarian gear from bone, ivory, or similar materials. <br/>
**Hunting Weapons.** Furchin are proficient with harpoons, a traditional hunting weapon used with corded retrieval for large prey. They are also familiar with spear-throwing devices and other mechanical aids that increase the range and force of thrown weapons.

#### Hardfell Halflings
Found in dense, resource-rich but often hostile environments, these halflings represent an extreme adaptation of the halfling ethos: self-sufficiency taken to its limit. Where most halflings cultivate comfort, Hardfell halflings cultivate survival—shaping their communities to exist in careful balance with terrain that would overwhelm less disciplined societies.

They are slightly taller than other halflings, but leaner and more sinewy, built for agility rather than ease. Their features are weathered by sun and exposure, and they favor minimal clothing suited to heat and humidity. Hair is often worn long or bound for practicality, and ornamentation tends toward bone, wood, and featherwork.

Hardfells are intensely insular. Their communities are organized around extended kin-groups that share labor, knowledge, and responsibility for survival. While not inherently hostile, they are deeply suspicious of outsiders and enforce strict territorial boundaries. Contact with other cultures is cautious and deliberate, typically conducted at a distance or through controlled intermediaries.

Their settlements are often temporary or semi-permanent, shifting with the needs of the land. Some construct elevated dwellings or concealed paths through dense terrain, minimizing their visibility and vulnerability. Others maintain central gathering sites used periodically by dispersed groups.

Unlike other halflings, Hardfells do not idealize domestic permanence. Instead, they value **continuity of the group** over attachment to place, and **adaptability over comfort**. What they preserve is not the home, but the **way of living well within constraint**.

**Ability Score Adjustment**: -1 Strength, +1 Dexterity, +1 Wisdom <br/>
**Frequently Known Languages**: Halfling, Regional Human Languages <br/>
**Jungle Adaptation.** Hardfells suffer no movement penalties in dense vegetation, undergrowth, or swampy terrain, and gain a +2 bonus to checks involving navigation, tracking, or movement in jungle environments. <br/>
**Forager-Survivalist.** Hardfells gain **Foraging** as a bonus nonweapon proficiency, as well as one of the following: **Herbalism, Hunting, or Survival (Tropical)**. <br/>
**Toxic Lore.** Hardfells are practiced in the preparation and handling of natural toxins. They gain a +2 bonus to proficiency checks involving poisons, antitoxins, or medicinal compounds derived from natural sources. <br/>
**Missile Familiarity.** This trait is expanded for Hardfell halflings to include blowguns and darts, reflecting their reliance on lightweight, easily crafted hunting weapons suited to dense jungle terrain.

#### Stout Halfling
Stouts are a widespread halfling subrace distinguished less by height than by their dense, compact build and remarkable endurance. They average just under three feet in height, but are notably heavier-set than other halflings, with a solid, grounded physique that speaks more to resilience than raw strength.

Their complexions tend toward ruddy tones that flush readily with emotion—brightening when pleased or embarrassed and deepening when angered. Hair is usually light in colour, ranging from blond to sandy red, and eyes are commonly blue, grey, or green. Male Stouts frequently develop limited facial hair, most often in the form of thick sideburns or muttonchops, which are treated as minor marks of pride within their communities.

Stouts favour durable, practical clothing, typically made from leather, wool, and other hard-wearing materials. Everyday dress is often plain and uniform, reflecting their practical outlook, though most Stouts maintain at least one well-crafted or brightly coloured outfit reserved for festivals and formal occasions. Footwear is common and typically consists of sturdy moccasin-like boots suited to rough or uneven terrain.

Stouts are culturally the most closely aligned of all halflings with dwarves, sharing an affinity for stonework, craft, and life shaped by endurance and labour. Their settlements are often found in hilly, rocky, or mineral-rich regions, frequently near dwarven territories. Trade, defensive cooperation, and shared artisanal traditions between the two peoples are common.

Stout communities are typically built around excavation and stonecraft. Their homes are most often fully carved burrow-dwellings, set into hillsides or bedrock, designed for stability, insulation, and longevity. Interiors tend to be cool, dim, and utilitarian, with limited but carefully placed openings for light and ventilation.

Stouts are the most industrious of the halfling subraces, excelling in mining, masonry, metalworking, boatbuilding, carving, and related crafts. While they are capable farmers, they show a clear preference for underground cultivation, particularly fungi and other low-light crops. Their economies are typically anchored in durable goods and resource extraction rather than agriculture or trade.

Though not physically strong by larger folk standards, Stouts are renowned for their stamina, persistence, and resistance to hardship. They are particularly at home in underground or rugged environments and are known for their ability to work steadily in conditions that would exhaust most others. Stouts reach adulthood at around 30 years of age and commonly live well beyond 130 years, with rare individuals exceeding two centuries.

**Ability Score Adjustment**: -1 Strength, +1 Dexterity **or** Constitution <br/>
**Frequently Known Languages**: Halfling, Common, Dwarven (Any) <br/>
**Dwarven Familiarity**: Stouts share a strong cultural affinity with dwarves. When interacting with dwarf NPCs, they gain an additional +2 bonus to their _Unassuming Presence_ social modifier.

#### Tallfellow Halfling
Tallfellows are a slender, light-framed halfling subrace most commonly found in temperate woodlands and forest margins. Averaging just over four feet in height, they are the tallest of the halflings but remain slight of build, weighing little more than a Hairfoot despite their added stature. Their features tend toward fine, elongated proportions, and their overall impression is one of agility rather than mass or strength.

Their hair is typically worn long, often bound or decorated with small, brightly coloured caps or ribbons. Like other halflings, they prefer to go barefoot, though their foot-fur is finer and less pronounced than that of their kin. Clothing tends toward natural woodland tones—greens, browns, and yellows—though Tallfellows are notable among halflings for cultivating unusually vivid dyes, particularly in green hues associated with forest canopy light.

Tallfellows are the most closely associated of all halfling subraces with elven society. Their settlements are commonly located near elven territories, and long-standing trade, cultural exchange, and shared stewardship of woodland regions are frequent. This proximity has shaped Tallfellow customs toward woodland craft, seasonal living, and a strong awareness of natural cycles.

They prefer above-ground dwellings, typically spacious wooden homes with large windows and open interiors that emphasise light and airflow. Ceilings are notably high for halfling construction, often approaching human proportions. While most homes include underground storage or cellar spaces, these are secondary in function and not central to daily life. During periods of extreme summer heat, Tallfellows often retreat underground temporarily for rest and cooler shelter.

Tallfellows are the most skilled of the halfling subraces in working with wood and living natural materials. They excel as carpenters, wagon-builders, boatwrights, loggers, carvers, musicians, and shepherds. They are also accomplished foragers, adept at harvesting wild food sources such as berries, nuts, roots, and grains, and are competent but not exceptional farmers when compared to other halflings.

Unlike their kin, Tallfellows are skilled riders, favouring small ponies bred specifically for agility and endurance. These mounts lack the raw impact of larger warhorses but are highly effective in fast-moving skirmishes and woodland travel. Tallfellow riders are often employed as scouts, messengers, or light cavalry in forested regions.

In open combat, Tallfellows favour spears and polearms, often forming disciplined defensive formations that rely on reach, coordination, and positioning rather than brute force. Such formations are most effective in forested or broken terrain, where mobility and terrain awareness offset the advantage of heavier troops.

Tallfellows are notably long-lived, with lifespans significantly exceeding those of humans and other halflings. They reach adulthood around 30 years of age and commonly live well beyond 180 years, with exceptional individuals surpassing 250.

**Ability Score Adjustment**: -1 Strength, +1 Dexterity **or** Wisdom <br/>
**Frequently Known Languages**: Halfling, Sylvan Elven, High Elven, Common <br/>
**Elven Familiarity.** Tallfellows are closely associated with elven communities. When interacting with elf NPCs, they gain an additional +2 bonus to their _Unassuming Presence_ social modifier. <br/>
**Woodland Aptitude.** Tallfellows gain one bonus non-weapon proficiency from the following list at character creation: **Riding, Land-Based (Pony); Foraging; Bowyer/Fletcher; Carpentry**. This reflects their deep integration with woodland livelihoods, mounted travel, and forest resource management. <br/>
**Woodland Attunement.** Tallfellows gain a +2 bonus to surprise rolls while in forest or wooded terrain. <br/>

#### Kender
Kender are a halfling-like people of uncertain origin, occasionally encountered across the wider planes. Though unrelated to true halflings, they closely resemble them in size and general appearance, leading many halflings to regard them as distant or “honorary” kin—albeit somewhat troubling ones.

Slightly taller and more slender than most halflings, Kender average around three and a half feet in height. Their features are fine and expressive, and they tend to show age more readily than halflings, maturing and weathering in a manner closer to humans. Their hair is typically worn long, often tied into distinctive topknots or trailing styles. Unlike halflings, Kender lack foot-fur and commonly wear shoes.

Kender are defined not by where they live, but by how they move through the world. They are intensely curious, impulsively exploratory, and largely unconcerned with danger in the way other folk understand it. Most experience a period of strong wanderlust beginning in early adulthood, during which they travel widely, following interest rather than obligation. Even those who eventually settle retain this restless curiosity.

This disposition is not recklessness in the conventional sense. Kender do not seek harm, nor do they perceive themselves as brave; rather, they lack the instinctive caution that restrains others. Concepts such as risk, threat, and even ownership are understood academically but rarely internalized. As a result, Kender often handle, borrow, or redistribute objects - without malice or intent to steal - leading to frequent friction with more conventionally minded cultures.

Kender culture places little emphasis on hierarchy, property, or long-term accumulation. Instead, it values experience, storytelling, novelty, and personal interaction. Kender are highly social on an individual level but do not integrate cleanly into most structured societies.

They mature quickly, reaching adulthood in their early twenties, and have lifespans comparable to shorter-lived halflings. However, their tendency toward curiosity-driven behaviour often leads them into hazardous situations, and many do not reach old age.

### Humans
Humans are the most widespread and varied of all peoples, found in nearly every climate and environment. Unlike other races, they are not defined by a shared nature so much as by their capacity for adaptation. Culture, rather than biology, shapes human identity.

A human character may belong to any cultural group the DM permits. These cultures can differ widely in outlook, environment, and practice—ranging from settled imperial societies to nomadic tribes, seafaring clans, or isolated highland communities. Language, dress, and custom vary accordingly, and these distinctions are often more significant than any physical differences.

Humans have only one universal trait: they may be of any character class and advance to any level without restriction. This flexibility, combined with their adaptability, has allowed humans to establish and maintain large, complex societies that would be difficult for longer-lived or more specialized races to sustain.

Humans are generally more socially flexible than other races, showing a greater willingness to cooperate with non-humans despite differences in outlook or habit. This tendency, along with their ambition and organizational capacity, has made them a dominant political force in many regions.
#### Cultural Traits (Optional)
At the DM’s discretion, a human character may select (or be assigned) a cultural background that exists within the campaign setting. This represents upbringing, environment, and inherited tradition rather than innate ability. A culture typically grants **one minor benefit** and may impose **one minor limitation** - reflecting specialization - without eclipsing other races.

Possible examples include:
- **Northern (Hardy, Seafaring)**: +1 to checks involving endurance, cold exposure, or open-water navigation; may suffer a −1 penalty in extreme heat or arid conditions.
- **Steppe Nomad (Mounted, Mobile)**: +1 to riding or mounted combat-related checks; reduced familiarity with dense urban environments or seafaring.
- **Desert Dweller (Adapted, Resourceful)**: +1 to checks involving heat tolerance, water finding, or survival in arid terrain; discomfort or −1 penalty in cold climates.
- **Imperial (Organized, Educated)**: +1 to one knowledge-based or administrative proficiency; may have stricter social obligations or expectations.
- **Woodland (Wary, Self-Sufficient)**: +1 to foraging, tracking, or woodland movement; reduced familiarity with large settlements or formal institutions.

These benefits should be comparable in scope to a narrow proficiency bonus or situational modifier. They are not intended to rival demi-human racial abilities, merely to give human cultures mechanical identity and texture.

Characters begin play as adults, with minimum starting ages determined by race. Higher starting ages may be chosen with agreement of the DM. The Age Table may be used to randomly generate starting age and estimate natural lifespan under normal conditions. A character’s individual maximum lifespan is determined by the DM adding the maximum age modifier to the venerable age (the base maximum age) and should remain unknown to the player.

As characters age, their abilities change. At half of the race's base maximum age (Middle Age), Strength and Constitution decrease while Intelligence and Wisdom increase. At two-thirds of the base maximum age (Old Age), Strength, Dexterity, and Constitution decline further, while Wisdom increases again. Finally when they exceed the base maximum age (Venerable Age), physical attributes decline once more while Intelligence and Wisdom again improve. These effects are cumulative, however natural aging cannot reduce an ability score below either 3 or three points beneath the racial minimum for that ability, whichever is higher. Magical aging may affect a character in two distinct ways. Some effects physically alter the character’s body, increasing or decreasing (apparent) age and any corresponding physical ability adjustments are applied, but mental ability improvements - which reflect long-term experience and learning - are not. 

Other magical effects instead alter the character’s lifespan without changing their current age. In these cases, only their individual maximum lifespan, as determined by the DM, is modified - normal aging effects continue to occur at their usual stages. Magical aging may reduce a character's ability scores below the minimums given for natural aging.

Beyond mechanical traits, characters also possess personal characteristics such as appearance, voice, mannerisms, and temperament. These are not governed by tables. Players are encouraged to define these freely in order to create a distinctive and engaging character. Some traits may be known at creation, while others will emerge through play.
