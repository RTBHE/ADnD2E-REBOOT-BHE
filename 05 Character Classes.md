# Chapter 5: Character Classes
After choosing your character’s race, you select his character class. A character class represents a character’s profession, training, and the body of knowledge and skills he has developed prior to beginning an adventuring career. Each character is assumed to have received instruction or experience sufficient to survive early adventuring life. Advancement thereafter is achieved through experience gained during play.

Character classes are divided into four class groups according to general vocation: warrior, wizard, priest, and rogue. Each group shares common patterns of advancement, including Hit Dice progression, combat ability, and saving throw advancement. Within each group are individual classes that define specific abilities, restrictions, and special features.

Each player selects a class group, then chooses a specific class within that group.

Fighter, mage, cleric, and thief are the standard character classes. They represent broadly applicable archetypes suitable for most campaign worlds. All other classes are optional and may be included or excluded at the Dungeon Master’s discretion depending on the campaign setting.

### Class Overview
Each class group is described briefly below. Full mechanical details are presented in later sections.

#### Warriors
Warriors are trained in combat, tactics, endurance, and the use of weapons and armour. They rely primarily on physical ability and martial skill rather than magic or subtlety.

The **fighter** is a general combatant skilled in weapons and battlefield tactics.

The **paladin** is a holy warrior dedicated to ideals of righteousness, discipline, and divine purpose.

The **ranger** is a wilderness warrior skilled in survival, tracking, and skirmishing.

The **barbarian** is a primal warrior shaped by harsh environments and direct survival, relying on instinct and physical power.

#### Wizards
Wizards are arcane spellcasters who rely on study, knowledge, and disciplined research to shape magical forces into spells.

The **mage** is a generalist arcane caster trained in a wide range of magical disciplines.

**Specialist wizards** focus on a single school of magic, gaining greater mastery in exchange for reduced breadth.

#### Priests
Priests serve as intermediaries between mortal society and higher spiritual or cosmological powers. Through devotion, ritual, and sacred discipline, they channel divine magic in accordance with their faith or spiritual tradition.

The **cleric** is a generalist priest of organized religion, serving temples, pantheons, or structured faiths.

The **druid** is a priest of natural balance and primal forces, devoted to the cycles of life, death, and renewal.

A **priest of a specific mythos** represents specialized religious traditions devoted to a single deity, principle, or supernatural power.

The **shaman** serves as an intermediary between the mortal world and the spirit realms, communicating with spirits, ancestors, and unseen forces.

#### Rogues
Rogues are defined by trained expertise, adaptability, and non-traditional solutions to problems. They rely on agility, perception, guile, charm, stealth, and specialized knowledge rather than direct martial or magical power.

The rogue group includes a wide range of callings: thieves, spies, scouts, infiltrators, investigators, burglars, smugglers, performers, diplomats, con artists, explorers, and many other roles that depend on skill rather than strength or formal authority.

Rogues are not defined by criminality. While some operate outside the law or at its margins, others work as legitimate professionals such as agents, guides, entertainers, or specialists in security and investigation. What unites rogues is method rather than morality or legality: they solve problems through skill, preparation, and opportunistic advantage.

All rogues share a common foundation of trained abilities expressed through a unified skill system, typically resolved using percentile dice. These abilities represent practiced expertise rather than innate power, though magic, equipment, and circumstance may modify their effectiveness.

The thief, bard, and ninja represent different expressions of the rogue tradition:

- the **thief** as a specialist in stealth, infiltration, and acquisition
- the **bard** as a master of influence, knowledge, and reputation
- the **ninja** as a disciplined operative of concealment and precision

### Ability Score Requirements
Each character class has minimum ability score requirements. A character must meet these requirements to select that class.

If a character does not meet the requirements for any class, the Dungeon Master may allow ability scores to be adjusted or rerolled.

The Dungeon Master may also allow a character to increase one or more ability scores to meet the minimum requirements of a desired class. This is intended specifically to permit class qualification and should not normally result in ability scores exceeding the limits of character creation as determined by the campaign rules.

### Experience Points
Experience points represent a character’s growth in skill, knowledge, and capability through adventuring.

Characters earn experience by completing adventures and performing actions appropriate to their class. Different classes emphasize different forms of advancement: warriors gain more experience from combat, rogues from skill use and problem-solving, priests from service and spiritual deeds, and wizards from magical and intellectual achievements.

When sufficient experience is accumulated, a character advances in level and gains new abilities.

Some campaigns may require training time or other conditions for advancement, as determined by the Dungeon Master.

### Level
Level measures a character’s overall power and advancement. Unless the DM indicates an adventure will start at a higher level all characters begin at 1st level and improve through experience gained in play.

Each class progresses at a different rate and gains distinct abilities at higher levels.

### Prime Requisites

A classes’ prime requisites are the ability score or scores most important to that class. While specific class roles or playstyles may rely on different skills, as a generalization:

- Warriors rely on Strength
- Wizards rely on Intelligence
- Priests rely on Wisdom
- Rogues rely on Dexterity or Charisma

Prime requisites influence experience gain and class suitability.

A character who has a score of 16 or higher in all prime requisites for his class receives a 10% bonus to all experience point awards.

## Warriors Group
The warrior group encompasses those classes whose place in the world is determined primarily by martial skill, physical courage, discipline, and mastery of arms. Fighters, paladins, and rangers all belong to the warrior group, though each approaches the arts of war from a different tradition and philosophy.

Warriors are the most capable combatants in the game. They possess the broadest access to arms and armour, the greatest durability in battle, and the fastest progression in direct martial ability. While other classes may learn to fight competently, warriors are specialists in violence, battlefield survival, and armed conflict.

Warriors are expected to stand in danger where others cannot. Their class features and combat progression reflect not merely technical skill with weapons, but also battlefield awareness, endurance, aggression, and the ability to exploit fleeting opportunities in combat.

#### Weapons and Armour
Warriors may become proficient in and make use of any weapon or armour, though faith, character kit, or other rules may restrict choices for a specific warrior. Unlike members of other class groups, warriors are not subject to broad class-based prohibitions on particular categories of arms or armour, though certain class abilities may function only while using specific equipment or while avoiding particular encumbrances.

#### Hit Dice and Constitution Bonuses
Warriors gain 1d10 hit points per level from 1st through 9th level. Constitution bonuses to hit points apply normally during these levels.

Beginning at 10th level, warriors no longer gain additional Hit Dice. Instead, they gain a flat 3 hit points per level thereafter, and no further Constitution bonuses are applied.

#### Magical Items and Spell Use
Warriors possess comparatively limited access to magical disciplines. Unless otherwise specified by class abilities, warriors cannot cast wizard spells and are restricted in their use of magical items intended for priests or wizards.

Paladins and rangers gain limited access to divine spellcasting at higher levels as described in their class entries.

#### Multiple Attacks
As warriors increase in experience, their growing combat skill allows them to strike more frequently in melee combat. This progression reflects increasing efficiency, aggression, and control in close combat rather than supernatural speed.

## Fighter

**Ability Requirement:** Strength 9  
**Prime Requisite:** Strength  
**Alignment:** Any  
**Races Allowed:** Any

Fighters are professional men-at-arms, soldiers, mercenaries, knights, duelists, guardsmen, raiders, and warlords. More than any other class, the fighter is defined by practical mastery of violence. Where other warriors combine martial ability with religious devotion, wilderness skill, or supernatural gifts, the fighter relies upon training, discipline, experience, and battlefield judgement.

The fighter is the most flexible and broadly capable martial class. Fighters may be heavily armoured infantry, lightly equipped skirmishers, mounted lancers, archers, sailors, gladiators, disciplined pikemen, tribal champions, or hardened veterans of countless campaigns. The class imposes few assumptions about background, culture, or fighting style.

Strength is the principal attribute of the fighter, governing both accuracy and effectiveness in melee combat. A high Dexterity is also valuable, improving defence, missile combat, and battlefield survivability.

### Weapon Specialization and Mastery
Fighters are the only class able to attain weapon specialization and weapon mastery.

Weapon specialization represents intensive training with a particular weapon or closely related group of weapons, granting improved accuracy, damage, speed, and combat efficiency. Higher degrees of mastery represent increasingly refined technical skill, battlefield familiarity, and instinctive command of a weapon.

A fighter is not required to specialize in any weapon. Many fighters instead choose broad proficiency across numerous arms, allowing them to adapt to changing circumstances and available equipment.

Rules for weapon proficiency, specialization, and mastery are presented in the Proficiencies chapter.

### Magical Items
Although fighters cannot cast spells, they may make use of a wide variety of magical items. Fighters may use enchanted weapons, armour, shields, potions, protection scrolls, many rings, and other magical items not specifically restricted to priests or wizards.

Because fighters lack magical versatility, magical arms and armour often become an important part of their long-term advancement and battlefield effectiveness.

### Strongholds and Followers
Upon reaching 9th level, a fighter’s reputation becomes sufficient to attract the service of professional soldiers, mercenaries, and lesser warriors seeking wealth, security, status, or the prestige of serving a renowned commander.

To attract such followers, the fighter must possess or establish a stronghold, fortress, manor, or similar domain capable of supporting armed retainers. Once established, warriors and men-at-arms begin entering the fighter’s service.

These followers are not automatons nor fanatics. They expect competent leadership, fair treatment, regular payment, and a reasonable expectation of survival and success. Poor leadership, abuse, unpaid wages, or catastrophic campaigns may result in desertion, indiscipline, or mutiny.

In addition to ordinary troops, the fighter also attracts a more loyal household guard or personal retinue composed of experienced and dependable soldiers. Such retainers expect superior treatment, equipment, and pay, but are generally more dependable than common mercenaries.

The composition of a fighter’s followers depends heavily upon the campaign setting, the fighter’s reputation, culture, alignment, and manner of rule. The DM should determine appropriate followers consistent with the surrounding world rather than relying solely upon random generation.

A fighter may own land, fortifications, or holdings before reaching 9th level. However, only upon attaining this degree of fame and reputation does the fighter naturally attract significant numbers of professional followers.

### Paladin

**Ability Requirements:** Strength 12, Constitution 9, Wisdom 13, Charisma 17  
**Prime Requisites:** Strength, Charisma  
**Alignment:** Lawful Good  
**Races Allowed:** Human

The paladin is a warrior bound to the service of law and righteousness through sacred oaths, religious devotion, and personal discipline. Paladins are expected to embody courage, justice, mercy, honesty, and self-sacrifice, standing as examples to both common folk and rulers alike. They are not merely skilled warriors, but champions entrusted with divine purpose and moral responsibility.

The path of the paladin is demanding. Many possess courage or martial skill, but far fewer can consistently uphold the discipline, restraint, humility, and moral certainty required of a true paladin. A paladin must remain lawful good in both conviction and conduct. This obligation extends beyond outward obedience to law and includes sincerity of purpose, respect for just authority, protection of the innocent, and opposition to cruelty, corruption, and evil.

Paladins are not expected to be naïve, humorless, or incapable of practical judgement. They may employ caution, strategy, secrecy, or deception when necessary to oppose evil or protect the innocent. However, cruelty, treachery for personal gain, abuse of authority, knowingly consorting with evil, or the willing commission of evil acts are incompatible with the calling of the paladin.

#### Conduct and Fall from Grace
A paladin who knowingly commits a chaotic act must seek out a lawful good priest of at least 7th level, confess the transgression, and undertake whatever penance is required. Minor failings, errors in judgement, unavoidable lesser evils, or actions taken in ignorance do not automatically strip a paladin of status, though repeated misconduct, selfishness, or disregard for lawful conduct may place the character in spiritual peril.

A paladin who knowingly and willingly commits an evil act immediately loses paladin status. This fall is ordinarily irrevocable. The character forever after functions as a fighter of the same level, retaining existing experience points but permanently losing all paladin abilities and privileges. A fallen paladin may not thereafter gain weapon specialization or mastery beyond what had already been attained prior to the fall.

A paladin who commits evil while magically compelled, dominated, or otherwise deprived of meaningful free will loses access to paladin abilities until atonement is completed, but is not considered permanently fallen. Such a character functions as a fighter of equal level until the required penance, quest, or sacred duty is fulfilled. No experience points are gained while the paladin remains under such disgrace.

The DM should distinguish carefully between intentional evil, moral failure under pressure, tragic necessity, ignorance, manipulation, and honest disagreement over lawful conduct. The paladin is held to a high standard, but should not become unplayable through excessive moral absolutism or adversarial interpretation.

#### Detect Evil Intent
By concentrating for one full minute, a paladin may sense the presence of malign supernatural evil or deliberate hostile intent within 60 feet in a chosen direction.

This ability detects creatures, enchantments, or supernatural influences strongly aligned with evil, as well as individuals presently intending serious harm, betrayal, murder, or similarly malevolent acts. It does not reveal general selfishness, petty vice, political disagreement, or moral imperfection, nor does it automatically distinguish between lawful and chaotic evil.

The paladin may use this ability as often as desired.

#### Divine Protection
Paladins receive a +2 bonus to all saving throws.

In addition, a paladin is immune to all natural diseases. Supernatural afflictions such as lycanthropy, mummy rot, or curses are not prevented unless specifically noted otherwise.

#### Lay on Hands
A paladin may heal wounds through the laying on of hands. Once per day, the paladin may restore a total number of hit points equal to 2 points per level. This healing may be applied to the paladin or to another creature touched by the paladin.

The healing need not be granted all at once, but the full daily amount must be used within a single encounter or scene at the DM’s discretion.

#### Cure Disease
A paladin may cure disease through touch. This ability functions once per week for every five levels attained:

| Paladin Level | Uses per Week |
| ------------- | ------------- |
| 1–5           | 1             |
| 6–10          | 2             |
| 11–15         | 3             |
| 16–20         | 4             |

This ability cures diseases, poisons of biological origin, and similar bodily afflictions, but does not remove curses, magical transformations, or supernatural conditions unless specifically allowed by the DM.

#### Aura of Protection
Paladins are surrounded by a palpable aura of divine authority extending 10 feet in all directions. Summoned evil creatures and creatures of strongly evil supernatural nature suffer a –1 penalty to attack rolls while within this aura, regardless of their target.

Creatures affected by the aura instinctively recognize its source, even if the paladin is disguised or concealed.

#### Turning Undead and Fiends
Beginning at 3rd level, a paladin gains the ability to turn undead and fiends through force of faith and divine authority. The paladin turns such creatures as a priest two levels lower than the paladin’s current level.

More information on turning is found in the Priest Class Description and in the Combat chapter.

#### Special Mount
At 4th level or later, a paladin may undertake the calling or quest that leads to a sacred mount entering the paladin’s service. Although commonly a warhorse, the mount may instead be another creature appropriate to the paladin’s culture, faith, or divine patron.

The paladin does not simply summon the mount into existence. Rather, the bond is formed through providence, quest, vision, divine guidance, or extraordinary circumstance. Such mounts are exceptionally intelligent, loyal, and courageous companions rather than ordinary trained animals.

The exact nature of the mount is determined by the DM.

#### Divine Spellcasting
Beginning at 9th level, paladins gain the ability to cast priest spells drawn from the spheres of Combat, Divination, Healing, and Protection.

Paladin spellcasting follows the normal rules for priestly magic except as follows:

- Paladins use the Paladin Spell Progression table.
- Paladins do not gain bonus spells for high Wisdom.
- Paladins cannot cast from priest scrolls unless otherwise permitted.
- Paladins may not use priest-exclusive magical items unless specifically allowed to warriors.

Paladin spells represent divine favour granted in recognition of continued devotion and faithful service rather than formal priestly training.

#### Holy Swords
If a paladin acquires and properly bears a holy sword, the weapon projects a circle of power with a 10-foot radius whenever drawn and wielded. Within this area, hostile magic may be dispelled as if by dispel magic cast at a level equal to the paladin’s own level.

Holy swords are exceptionally rare relics of divine significance, not ordinary magical weapons.

### Restrictions and Obligations
The powers granted to a paladin are balanced by strict obligations of conduct, charity, discipline, and service.

#### Wealth and Possessions
A paladin may not hoard wealth or pursue riches for personal luxury. The paladin may retain sufficient funds to maintain equipment, support dependents and retainers, uphold obligations, construct or maintain a stronghold, and continue the practical work of the paladin’s calling.

Excess wealth must be donated to religious, charitable, or otherwise worthy causes consistent with lawful good principles. Such donations may not be used to circumvent these restrictions by transferring personal wealth to companions, retainers, or secondary holdings under the paladin’s indirect control.

#### Tithes
A paladin must tithe no less than one tenth of all income and treasure acquired. This tithe must be paid to a lawful good religious institution, sacred order, charitable cause, or similar organization at the earliest reasonable opportunity.

#### Magical Items
A paladin may possess no more than ten magical items in total. Of these, no more than:

- one suit of magical armour,
- one magical shield,
- four magical weapons,
- and four additional magical items

may be retained at any time. Magical ammunition does not count toward this limit.

Paladins are expected to treat magical items as sacred tools in service of duty rather than objects of greed or prestige.

#### Followers and Associates
Unlike fighters, paladins do not automatically attract mercenary followers upon establishing a stronghold. However, they may hire soldiers, servants, and specialists of suitable conduct and reputation.

A paladin will ordinarily accept companions of differing alignments provided they are not evil and do not habitually engage in cruelty, corruption, treachery, or wanton wrongdoing. The paladin is expected to oppose evil acts and discourage immoral conduct, but is not required to abandon every ally over minor failings, cultural differences, or moral imperfection.

Paladins rarely tolerate persistent association with knowingly evil individuals except under extraordinary circumstances directly tied to a greater good, redemption, or sacred duty.

### Ranger

**Ability Requirements:** Strength 13, Dexterity 13, Constitution 14, Wisdom 14  
**Prime Requisites:** Strength, Dexterity, Wisdom  
**Alignment:** Any Good  
**Races Allowed:** Human, Elf, Half-Elf

The ranger is a warrior of the wild frontiers: hunter, scout, tracker, warden, and wandering protector. Rangers survive through vigilance, fieldcraft, patience, and practical knowledge of the wilderness. Though formidable warriors, rangers rely as much upon stealth, endurance, and judgement as upon strength of arms.

Rangers are commonly associated with forests and unsettled lands, but the class is not limited to a single environment. A ranger may be a mountain scout, marsh guide, desert rider, frontier outrider, coastal warden, or hunter of dangerous creatures wherever civilization meets the untamed world.

All rangers are committed in some fashion to the protection of life, the defence of communities, or the opposition of predatory evil. Unlike paladins, however, rangers are not bound to rigid codes of law or religious hierarchy. Rangers often operate independently, beyond the reach of courts and kingdoms, and may employ stealth, ambush, deception, or irregular tactics where necessary.

#### Arms and Armour
Rangers may use any weapon and wear any armour. However, several ranger abilities function only while wearing studded leather armour or lighter and, at the DM’s discretion, while carrying no more than a light encumbrance .

The ranger’s abilities depend heavily upon mobility, awareness, and freedom of movement. Heavy armour interferes with stealth, woodcraft, and rapid fighting techniques.

#### Two-Weapon Fighting
When wearing studded leather armour or lighter, a ranger may fight with a weapon in each hand without suffering the normal penalties for two-weapon fighting. The ranger may not use a shield while fighting in this manner.

When wearing heavier armour, the ranger may still fight with two weapons, but suffers the standard penalties.

This ability reflects extensive training in aggressive skirmishing and mobile combat rather than supernatural speed or coordination.

#### Tracking
Rangers gain the Tracking proficiency without spending a proficiency slot.

In addition, the ranger gains a +1 bonus to Tracking checks for every three levels attained:

| Ranger Level | Tracking Bonus |
| ------------ | -------------- |
| 1–2          | —              |
| 3–5          | +1             |
| 6–8          | +2             |
| 9–11         | +3             |
| 12–14        | +4             |
| 15–17        | +5             |
| 18–20        | +6             |

Rules for tracking are found in the Proficiencies chapter.

#### Stealth and Concealment
While wearing studded leather armour or lighter, a ranger may attempt to move silently and hide in shadows.

The ranger’s chances are given on the Ranger Abilities table, modified by race and Dexterity where appropriate. In natural outdoor environments, the ranger uses the listed values normally. In urban, subterranean, or otherwise unnatural environments, these chances are halved.

Unlike a thief, the ranger’s stealth abilities represent camouflage, fieldcraft, patience, and careful movement rather than specialized infiltration techniques.

#### Favoured Enemy
Before advancing to 2nd level, every ranger must select a species or broad creature type that serves as the ranger’s principal enemy. Common choices include orcs, goblinoids, giants, trolls, undead, lizard men, or similar threats common to the campaign region. The DM must approve the choice.

When attacking creatures of the chosen type, the ranger gains a +4 bonus to attack rolls.

This hatred is rarely subtle. The ranger suffers a –4 penalty to reaction rolls when dealing with creatures of the hated type, and will ordinarily seek them out in battle ahead of other threats unless doing so would be tactically foolish or endanger innocents.

The favoured enemy represents long experience, bitter enmity, cultural duty, personal trauma, or dedicated study rather than supernatural destiny.

The DM may permit especially broad or especially narrow enemy choices only where appropriate to the campaign.

#### Animal Empathy
Rangers possess an instinctive understanding of natural animals and their behaviour.

Domestic animals and non-hostile wild creatures may generally be calmed, approached, handled, or befriended by a ranger without the need for a check under ordinary circumstances.

When dealing with frightened, aggressive, trained, or dangerous animals, the ranger may attempt to alter the creature’s reaction through careful behaviour and animal handling. The creature must make a saving throw versus rods. The saving throw suffers a –1 penalty, with an additional –1 penalty for every three ranger levels beyond 1st.

If the save fails, the ranger may shift the creature’s reaction one category in a favourable direction.

This ability is non-magical and does not grant direct communication, mind control, or supernatural command over animals.

The ranger must ordinarily approach openly and without obvious fear for the ability to function.

#### Divine Spellcasting
Beginning at 8th level, rangers gain limited access to priest spells drawn from the Animal and Plant spheres.

Ranger spellcasting follows the normal rules for priestly magic except as follows:

- Rangers use the Ranger Spell Progression table.
- Rangers do not gain bonus spells for high Wisdom.
- Rangers may not use priest scrolls unless specifically permitted.
- Rangers may not use priest-exclusive magical items unless otherwise noted.

Ranger spells represent spiritual harmony with the natural world, instinctive wisdom, and the favour of powers tied to nature rather than formal priestly authority.

#### Followers
At 10th level, a ranger’s reputation and deeds attract loyal followers from among frontier folk, scouts, hunters, woodsmen, and creatures of the wild.

The ranger attracts 2d6 followers over the course of several months. These followers may include humans, demi-humans, animals, or more unusual beings associated with the wilderness and borderlands. Some may seek service directly, while others are encountered naturally during adventures.

The exact composition of these followers should reflect the campaign setting and the ranger’s personal history rather than relying solely upon random tables.

Followers are initially loyal, but their continued service depends upon the ranger’s conduct and leadership. Rangers gain no supernatural ability to communicate with animal followers beyond ordinary means.

A ranger is never required to accept followers and may release them freely.

Unlike fighters, rangers do not automatically attract soldiers through strongholds or political authority.

#### Conduct and Restrictions
Rangers must remain of good alignment. Though not bound to strict lawful behaviour, rangers are expected to oppose cruelty, predation, corruption, and needless destruction.

A ranger who knowingly and willingly commits an evil act permanently loses ranger status and thereafter functions as a fighter of equal level. Excess experience points beyond the maximum for that fighter level are lost.

A ranger who commits evil unwillingly, under coercion, magical influence, deception, or extreme necessity loses access to ranger abilities until the wrong is corrected and appropriate atonement completed. During this time the ranger gains no additional experience.

The exact nature of such atonement depends upon the offence committed and should arise naturally from the campaign rather than through arbitrary punishment.

#### Solitary Nature
Rangers are traditionally independent wanderers uncomfortable with courts, large organizations, and permanent authority. Until reaching 8th level, a ranger may not maintain permanent henchmen, mercenary companies, or large numbers of retainers.

This restriction does not prevent ordinary travelling companions, temporary allies, guides, or similar associates.

#### Wealth
Rangers do not ordinarily accumulate large stores of wealth or luxury possessions. A ranger may possess whatever equipment and treasure can reasonably be carried, transported, or actively maintained.

Excess wealth is generally converted into practical portable valuables, expended in pursuit of the ranger’s duties, distributed among companions, or donated to worthy causes and communities.

Rangers are not forbidden from owning property or maintaining hidden refuges, cabins, lodges, or similar holdings, but they rarely become sedentary nobles or wealthy landholders.

## Wizards Group
Wizards are practitioners of arcane magic who study the hidden structures and energetic principles underlying the material substance of the multiverse. Through study, discipline, experimentation, and the manipulation of complex formulae, they learn to shape the forces associated with the “weft” of the Weave: the aspect of reality most closely tied to substance, transformation, energy, and elemental expression.

The weft is understood by many arcane scholars to radiate from the Inner Planes, giving material form and energetic structure to existence itself. Fire, stone, lightning, shadow, motion, growth, decay, positive and negative energy, and the countless interactions between elemental forces all arise from the interplay of these underlying patterns. Arcane magic manipulates these structures directly.

Unlike priests, who invoke the ordering principles of the cosmos through alignment with the warp, wizards impose learned formulae upon the weft itself. Arcane magic therefore relies not upon faith or spiritual authority, but upon knowledge, perception, symbolic logic, force of intellect, and precise technical execution.

Because the Weave is impossibly vast and cannot be fully understood through any single theory, many different arcane traditions exist. Some wizards classify magic according to schools of effect such as illusion, necromancy, or invocation. Others organize magic through elemental theory, philosophical principles, operational methods, symbolic correspondences, or entirely different metaphysical frameworks.

These differing traditions are not necessarily contradictory. Rather, each represents a partial model through which mortal minds attempt to interpret and manipulate structures far greater than themselves.

The wizard group therefore encompasses a wide variety of arcane practitioners, including:

- mages,
- specialist wizards,
- elementalists,
- wild mages,
- shadow mages,
- and other arcane traditions introduced in particular campaign settings or supplements.

Though their methods differ, all wizards share certain defining traits. They rely upon written magical formulae, individual spell acquisition, disciplined memorization, experimentation, magical research, and the deliberate manipulation of arcane energies.

Wizards are among the most intellectually powerful and versatile characters in the game world, but also among the most physically vulnerable. Their studies leave little time for martial training, and many arcane techniques require freedom of movement, careful breath control, precise gestures, and uninterrupted concentration. As a result, most wizards are limited in the armour and weapons they can employ effectively.

Despite these limitations, few forces rival the versatility of arcane magic. Wizards may conjure flame from empty air, transform matter, alter perception, traverse great distances, command unseen forces, summon extraplanar beings, reshape living bodies, bind power into enchanted objects, or uncover secrets long buried by time and death. Their pursuit of knowledge often leads them toward mysteries, powers, and dangers that others were never meant to encounter.
#### Arcane Magic
Arcane spells are formulae that shape and direct the energetic and elemental structures of the weft. These formulae combine symbolic language, gesture, mental discipline, material correspondences, and precise energetic sequencing in order to produce supernatural effects.

Unlike divine magic, arcane spellcasting does not depend upon doctrine, moral alignment, or spiritual authority. Any sufficiently trained and capable mind may potentially learn to manipulate arcane forces, though the dangers involved are considerable.

Arcane magic is inherently less stable than divine magic because it manipulates the substantive energies of reality directly rather than invoking higher ordering principles. Miscalculation, flawed understanding, environmental instability, or uncontrolled energetic interaction may produce unintended consequences ranging from harmless anomalies to catastrophic magical disasters.

Different wizardly traditions interpret arcane manipulation through different theoretical frameworks. A necromancer, elementalist, wild mage, illusionist, and philosopher may all describe the same magical phenomenon in radically different terms while still producing functional results.

These competing systems coexist because no mortal tradition fully comprehends the totality of the Weave.

#### Spellbooks and Magical Formulae
All wizards maintain some form of spellbook, grimoire, codex, scroll collection, mnemonic diagram, or other repository of magical knowledge. A spellbook contains the symbolic structures and procedural formulae required to shape arcane energies safely and consistently.

A wizard’s spellbook is both laboratory journal and personal reference work, recording the spells, theories, experiments, annotations, and discoveries accumulated throughout the wizard’s life. Most wizards guard their spellbooks obsessively, for the loss of such knowledge can cripple years or even decades of study.

Arcane spells cannot ordinarily be improvised from casual reading alone. Before a spell may be cast quickly and reliably, the wizard must study and memorize the formula, imprinting a temporary energetic pattern within the mind. Casting the spell releases this prepared structure, erasing it from memory until studied again.

However, wizards may sometimes cast certain spells directly from their spellbooks or ritual texts without prior memorization. Such ritual casting requires substantially more time than normal spellcasting and demands uninterrupted concentration, full material components, and access to the written formula throughout the casting process. Ritual spells are unsuitable for combat or other immediate situations, but allow the wizard greater flexibility in employing rarely needed magic.

#### Learning Spells
Wizards do not automatically gain complete knowledge of new spells when advancing in level. Arcane knowledge must be discovered, traded, researched, purchased, copied, stolen, or otherwise acquired during play.

When a wizard encounters a spell not already known, he may attempt to learn it by studying the formula and deciphering its structure. Success is determined by the character’s chance to learn spells based upon Intelligence.

Once understood, the spell may be recorded within the wizard’s spellbook and prepared normally thereafter.

Because arcane traditions differ greatly in notation, symbolism, and theory, deciphering unfamiliar magical writings can be difficult even for experienced wizards.

#### Magical Research
The wizard group possesses unmatched ability to conduct magical research. Through experimentation, calculation, and prolonged study, wizards may:

- create new spells,
- modify existing spells,
- develop magical theories,
- analyze supernatural phenomena,
- and discover previously unknown magical principles.

Such research is expensive, time-consuming, and potentially dangerous. Failed experiments may produce unstable magical effects, destructive surges, planar disturbances, curses, or unintended transformations.

The exact results and risks of magical research are determined by the DM.

#### Magical Item Creation
Wizards are the foremost creators of permanent magical items. Through arcane formulae and specialized rituals, they may bind supernatural forces into physical objects, granting them enduring magical properties.

At higher levels, wizards may create scrolls, potions, enchanted weapons, protective devices, rings, rods, wands, staves, and many other magical objects, provided they possess the necessary knowledge, spells, materials, facilities, and time.

Even magical items intended primarily for use by warriors, priests, rogues, or other classes often depend upon arcane construction techniques during their creation. Priests may sanctify holy relics, druids may attune sacred natural objects, but the stabilization and binding of enduring magical structure usually falls within the domain of wizardly craft.

#### Arms and Armour
Wizards receive little martial training and may use only weapons requiring minimal specialized combat instruction. Most wizard classes are limited to daggers, darts, knives, slings, and staves, though specific traditions may alter these restrictions.

Wizards normally cannot cast arcane spells while wearing armour. Arcane spellcasting demands exact movement, breath control, symbolic gesture, and uninterrupted energetic shaping; armour interferes with the precision required to manipulate arcane structures safely.

This limitation reflects practical and technical constraints rather than any inherent incompatibility between metal and magic.

#### Hit Dice and Constitution Bonuses
Wizards gain 1d4 hit points per level from 1st through 10th level. Constitution bonuses apply normally during these levels.

Beginning at 11th level, wizards no longer gain additional Hit Dice. Instead, they gain a flat 1 hit point per level thereafter, and no further Constitution bonuses are applied.

## Priests Group
Priests are intermediaries between the mortal world and the powers beyond it. Through devotion, ritual, discipline, sacrifice, and sacred knowledge, priests gain the ability to invoke divine magic and act in service of religious, spiritual, or cosmological principles greater than themselves.

The priest group encompasses many different traditions. Clerics serve organized religions, temples, and established pantheons; druids preserve ancient natural and cosmic orders; shamans mediate between mortals and the spirit world through ecstatic rites, visions, and ancestral traditions. Though their practices differ greatly, all priests derive power from faith, spiritual authority, and recognized connection to the higher powers and structures underlying the cosmos.

Priests are not merely believers. Common worshippers may pray, sacrifice, or observe religious customs without possessing any divine power whatsoever. A priest is instead one who has been entrusted with sacred authority and granted the ability to act directly as an agent of divine or spiritual forces.

Priests combine spiritual power with practical capability. Though less specialized in combat than warriors, priests are expected to defend their faith, protect their followers, confront supernatural threats, and survive the dangers associated with their calling.

#### Divine Magic
All priests cast divine spells. Unlike wizardly magic, priestly magic is not produced through personal manipulation of arcane forces, but through alignment with the greater powers and principles that shape the structure of the multiverse itself.

The powers worshipped by priests—whether gods, pantheons, cosmic principles, ancestral powers, or sacred natural orders—are understood by many scholars and theologians to be connected fundamentally to the “warp” of the Weave: the ordering structure that grants meaning, identity, purpose, and form to existence.

Through prayer, ritual, devotion, sacrifice, and obedience to sacred obligations, priests learn to invoke and direct these forces in limited ways. Divine magic is therefore neither purely external nor purely personal. A priest does not simply command power, but acts as an intermediary through whom higher structures of reality are expressed.

Because divine magic depends upon spiritual alignment and sacred relationship rather than technical formulae, priests are constrained by doctrine, obligation, taboo, and the expectations of the powers they serve. These differing relationships to divine power are represented mechanically through categories known as spheres of influence. A priest’s class or specific faith determines which spheres are available and whether access is major or minor.

A priest with major access to a sphere may eventually cast all spells within it. A priest with minor access may cast only spells of 1st through 3rd level from that sphere.

The spheres are:

- All
- Animal
- Astral
- Charm
- Combat
- Creation
- Divination
- Elemental
- Guardian
- Healing
- Necromantic
- Plant
- Protection
- Summoning
- Sun
- Weather

Additional spheres may exist in particular campaign settings or mythoi.

Divine magic should not be confused with mysticism. Priests invoke powers associated with the ordering structure of the cosmos itself, while mystics cultivate mastery over the essential cohesion that exists between the threads of the Weave. Though both may produce supernatural effects, their methods, philosophies, and underlying principles are fundamentally different.

#### Clerics, Druids, and Other Priests
Not all priests serve religion in the same manner.

Clerics are the most common and broadly trained form of priest. In most cultures, clerics serve not merely a single deity in isolation, but an entire religious tradition or pantheon. Though a cleric may favour particular gods, saints, or aspects of worship, they are ordinarily expected to understand and uphold the broader sacred order recognized by their faith.

This distinguishes the cleric from more specialized priesthoods devoted exclusively to a single deity, esoteric mystery, philosophical tradition, or other supernatural patron.

In practical terms, clerics are generalists within their religious tradition much as mages are generalists among arcane practitioners.

Druids serve ancient natural mysteries, seasonal cycles, sacred landscapes, and primal balances that bind the ordered structure of the cosmos to the living world.

Other priestly traditions may exist depending upon the campaign setting, including shamans, spirit-speakers, ancestral priests, philosophers, tribal holy men, or specialized priesthoods dedicated to individual deities or supernatural powers.

The exact structure and role of priesthoods varies greatly between cultures and mythologies.

#### Arms and Armour
Priests are trained for battle, though not to the same degree as warriors. Their permitted weapons and armour vary according to faith, doctrine, spiritual tradition, and religious obligation.

Some priesthoods forbid edged weapons, others require ritual arms, and still others permit unrestricted martial training. Restrictions are described in the individual class entries or faith descriptions.

#### Hit Dice and Constitution Bonuses
Priests gain 1d8 hit points per level from 1st through 9th level. Constitution bonuses apply normally during these levels.

Beginning at 10th level, priests no longer gain additional Hit Dice. Instead, they gain a flat 2 hit points per level thereafter, and no further Constitution bonuses are applied.

#### Wisdom and Bonus Spells
Priests are the only class group able to gain additional spell slots from high Wisdom scores.

These bonus spells reflect exceptional spiritual insight, devotion, discipline, and closeness to divine power rather than superior memory or intellect.

### Cleric
Clerics are trained intermediaries of established religious traditions who serve as defenders of the communities placed under the care of their faith. Their role is not merely devotional, but protective: they preserve the spiritual, social, and metaphysical stability of the world as interpreted through their religion’s understanding of the divine order.

Within most cultures, clerics serve pantheons, syncretic religious systems, or broader sacred traditions that encompass multiple divine beings, aspects, or principles of a unified cosmological order. Individual clerics may hold personal devotion to particular gods or spirits, but their authority derives from their place within a wider religious structure rather than exclusive service to a single patron.

This distinguishes the cleric from more specialized priesthoods devoted exclusively to a single deity, esoteric mystery cult, philosophical tradition, or powerful supernatural patron. Such priests act primarily as guardians of doctrinal purity and the integrity of their specific faith, whereas clerics are generalists tasked with sustaining the broader spiritual and communal framework within which many beliefs coexist.

Clerics combine spiritual authority with practical capability. They are expected to defend the faithful, protect settlements, oppose supernatural threats, and maintain the stability of the communities that depend upon their guidance.

#### Arms and Armour
Clerics may wear any armour and use shields without restriction.

Weapon use is shaped by religious practice rather than martial doctrine alone. Clerics are typically restricted to bludgeoning weapons, reflecting both symbolic and practical aspects of their role within a shared sacred order.

Bludgeoning weapons are understood as the most direct and least ritually disruptive form of violence available to mortals. They avoid the symbolic separation inherent in shedding blood between peoples who may all fall under overlapping divine jurisdictions within a broader pantheon or sacred system.

This restriction also reflects the cleric’s role as a stabilising agent within a multi-deity or multi-aspect religious framework, where excessive bloodshed risks escalating spiritual tension between competing divine influences.

Some traditions may interpret or modify this restriction according to their doctrines, but such variation is specific to individual faiths rather than the cleric class as a whole.

#### Divine Magic
Clerics cast divine spells through alignment with the ordering structures of the cosmos as expressed through their faith tradition.

In cosmological terms, clerical magic arises from the “warp” of the Weave—the structural principle that gives form, meaning, and purpose to reality. Clerics act as conduits through which these higher ordering forces are expressed in the mortal world.

Clerics are generalists among priests, with broad access to divine spheres of influence. Their magic reflects the needs of organized religious life: healing, protection, guidance, judgment, purification, and the containment or correction of supernatural disorder.

Clerics have major access to all spheres except Animal, Plant, Weather, and Elemental, and minor access to the Elemental sphere.

Divine magic is not a transactional relationship with a single patron, but a structured alignment between the cleric, their faith tradition, and the broader cosmological order that faith interprets and serves.

#### Turning Undead
Clerics are granted authority over undead creatures—entities that represent disruption or inversion of the proper relationship between form, essence, and spiritual continuity.

Undead are treated as violations of the intended balance of existence, whether arising from necromantic corruption, cursed persistence, or metaphysical rejection of death’s proper role.

Clerics may repel, command, or destroy undead depending on alignment, doctrine, and circumstance. Evil-aligned clerics may instead assert dominion over such forces, reflecting alternate theological interpretations of authority over death and unlife.

#### Followers and Temple Authority
At higher levels, clerics who establish a recognised place of worship may attract followers drawn from the faithful, temple staff, militia, and lay adherents of their tradition.

These followers represent the cleric’s role as a stabilising figure within a living religious community. Their loyalty is grounded in faith, social order, and institutional authority rather than personal obedience alone.

The number and nature of followers depend on the cleric’s reputation, deeds, and religious context, and they arrive gradually over time rather than as a single organised force.

Additional retainers may be hired, but do not possess the same inherent loyalty as devoted followers.

#### Religious Strongholds
High-level clerics may establish formal religious strongholds such as temples, abbeys, monasteries, shrines, or sanctuaries.

At 9th level, such institutions may receive formal recognition from the cleric’s religious tradition, granting institutional support such as reduced construction costs, donated labour, or protective influence depending on the nature of the faith.

Earlier construction is possible, but without recognition these holdings function as private expressions of faith rather than sanctioned institutions of the broader religious order.

### Priest of a Specific Mythos
In some campaigns, there are priests who do not serve broad pantheons or general religious traditions, but instead belong to tightly defined faiths dedicated to a single deity, spirit, principle, or supernatural power. These are collectively referred to as priests of a specific mythos.

Unlike clerics, who operate as generalist priests within larger religious systems, priests of a specific mythos derive their abilities entirely from the doctrines, strictures, and metaphysical relationship of a single faith. Their powers, limitations, and role in the world are defined by that faith rather than by a broader religious framework.

Such priests are not a separate class, but a specialized expression of the priest group. Their differences from clerics arise from their faith rather than from their fundamental nature as priests.

The structure, obligations, and capabilities of a priest of a specific mythos are determined by the details of that faith, which should be defined in cooperation between player and Dungeon Master.

At minimum, each mythos should define:

- **Requirements** (including ability scores, alignment constraints, or initiation conditions)
- **Arms and Armour Restrictions**
- **Spell Access** (via spheres of influence and any modifications to progression)
- **Granted Powers** (unique abilities or divine effects)
- **Ethos** (behavioural expectations, taboos, and obligations)

These elements replace or override the corresponding assumptions of the generic cleric where appropriate.

#### Requirements
All priests must have a Wisdom score of at least 9. Beyond this, each mythos may impose additional requirements reflecting its nature, expectations, or initiatory structure. These may include minimum ability scores, alignment restrictions, or behavioural prerequisites.

A war deity may require strength and endurance. A principle of healing or insight may require wisdom and empathy. More esoteric or demanding powers may impose stricter conditions still.

#### Arms and Armour
Weapons and armour available to a priest of a specific mythos are defined by that faith.

Some mythoi impose strict limitations on weapon use for symbolic, ritual, or doctrinal reasons. Others grant extensive martial training or demand particular sacred arms. These restrictions are expressions of the faith itself rather than a universal rule of priesthood.

#### Spell Access
Each mythos determines its own access to spheres of influence. These define which forms of divine magic are available to its priests.

A deity, spirit, or principle will grant major or minor access to specific spheres according to its nature and portfolio. This determines the spells available to its priests at each level.

The balance of access across spheres replaces the broader spell access pattern of the cleric class.

#### Granted Powers
In addition to spellcasting, each mythos grants one or more special powers reflecting its nature.

These powers are expressions of the priest’s relationship with the mythos rather than universal clerical abilities. They may enhance combat ability, alter reactions, influence emotions, protect followers, or manifest supernatural effects appropriate to the faith.

No fixed list is provided; powers should be designed to reflect the identity and role of the mythos in the campaign world.

#### Ethos
All priests of a specific mythos are bound by the expectations of their faith. This includes behavioural requirements, taboos, obligations, and codes of conduct.

Ethos defines how the priest must act in order to maintain their connection to the mythos. Failure to uphold these expectations may result in loss or restriction of powers at the DM’s discretion.

Ethos replaces the general behavioural assumptions of clerics, and is specific to each faith.

#### Design Considerations
Priests of a specific mythos should be balanced holistically against clerics and other priests in the campaign.

A mythos that grants broad weapon access or powerful granted abilities should typically impose greater restrictions on spell access, ethos, or other aspects of the class. Conversely, highly restrictive mythoi may grant stronger divine abilities to compensate.

The goal is not uniformity, but parity of overall capability expressed through different forms of limitation and advantage.

## Druid

**Alignment:** True Neutral  
**Races Allowed:** Human, Half-Elf  
**Hit Dice:** d8  
**Weapons Allowed:** Club, dagger, dart, scimitar, sickle, sling, spear, staff  
**Armour Allowed:** Padded, leather, hide  
**Shields:** Wooden only  
**Spellcasting:** Divine

Druids are priests of the natural order and guardians of the cycles that sustain the living world. Where clerics preserve the stability of mortal communities through organized religion, druids concern themselves with the balance of the greater natural and cosmic systems upon which all life depends.

To the druid, civilization is neither inherently good nor inherently corrupt. Settlements, agriculture, hunting, war, growth, decay, and death are all parts of the natural cycle. Druids become hostile only when these cycles are disrupted through reckless exploitation, unnatural corruption, or attempts to impose permanent dominion over forces that must remain in balance.

Druids are also an example of a priesthood defined by a specific mythos. Their powers arise not from service to civic pantheons or institutional religion, but from attunement to the primal order of the living world itself: the cycles of season, weather, growth, death, and renewal.

#### Arms and Armour
Druids reject worked metal armour and most heavily manufactured weapons, viewing them as symbols of excessive separation from the natural world and the cycles that sustain mortal life.

A druid may wear only padded, leather, or hide armour, and may use only wooden shields. Magical versions of otherwise permitted armour and shields are allowed provided they remain fundamentally natural in form.

Druid weapons are similarly restricted to those associated with hunting, husbandry, ritual practice, or simple natural craftsmanship: club, dagger, dart, scimitar, sickle, sling, spear, and staff.

#### Divine Magic
Druidic magic reflects attunement to the living and elemental forces of the natural world rather than the ordered structures emphasized by clerics.

Druids have major access to the spheres of:

- All
- Animal
- Elemental
- Healing
- Plant
- Weather

They have minor access to the Divination sphere.

Druids do not receive the broader protective and institutional spell access typical of clerics, but instead command powerful magics relating to nature, growth, weather, beasts, and elemental forces.

Unlike clerics, druids cannot turn undead.

#### Druidic Language
All druids know a secret druidic tongue in addition to their normal languages. This language does not require a proficiency slot.

The language is intentionally limited in vocabulary and structure, focusing primarily upon nature, weather, geography, living things, ritual practice, and druidic philosophy. Druids guard it carefully as a means of recognition and communication within their order.

#### Woodland Affinity
Beginning at 3rd level, druids may identify natural plants, animals, and pure water with perfect accuracy.

At the same level, druids may move through natural overgrowth—including briars, dense brush, thorn thickets, and similar terrain—at normal movement speed and without leaving a trail.

Also beginning at 3rd level, druids gradually learn the languages of woodland and wilderness beings. A druid learns one such language at 3rd level and one additional language each level thereafter. Typical choices include the tongues of dryads, treants, centaurs, fauns, pixies, sprites, and similar creatures tied closely to the natural world.

#### Resistance to Enchantment
At 7th level, druids become immune to charm effects cast by woodland creatures such as dryads, nixies, and similar beings.

#### Shapechanging
Beginning at 7th level, a druid gains the ability to assume the form of natural animals.

The druid may shapechange up to three times per day:

- once into a mammal,
- once into a bird,
- and once into a reptile.

The chosen form may range in size from very small creatures to approximately the size of a black bear. The druid gains the movement abilities, natural attacks, Armor Class, and physical capabilities of the assumed form, but retains his own mind, personality, and hit points.

Upon assuming a new form, the druid also heals 1d6 × 10% of all current damage suffered.

Clothing and one handheld item in each hand merge into the new form and reappear when the druid returns to normal shape.

#### Ethos
Druids must remain true neutral in alignment.

This neutrality does not imply indifference or passivity. Druids may oppose tyranny, defend the innocent, wage war against corruption, or ally temporarily with powerful forces of good or evil. However, druids reject ideologies that seek absolute dominance, permanent stasis, reckless destruction, or unnatural disruption of the world’s essential cycles.

Druids are expected to protect wilderness, preserve natural balance, and resist exploitation that threatens long-term ecological or metaphysical stability.

They are often patient, subtle, and unforgiving in pursuing these goals.

#### Mistletoe and Sacred Symbols
Mistletoe serves as a primary sacred symbol of the druidic tradition and is required for many druidic spells.

Traditionally, ritual mistletoe is harvested beneath the full moon using a specially prepared golden or silver sickle. Improperly gathered mistletoe weakens the effectiveness of spells requiring it, typically granting targets improved saving throws or reducing spell potency at the DM’s discretion.

#### Druidic Hierarchy
Unlike clerics, druids belong to a highly structured initiatory hierarchy tied to geographic regions and sacred natural authority.

At lower levels, druids are considered initiates within the broader order. Advancement beyond 11th level requires acceptance into the ruling circles of the druidic hierarchy, which maintain strict limits on the number of druids permitted at each rank within a given region.

Positions within the hierarchy are traditionally earned through challenge, succession, recognition, or vacancy rather than simple accumulation of experience alone. Such contests are not always lethal, but they are always binding.

As druids rise in rank they attract initiates, attendants, and lesser druids who serve under their authority within the natural order.

#### The Grand Druid and Hierophants

At the highest levels of the hierarchy stand the Great Druids, the Grand Druid, and finally the Hierophant Druids who transcend ordinary mortal concerns and become living embodiments of druidic philosophy.

Hierophants no longer gain additional spell progression, instead acquiring increasingly profound supernatural abilities tied to transcendence of mortality, elemental attunement, and union with the deeper natural order.

These powers include:

- immunity to natural poisons,
- freedom from aging infirmity,
- alteration of appearance,
- hibernation,
- and eventual ability to enter and survive within the Elemental Planes.

The precise structure and advancement of the higher druidic hierarchy is detailed in the accompanying tables and hierarchy rules.

### Rogues Group
Rogues survive through adaptability, skill, cunning, and opportunism. Where warriors rely upon strength and priests upon faith, rogues prosper through agility, perception, charm, stealth, and a willingness to go where others will not.

The rogue group encompasses many different callings. Thieves, scouts, spies, burglars, swindlers, performers, confidence artists, explorers, investigators, tomb robbers, assassins, and countless other professions all fall within the broad category of rogue. Some operate openly as respected specialists or entertainers, while others survive at the edges of law, custom, and polite society.

Not all rogues are criminals, though many acquire reputations that invite suspicion. Some are adventurers, scouts, agents, diplomats, couriers, or wandering performers; others are smugglers, cutpurses, fences, or informants. Many rogues drift between legitimate and illicit work as circumstance demands.

Rogues combine talents drawn from many disciplines. Though not as formidable in battle as warriors, nor as magically powerful as priests or wizards, they compensate through versatility and practical skill. Rogues can use a wide variety of weapons, armour, and magical items, though individual classes may impose further restrictions.

The rogue group also includes traditions that rely upon secrecy, espionage, disguise, infiltration, and unconventional methods of conflict. Future supplements or campaign settings may introduce additional rogue classes, such as ninjas, spies, or other specialized operatives.

#### Rogue Abilities
All rogues possess specialized skills reflecting agility, awareness, and practical experience.

Most rogues are adept climbers, capable of scaling surfaces that would challenge ordinary men. They are observant and attentive, often noticing faint sounds, hidden movement, or signs of danger overlooked by others. Rogues also tend to possess broad familiarity with languages, codes, dialects, and obscure forms of writing encountered during their travels.

Many rogues possess additional talents such as stealth, sleight of hand, disguise, infiltration, forgery, acrobatics, lock-picking, trap-handling, or performance. The exact abilities available depend upon the specific rogue class.

Most rogue abilities are resolved using percentile rolls. A result equal to or less than the listed chance of success indicates the attempt succeeds. These chances are usually modified by level, Dexterity, race, equipment, circumstances, and the difficulty of the task attempted.

Rogue abilities represent practised expertise rather than supernatural powers, though magical effects may enhance or hinder them normally.

#### Arms and Armour
Rogues favour mobility, concealment, and versatility over heavy protection. Most rogue classes permit a moderate range of weapons and lighter forms of armour, though exact restrictions vary by class.

Certain rogue abilities may function only while wearing light armour or avoiding excessive encumbrance.

#### Magical Items
Rogues are able to use a wider variety of magical items than most characters outside the wizard group. Depending upon class, rogues may employ magical scrolls, potions, rings, enchanted weapons and armour, and certain specialized magical devices.

The exact magical items available depend upon the individual rogue class.
#### Hit Dice and Constitution Bonuses
Rogues gain 1d6 hit points per level from 1st through 10th level. Constitution bonuses apply normally during these levels.

Beginning at 11th level, rogues no longer gain additional Hit Dice. Instead, they gain a flat 2 hit points per level thereafter, and no further Constitution bonuses are applied.

### Thief

**Ability Requirement:** Dex 9  
**Prime Requisite:** Dex  
**Alignment:** Any except Lawful Good  
**Races Allowed:** Any  
**Hit Dice:** d6  
**Weapons Allowed:** Club, dagger, dart, hand crossbow, knife, lasso, short bow, sling, broad sword, long sword, short sword, staff  
**Armour Allowed:** Leather, padded leather, studded leather, elven chain  
**Shields:** No  
**Spellcasting:** No

The thief is the archetypal rogue: agile, adaptable, opportunistic, and skilled in the arts of stealth, infiltration, deception, and survival. Thieves thrive where caution, subtlety, and quick reflexes matter more than brute force or open confrontation.

Though often associated with burglary, smuggling, confidence schemes, espionage, or other illicit professions, not all thieves are hardened criminals. Scouts, treasure hunters, spies, scouts, investigators, urban guides, and wandering adventurers may all possess the same specialized talents. In many stories and legends, the thief appears as a clever trickster or folk hero: a scoundrel perhaps, but not always a villain.

Because their training emphasizes stealth and precision rather than battlefield combat, thieves are limited in the weapons and armour they can employ effectively. A thief may use only the weapons listed above and may wear only leather, padded leather, studded leather, or elven chain armour. Shields are not permitted.

Many thieving abilities depend upon agility, freedom of movement, and careful control of body and equipment. When wearing armour heavier than leather, the thief suffers penalties to several class abilities.

#### Thieving Skills
Thieves possess a number of specialized skills unavailable to most other characters. These abilities are resolved using percentile dice. If the result rolled is equal to or less than the character’s adjusted skill score, the attempt succeeds.

The standard thieving skills are:

- Pick Pockets
- Open Locks
- Find/Remove Traps
- Move Silently
- Hide in Shadows
- Detect Noise
- Climb Walls
- Read Languages

To determine a thief’s starting skill values, begin with the base scores listed on [[Thieving Skill Base Scores]]. Apply any modifiers for race, Dexterity, and armour worn as described on [[Table 27 - Thieving Skill Racial Adjustments]], [[Thieving Skill Dexterity Adjustments]], and [[Table 29 - Thieving Skill Armor Adjustments]].

##### Skill Advancement
Beginning thieves receive 60 discretionary percentage points at 1st level to distribute among their thieving skills. No more than 30 points may be assigned to any single skill at character creation.

Each time the thief gains a level, he receives an additional 30 points to distribute. No more than half of these points may be assigned to a single skill. No skill may exceed 95%, including all modifiers from race, Dexterity, and armour.

At the DM’s discretion, some portion of these advancement points may be required to improve skills actively used during recent adventures.

Some racial or Dexterity modifiers may reduce a skill below 0%. In such cases, the thief must first raise the skill to at least 1% before it can be attempted successfully.

A thief wearing only magical protective garments such as _bracers of defense_ or an ordinary cloak without bulky armour uses the “No Armour” column for skill adjustments.

##### Pick Pockets
This skill governs filching small objects from another person, palming items unnoticed, concealing small objects, and performing simple acts of sleight of hand.

A failed attempt does not necessarily mean the attempt was noticed. To determine whether the victim detects the attempt, subtract three times the victim’s level from 100. If the thief’s Pick Pockets roll equals or exceeds this number, the attempt is noticed. Thus, an inexperienced victim notices only the clumsiest attempts, while seasoned adventurers are much harder to deceive.

At the DM’s option, highly experienced thieves are more difficult to detect. If the thief’s level exceeds the victim’s, the difference may be added to the detection threshold.

A thief may attempt to pick pockets repeatedly, though discovery often carries consequences more severe than mere failure.

##### Open Locks
This skill allows a thief to pick conventional locks, manipulate combination mechanisms, and solve puzzle locks or concealed latching systems.

Opening locks normally requires proper thieves’ tools. Improvised tools may impose penalties at the DM’s discretion depending upon their suitability.

Attempting to pick a lock requires 1d10 rounds. A thief may attempt to open a particular lock only once per experience level. Failure indicates the mechanism exceeds the character’s current expertise.

##### Find and Remove Traps
This skill enables a thief to locate and disable small mechanical traps and alarms such as poisoned needles, spring blades, gas vents, hidden catches, and warning bells.

It does not ordinarily apply to large structural hazards such as collapsing ceilings, moving walls, or massive mechanical devices.

The thief must be able to inspect and manipulate the object directly. Searching for a trap requires 1d10 rounds. If a trap is found, the thief understands its general function, though not necessarily every detail of its construction.

Disarming a trap also requires 1d10 rounds. Failure usually means the trap cannot presently be defeated. A roll of 96–100 when attempting removal triggers the trap accidentally.

Magical or invisible traps are significantly more difficult to disable; chances of success are typically halved.

##### Move Silently
A thief may attempt to move with exceptional quietness, reducing movement to one-third normal speed. Success is determined secretly by the DM.

Successful silent movement improves the thief’s ability to avoid detection, surprise opponents, or maneuver into advantageous positions such as a backstab.

Moving silently provides no benefit if the thief remains plainly visible.

##### Hide in Shadows
A thief may conceal himself within shadows, foliage, curtains, alcoves, or similar cover.

This ability functions only when the observer is not actively watching the thief conceal himself. Once hidden, the thief must remain nearly motionless, though small deliberate movements are permitted.

Complete darkness does not aid this skill, as hiding in shadows relies partly upon visual misdirection and camouflage rather than invisibility.

Magical effects capable of revealing hidden or invisible creatures may expose a concealed thief.

##### Detect Noise
Thieves are unusually attentive to subtle sounds. By remaining still and concentrating for one round, a thief may detect faint noises beyond doors, through passages, or in nearby chambers.

The thief must remove any helmet or head covering and have relative silence nearby in order to listen effectively.

##### Climb Walls
Thieves are exceptional climbers capable of scaling rough surfaces, sheer walls, and even many smooth surfaces without specialized equipment.

While climbing, however, the thief remains vulnerable and generally unable to fight effectively.

##### Read Languages
Through long exposure to maps, codes, records, and obscure writings, thieves gradually acquire the ability to decipher unfamiliar languages.

Beginning at 4th level, a thief gains a chance to interpret nonmagical written material. Understanding remains imperfect and depends upon the success of the roll. A successful attempt represents partial or complete comprehension proportional to the character’s skill percentage.

Each document requires a separate roll, even if written in the same language. A thief may attempt to decipher a given document only once per experience level.

Languages already known through proficiencies or normal literacy require no roll.

#### Backstab
Though poor at prolonged toe-to-toe combat, thieves excel at striking unsuspecting opponents from advantageous positions.

When attacking from behind against a target unaware of the impending attack, the thief gains the normal rear attack benefits and inflicts additional damage according to [[Table 30 - Backstab Damage Multipliers]].

Only the first successful strike benefits from the multiplier. Once combat begins openly, the opportunity is lost.

Backstab functions only against creatures with vulnerable anatomies and identifiable weak points. Most humanoids are valid targets; many amorphous, gigantic, or anatomically alien creatures are not.

#### Thieves’ Cant
All thieves learn a specialized criminal argot known as thieves’ cant.

Thieves’ cant is not a separate language, but a collection of slang, coded phrases, gestures, euphemisms, and layered meanings embedded within ordinary speech. It is used to identify fellow rogues, exchange warnings, discuss illicit business, and conceal meaning from outsiders.

Because it relies upon an existing spoken language, two thieves must still share a common tongue in order to communicate effectively.

#### Use Scrolls
At 10th level, a thief gains a limited ability to employ wizard and priest scrolls through practical familiarity with magical notation and ritual structure.

This understanding remains imperfect. Whenever a thief attempts to use a scroll, there is a 25% chance the spell is misread or improperly invoked, causing unintended or reversed effects as determined by the DM.

Such mishaps are rarely beneficial.

#### Strongholds and Followers
Thieves rarely construct obvious castles or fortresses. Instead, they prefer concealed headquarters, fortified safehouses, hidden compounds, or seemingly innocuous buildings concealing more secretive operations beneath.

A thief’s stronghold often reflects his profession and personality: a disguised warehouse, a maze-like manor filled with traps and hidden passages, a secluded lodge used by scouts and spies, or a covert urban network concealed behind legitimate businesses.

Upon reaching 10th level, a thief’s reputation attracts followers. These may be criminals, scouts, informants, apprentices, burglars, fences, or other rogues seeking protection, employment, or instruction.

The thief attracts 4d6 followers. Their exact nature may be determined by [[Table 31 - Thief's Followers]] or selected by the DM to suit the campaign.

Thieves are notoriously territorial. Rival gangs operating within the same region frequently come into violent conflict unless some arrangement or hierarchy can be established.

### Bard

**Ability Requirements:** Dex 12, Int 13, Cha 15  
**Prime Requisites:** Dex, Cha  
**Alignment:** Any Neutral  
**Races Allowed:** Human, Half-elf  
**Hit Dice:** d6  
**Weapons Allowed:** Any  
**Armour Allowed:** Up to chain mail  
**Shields:** No  
**Spellcasting:** Arcane

Bards live by wit, talent, charm, and adaptability. They are storytellers, musicians, heralds, wanderers, messengers, chroniclers, and negotiators, moving easily between courts, camps, taverns, and marketplaces. Inspired by the poets, skalds, troubadours, and oral historians of the real world, the bard in the game world preserves culture, carries news, immortalizes deeds, and navigates the shifting boundaries between social classes and peoples. Their required neutrality reflects the degree of detachment necessary to move among competing factions and survive in uncertain company.

The bard is the consummate generalist: capable in combat, skilled in stealth and social intrigue, and possessed of a small but versatile command of arcane magic. Though they fight as rogues, bards may become proficient with any weapon. They may wear armour up to chain mail, though shields are forbidden. Like thieves, bardic abilities suffer penalties when heavier armour is worn.

All bards are accomplished performers. Every bard begins play proficient as a singer, chanter, poet, or storyteller, and also gains proficiency with one musical instrument of the player’s choice. These proficiencies are gained without cost. If the bard later spends proficiency slots on musical instruments, each slot grants proficiency with two instruments rather than one.

#### Arcane Magic
During their travels, bards inevitably acquire fragments of magical learning: a half-remembered incantation from a hedge wizard, a song with hidden power, a forgotten charm copied from a noble’s library, or the secret refrain of an old ballad. Bardic magic is eclectic, opportunistic, and rarely systematic.

Bards cast arcane spells and maintain spell books much like wizards. Intelligence determines the number of spells a bard may learn and the chance to learn any particular spell. Bards follow the normal restrictions on memorization and spell use that apply to wizards, including the inability to cast arcane spells while wearing armour.

Unlike wizards, bards cannot specialize in a school of magic, nor do they automatically gain spells when advancing in level. The spells known at 2nd level are determined randomly or chosen by the DM, subject to normal spell learning checks. Additional spells must be discovered during play and copied into the bard’s spell book through study or adventure.

Because their magic is informal and pieced together through experience, bards often favour practical, entertaining, deceptive, or socially useful spells over purely destructive magic. Their caster level is equal to their bard level.

#### Bardic Talents
The bard’s true strength lies not in battle or spellcraft, but in versatility, knowledge, and influence over others. To represent this, bards possess a limited selection of rogue abilities.

The base percentages for bardic abilities are given on the appropriate table. These are modified by race, Dexterity, and armour in the same manner as thief abilities. After all adjustments are made, the player distributes 20 discretionary percentage points among the bard’s abilities at 1st level. Each additional level grants another 15 points to distribute. No ability may exceed 95%.

Bards possess the following rogue abilities:

- Climb Walls
- Detect Noise
- Pick Pockets
- Read Languages

These abilities function exactly as described for thieves.

#### Influence and Inspiration
A bard can influence the reactions of listeners through performance, speech, poetry, music, humour, or storytelling. This ability functions only when the audience is not already engaged in immediate combat. Those listening must attempt a saving throw vs. paralyzation. The save is modified by −1 for every three levels of the bard.

Those who fail may have their reaction shifted one category toward either friendliness or hostility, at the bard’s choice. Those who succeed instead shift one category in the opposite direction. The performance must suit the audience and circumstances; a solemn dirge may calm frightened soldiers, while a rousing war song may inflame a mob.

Bards may also inspire allies before battle. If companions are aware of an impending conflict, the bard may spend three rounds performing to grant one of the following benefits to allies within 10 feet per level:

- +1 to attack rolls
- +1 to saving throws
- +2 morale

The inspiration lasts one round per level of the bard. Once expired, it cannot be renewed during the same battle, though troops withdrawn from combat may be inspired again later.

#### Counter Song
Bards can oppose magical effects conveyed through speech, music, chanting, poetry, or similar performance. By singing, reciting, or playing continuously, the bard grants allies within 30 feet protection against such attacks.

To maintain the counter song, the bard may take no action other than slow movement. If struck or forced to make a saving throw, the effort is disrupted. The bard must successfully save vs. spell for the counter song to function. If successful, the magical effect is negated for protected listeners; if failed, the effect resolves normally.

This ability functions against magically empowered speech, songs, compulsions, and similar effects, but not against ordinary verbal spell components or command words.

#### Lore and Knowledge
Bards gather scraps of knowledge everywhere they travel. All bards can read and write their native language if it possesses a written form, and all possess broad familiarity with the local history, legends, notable families, songs, battles, and scandals of their wider homeland.

In addition, bards have a 5% chance per level to identify the general nature of a magical item through examination and recollection of stories, songs, symbols, or historical references. Exact properties are not revealed, but the bard may discern whether an item is cursed, intelligent, legendary, associated with a notable wielder, or possessed of unusual significance.

#### Strongholds and Followers
At 9th level, a bard may establish a hall, theatre, manor, school, inn, courtly household, or similar stronghold. Upon doing so, the bard attracts 10d6 loyal 0-level soldiers, retainers, performers, students, or attendants over time. These followers are not automatically replaced if slain or dismissed.

At 10th level, the bard gains the ability to use magical writings normally restricted to other classes, including scrolls and certain magical books. However, bardic understanding of formal magic is imperfect. Whenever using such an item, there is a 15% chance the bard misreads or misinterprets it, causing the effect to function incorrectly or disastrously at the DM’s discretion.
