# Chapter 8 - Money and Equipment
## Money and Wealth
Although characters possess skills, training, and special abilities, they cannot adventure effectively without proper equipment. Arms, armour, mounts, tools, provisions, transportation, lodging, and hired services all require money.
### Coinage

The standard AD&D monetary system is based on coinage measured in copper, silver, electrum, gold, and platinum pieces. Unless otherwise noted, all prices in this book are listed using the standard exchange rates below.
##### Standard Exchange Rates

| Coin                | CP  | SP   | EP   | GP    | PP    |
| ------------------- | --- | ---- | ---- | ----- | ----- |
| Copper Piece (CP)   | 1   | 1/10 | 1/50 | 1/100 | 1/500 |
| Silver Piece (SP)   | 10  | 1    | 1/5  | 1/10  | 1/50  |
| Electrum Piece (EP) | 50  | 5    | 1    | 1/2   | 1/10  |
| Gold Piece (GP)     | 100 | 10   | 2    | 1     | 1/5   |
| Platinum Piece (PP) | 500 | 50   | 10   | 5     | 1     |

Copper and silver coins are the currencies most commonly used in daily trade. Gold pieces are comparatively rare in ordinary circulation and chiefly serve as a standard of account for large transactions, taxes, fines, mercantile contracts, and the valuation of property. Platinum and electrum coins are uncommon in most civilized realms and are often associated with older empires, treasure hoards, or foreign kingdoms.

The names, values, and purity of coins may vary between nations, cities, or historical periods. A campaign setting may employ different exchange rates, local currencies, debased coinage, trade bars, shell money, or other systems of value. The DM determines the coinage standards used within the campaign world.

### Wealth Beyond Coinage

Coin is only one form of wealth. Land, livestock, trade goods, jewelry, legal rights, military obligations, tolls, taxation privileges, and precious objects may all represent significant value.

Jewelry in particular commonly serves as portable wealth. Silver armbands, jeweled rings, worked gold chains, and similar items may readily be exchanged for goods or services where minted currency is scarce or distrusted.

The purchasing power of money also varies considerably by region. A single gold piece may represent substantial wealth in an isolated village yet hold little significance in a prosperous trade city. War, famine, taxation, shortages, trade disruption, or the actions of wealthy adventurers can rapidly alter local prices.

Foreign or politically suspect coinage may also be discounted or refused entirely. Money changers and merchants commonly exchange unfamiliar currency for local coinage, usually charging commissions ranging from 10% to 30%.

## Starting Money

Beginning characters receive a quantity of starting funds used to purchase equipment prior to play. These funds may represent savings, gifts, military pay, inheritance, stolen wealth, or any other suitable origin determined by the player.

To determine starting funds, roll the appropriate dice for the character’s class group.

##### Starting Funds

| Character Group | Starting Funds    |
| --------------- | ----------------- |
| Warrior         | 5d4 × 10 gp       |
| Wizard          | (1d4 + 1) × 10 gp |
| Rogue           | 2d6 × 10 gp       |
| Priest          | 3d6 × 10 gp       |

Multi-class characters without kits use the most favourable starting funds available among their classes.

Characters that adopt a character kit (See Chapter 6) may receive different starting funds or equipment than what is given above.
##### Priest Characters
Priest characters are normally expected to spend their starting funds on equipment, offerings, vestments, supplies, and other necessities appropriate to their faith. Once initial purchases are completed, all but a few remaining coins are typically surrendered to the priesthood, temple, or religious order supporting the character.

Priests may not ordinarily transfer or lend starting funds to other player characters unless specifically permitted by the DM or by the customs of their faith.

## Equipment Lists
The equipment tables presented in this chapter include examples of adventuring gear, weapons, armour, mounts, clothing, trade goods, tools, provisions, transportation, and services that may be available.

Not every item is available in every town, city, region, or campaign. The technological level, culture, geography, trade conditions, and historical era of the setting may restrict or eliminate certain goods. Likewise, unusual or exotic equipment may only be obtainable in major cities or specialized markets.

Prices listed throughout this chapter represent average values under normal economic conditions. Local scarcity, war, taxation, distance, monopolies, seasonal shortages, or sudden influxes of treasure may significantly increase costs. Particularly isolated or dangerous regions may impose substantial markups on food, mounts, weapons, lodging, or other essential goods.

Characters may attempt to bargain over prices where appropriate, although routine haggling is generally assumed to produce only minor variations unless roleplayed in detail or supported by proficiencies, reaction adjustments, or exceptional circumstances.

Tables are supported by text descriptions where appropriate - not every piece of equipment is described as some things have obvious function, form, and purpose.
### Social Restrictions
Certain realms may restrict the purchase, possession, or use of certain items to members of particular classes, guilds, priesthoods, or noble houses or declare specific items to be contraband (many specialized tools for thieves fall in this category). Such restrictions may range from detailed regulations concerning colours, materials, or styles of dress to broad prohibitions against commoners bearing weapons or wearing armour. Wearing restricted attire without proper status may result in fines, confiscation, ridicule, arrest, or hostile reactions.

Conversely, characters appropriately equipped and dressed for their apparent station may receive favourable reaction modifiers when dealing with nobles, officials, merchants, priesthoods, or other social institutions.
### Clothing
Clothing may immediately identify occupation, wealth, nationality, religion, and social standing. Characters dressed appropriately for their station may receive more favourable reactions in civilized society, while visibly poor, foreign, or inappropriate dress may impose penalties at the DM’s discretion.

Peasants and laborers typically wear layered wool or linen garments intended for warmth and durability: tunics, braies, cloaks, aprons, hoods, and heavy boots. Clothing is practical, patched repeatedly, and seldom decorative.

Merchants, soldiers, guildsmen, and prosperous travellers favour fitted garments, dyed fabrics, quality cloaks, riding boots, belts, gloves, and ornamented accessories.

Priests, scholars, and wizards commonly wear robes, cassocks, layered tunics, hoods, and other conservative garments reflecting education or religious office.

Nobles and courtiers favour fine tailoring, imported fabrics, jewellery, decorative belts, elaborate hats, embroidered gloves, rare dyes, and fur trim. Court fashion often values appearance over practicality.

### Clothing Descriptions

**Baldric or Bandoleer:** A shoulder strap used to carry weapons, pouches, horns, or tools.

**Braies:** Loose trousers or undergarments commonly worn by laborers and travelers.

**Cassock:** A long coat or outer garment commonly associated with priests, scholars, soldiers, and officials.

**Chemise:** A simple linen undertunic or shirt worn beneath outer garments.

**Coif:** A close-fitting cloth cap worn alone or beneath helmets and hats.

**Doublet:** A fitted outer jacket commonly worn by merchants, nobles, and townsmen.

**Girdle:** A decorated belt or sash worn around the waist.

**Ruff:** A stiff or pleated formal collar associated with aristocratic fashion.

**Surcoat:** A loose outer garment worn over a tunic, robe, or armour.

**Tabard:** A sleeveless overgarment bearing heraldic colors or insignia.

### Equipment for Mounts

Mounts require specialized equipment for riding, transport, warfare, and husbandry. While many animals can be led with simple ropes or improvised gear, proper tack and harness improve control, comfort, and safety for both rider and beast. Military mounts may also be equipped with barding to protect them in combat.

**Bit and Bridle:** A bridle consists of straps fitted around a mount's head together with a metal bit placed in its mouth. Reins attached to the bit allow the rider to direct and control the animal. Riding and war mounts are normally assumed to be equipped with a suitable bit and bridle.

**Bridoon:** A reinforced military bridle fitted with a snaffle bit and heavy reins. Bridoons are more resistant to cutting, snaring, and battlefield damage than ordinary bridles and are commonly used by cavalry.

**Cart Harness:** A set of straps, traces, collars, and fittings used to attach a draft animal to a wagon, cart, plow, or similar vehicle. Harnesses are normally constructed for a particular class of animal and may not fit significantly larger or smaller beasts.

**Cavesson:** A specialized training bridle used when breaking, schooling, or handling mounts. Additional rings permit the attachment of lead ropes and training lines. Characters using Animal Handling or Animal Training proficiencies frequently employ cavessons when working with valuable or difficult animals.

**Halter:** A simple arrangement of straps or rope fitted around a mount's head and used for leading, tying, or securing the animal when not ridden.

**Horseshoes and Shoeing:** Domesticated horses, ponies, and similar mounts commonly wear iron shoes to protect their hooves from wear. Proper shoeing improves endurance and reduces the likelihood of lameness during extended travel. The listed cost includes a complete set of shoes and professional fitting.

**Saddle:** Saddles distribute weight across a mount's back while improving stability for both animal and load.

A riding saddle is designed to carry a rider and provides greater control, comfort, and security than riding bareback. A pack saddle is a reinforced frame intended for transporting equipment, provisions, trade goods, and other cargo; it is not designed for carrying riders. A war saddle is a heavy military saddle fitted with high pommels, cantles, straps, and attachment points intended to keep a rider secure during combat.

Characters possessing an appropriate Riding proficiency are assumed to know how to use a riding or war saddle. At the DM's discretion, characters riding without a saddle may suffer penalties to proficiency checks or attempts to remain mounted during difficult manoeuvres or combat.

**Saddle Bags:** Saddlebags are paired bags suspended from either side of a saddle and used to carry equipment, provisions, and personal belongings.

**Saddle Blanket:** A padded blanket placed between the saddle and the mount. It protects the animal from chafing, helps distribute weight, and reduces the likelihood of the saddle shifting during travel.

**Spurs:** Spurs are metal attachments fastened to a rider's boots and used to encourage a mount to greater effort or speed. Ordinary use has no special game effect, though they may be required to control spirited or poorly trained animals at the DM's discretion.

**Stirrups:** Stirrups are foot supports suspended from either side of a saddle. They improve balance, aid mounting, and provide stability in combat.

Mounted warriors equipped with stirrups can deliver more effective charges and are less likely to be unhorsed. Riders without stirrups who suffer a significant impact or are struck while mounted may be required to make a saving throw vs. Paralyzation or fall from their mount.

**Yoke:** A wooden frame used to harness draft animals for pulling wagons, plows, sledges, and similar burdens. Horse yokes are generally lighter and more flexible than those intended for oxen.

**Bridle Cutter:** A hooked cutting tool designed to sever an opponent's reins during mounted combat. In addition to its intended purpose, a bridle cutter may be used as a weapon. It inflicts 1d4 points of damage against Small or Medium creatures and 1d3 points of damage against Large creatures.

#### Barding
Barding is armour designed specifically for mounts. Warhorses, elephants, and other combat-trained animals represent a significant investment, and barding provides valuable protection against missiles, melee attacks, and battlefield hazards, protecting the head, neck, chest, flanks, and hindquarters of the mount. Half barding, which protects only the front half of the mount is also available; it's reduced weight and cost make it common among light cavalry and frontier forces.

The Armour Class provided by barding depends on the type of armour as shown in the table below. Unless otherwise noted, barding follows the same general construction and appearance as the corresponding type of armour that may be worn by characters. Construction times given are for full barding, half barding requires 2 weeks less than given.

Not all mounts are capable of functioning in every type of barding. Light warhorses, riding horses, and ponies can normally wear only padded or leather barding (AC6) while medium warhorses can wear up to chain barding (AC4). When using half barding, such mounts may wear barding one category heavier than normally permitted (AC 5 for light warhorses and riding horses, AC 3 for medium warhorses). Heavy warhorses can wear any form of barding.

Flying mounts can normally wear only leather, padded, or magical barding. Aquatic creatures cannot ordinarily wear barding unless specially designed or magical equipment is available.

Barding must be carefully fitted to both mount and rider. Poorly fitted armour may chafe, restrict movement, or cause injury at the DM's discretion. Barded mounts are generally unsuited for use as pack animals, as the armour already consumes much of the creature's carrying capacity.

Leather or padded barding requires approximately 15 minutes to remove and 30 minutes to fit. Other types of barding require approximately 30 minutes to remove and one hour to fit.

Creatures possessing natural Armour Classes superior to those of ordinary mounts may still benefit from barding. Refer to the rules given under the heading "Creatures with Natural Armour" later in this chapter. 

Some campaigns may permit piecemeal barding assembled from individual sections of armour. Such equipment is less protective than a complete suit but may be cheaper, easier to obtain, or salvaged from battlefield remains. It may also be the only practical option for unusual mounts that would otherwise require custom barding to be crafted.

A suit of barding consists of six principal sections: a head piece, neckguard, breast plate, two flank guards, and a crupper protecting the hindquarters. Individual sections protect only the areas they cover. The DM may resolve attacks against the Armour Class of the piecemeal suit as a whole, against the Armour Class of a specific barding section (such as for a called shot), or against the mount's natural Armour Class when an attack strikes an unprotected area. The latter is usually determined by the direction of attack (front, flank, or rear).

To determine the Armour Class of a piecemeal suit, total the AC Bonus values (given in table below) of all six barding sections (0 for pieces not worn), divide the result by five, and round fractions down. Subtract the resulting value from AC 10 to determine the suit's effective Armour Class. Even when all sections are of the same armour type, piecemeal barding remains less effective than a properly fitted suit unless a skilled armourer spends time refitting and rebuilding the pieces.

| Barding Type                     | AC Bonus |
| -------------------------------- | -------- |
| Leather or Padded                | +1       |
| Ring, Studded, Scale, Brigandine | +2       |
| Chain                            | +3       |
| Banded or Splint                 | +4       |
| Plate                            | +5       |
| Field Plate                      | +6       |
| Full Plate                       | +7       |

To determine the weight of a piecemeal barding set, multiply the weight of a full suit of the appropriate barding type by the percentages given below. The same percentages may be used to determine the cost of individual sections when purchased separately.

**Cart:** A simple two-wheeled vehicle used for hauling supplies, trade goods, and agricultural produce.

**Wagon:** A four-wheeled freight vehicle intended for transporting heavy cargo over long distances.

**Carriage:** An enclosed passenger vehicle designed for comfortable travel.

**Coach, Ornamented:** A luxurious carriage fitted with fine furnishings, decorations, and other displays of wealth or status.

**Chariot:** A light two-wheeled vehicle used for racing, hunting, ceremony, or warfare. A riding chariot accommodates two occupants: one driver and one passenger who may make attacks. A war chariot is wider and more heavily braced, accommodating three occupants; the third is commonly a shield-bearer who provides cover to the crew. 

The war chariot’s design provides a more stable platform for making missile attacks. For the purposes of _Archery from Mounts and Vehicles_, a war chariot is treated as moving one category slower than its actual movement. Thus, at a run it is treated as moving at full movement, at full movement it is treated as half movement, and at half movement it is treated as stationary.

**Sedan Chair:** An enclosed chair carried by servants or hirelings. Sedan chairs are commonly used in large cities where streets are crowded or unsuitable for wheeled traffic.

**Leather armour (AC 8):** Hardened and shaped leather armour worn over ordinary clothing. It provides modest protection while remaining light, durable, and relatively comfortable for extended wear.

Leather armour is common among militias, scouts, hunters, marines, and other lightly equipped warriors. It is quieter than most metal armour and requires little maintenance, making it a popular choice for travellers and adventurers.

**Padded armour (AC 8):** Layers of quilted cloth, batting, fur, or similar materials stitched together to provide basic protection. Padded armour is inexpensive and can be produced almost anywhere with simple tools and materials.

Although bulky and less durable than leather armour, padded armour remains common among militias, bandits, tribal warriors, and others unable to obtain better equipment. Several layers of heavy clothing or furs may be treated as padded armour at the DM's discretion.

**Ring Mail (AC 7)**: Ring mail consists of large metal rings sewn or riveted to a backing of leather or heavy cloth. It represents an early or low-sophistication approach to metal protection, occupying a similar role in earlier arms traditions that chain mail later fulfills.

It is heavy for the protection it offers and is generally less effective than more advanced mail or plate constructions of comparable weight. In most settings, it is also more expensive than chain mail while providing inferior protection.

Ring mail is most commonly found among lightly equipped militias, poorly supplied garrisons, or irregular forces where materials and craftsmanship are limited. It suffers from the same maintenance issues as other backed armours, as the textile foundation is prone to wear, soiling, and degradation over time if not carefully maintained.

**Studded Leather armour (AC 7):** Soft leather or heavy cloth reinforced with closely spaced metal studs or rivets. The flexible construction provides greater protection than ordinary leather while preserving freedom of movement.

Studded leather is relatively inexpensive and easier to manufacture than most metal armours. It is commonly worn by guards, sailors, pirates, and other warriors who require good protection without sacrificing mobility.

**Spiked Leather armour (AC 7):** Studded leather armour that is custom made and fitted with projecting spikes. Favoured by some barbarians, gladiators, and pit fighters.

When overbearing or grappling, the wearer inflicts additional piercing damage on a successful attack. A Small wearer inflicts 1d2 points of damage, a Medium wearer 1d3 points, and a Large wearer 1d4 points. This damage is inflicted for each successful overbear or grapple attack, not for each round a target remains grappled.

**Gnomish Workman's Leather armour (AC 7):** A specialised form of high-quality gnomish leather armour, workman's leather is covered with pouches, loops, tool racks, hidden compartments, and ingenious fastening systems. Laden with tools, spare parts, and half-finished inventions, it provides protection equivalent to studded leather armour. A suit can carry up to 10 pounds of equipment, of which up to half may be concealed in hidden compartments.

In theory, the underlying armour is as quiet and flexible as other high-quality gnomish leather. In practice, the assortment of gadgets, tools, and curiosities attached to it usually produces a constant rattle of metal, leather, and mechanisms. Most suits are highly individualized and frequently modified, with owners adding new compartments, locks, traps, and storage solutions whenever inspiration strikes.

Among gnomes, workman's leather serves as both practical equipment and a display of craftsmanship. Elaborate designs, ingenious concealments, and clever modifications are often sources of professional pride and friendly competition. Outside gnomish communities, however, the armour is uncommon, valued primarily by those who appreciate its unusual storage capacity and concealed compartments.

**Hide armour (AC 6):** Hide armour is made from the thick hide of large animals or from multiple layers of ordinary leather bonded into a heavy protective shell. Cruder than leather armour, it relies on thickness rather than craftsmanship for protection.

Hide armour is most commonly worn by barbarians, humanoids, and other primitive cultures. It is heavier and less comfortable than leather armour, but offers superior protection and can be made with minimal skill and simple tools from a suitable hide.

Among tribal peoples, hide armour is often marked with clan symbols and decorated with horns, teeth, claws, or other trophies taken from notable kills. Such additions serve as signs of status, ferocity, and personal achievement. Some human barbarian cultures also favour hide armour, and legends persist of enchanted hides imbued with the strength or spirit of great beasts.

**Scale Mail (AC 6):** A coat of leather or cloth covered with overlapping metal scales sewn or riveted to a backing, resembling fish scales. It provides protection comparable to other medium armours but is heavier and less efficient in construction.

Scale mail is an older form of armour found in cultures that favour small metal plates over large forged pieces, often where metalworking traditions are less focused on heavy plate construction or where older military styles persist. It is typically worn by soldiers in regions where its materials are readily available or where traditional armour forms remain in use.

The armour requires regular maintenance, as scales can loosen or detach over time; poorly maintained suits lose effectiveness. Like other layered armours with fabric or leather backing, it is also prone to discomfort and wear during prolonged use.

**Coin armour (AC 6)**: A rare variant of scale mail fashioned from overlapping coins of precious or common metals affixed to a backing of leather or cloth. Though structurally similar to other scale armours, it is worn more for display and status than practicality, and is most often seen among dignitaries, high-ranking commanders, or the excessively wealthy.

Each suit can incorporate a substantial number of coins (often up to 1,000), arranged either uniformly or in heraldic patterns representing family, clan, or allegiance. While occasionally targeted by thieves, such armour is typically worn only by those with significant personal security.

**Sea Elf Scale Mail (AC 6)**: A refined variant of scale mail crafted in the submerged kingdoms of the sea elves. It is designed for both ceremonial display and warfare, with construction techniques adapted to prolonged use underwater.

The scales are affixed to a backing of treated eel-skin that resists degradation in salt water, and the metals used are selected for their resistance to corrosion. The result is an ornate, silvery armour whose exact composition is the subject of much speculation among surface smiths.

Rare and costly, it is worn primarily by sea elf nobles and elite warriors, and is typically brought forth only in times of war or formal ceremony. In value and craftsmanship it is often considered comparable to elven chain mail.

**Brigandine armour (AC 6)**: Brigandine is a composite armour of small metal plates riveted between or onto layers of leather or heavy cloth, often finished with an outer fabric covering. It represents a practical middle ground between rigid mail and flexible leather, offering solid protection without the full weight or complexity of heavier armours.

It is typically quieter than chain or other full metal armours, though not as silent as plain leather. Its layered construction makes it moderately flexible, though somewhat stiffer than scale or hide armour.

Brigandine is often the highest-quality armour producible by modest workshops, making it common among militia officers, mercenaries, and lightly equipped professionals. It is also favored by irregular forces who improvise materials—canvas, scrap metal, or other available components—while still achieving reliable protection against cutting and slashing attacks.

Its layered structure allows for limited concealment of small items between layers, a feature sometimes exploited by scouts, smugglers, or other covert operators, whether as practical utility or rumor-driven myth.

**Chain Mail (AC 5):** Interlocking metal rings worn over a layer of padded cloth or soft leather to reduce chafing and absorb impact. Chain mail is the standard medium armour in most campaign settings, valued for its strong protection relative to its cost and availability.

Although only slightly heavier than hide or brigandine armour, chain mail places most of its weight on the shoulders, making it tiring to wear for extended periods. Many experienced warriors keep a lighter suit of armour for travel, guard duty, or other periods when full protection is unnecessary.

Chain mail is commonly worn by professional soldiers, guards, mercenaries, and armed retainers. It is frequently used as the foundation for heavier armours and is often issued in military and noble service.

**Elven Chain Mail (AC 5)**: Elven chain mail is forged from a rare silvery alloy of exceptional strength and lightness. While all high-quality elven armour is less weighty than ordinary armour, Elven artisans have managed to reduce the weight of chain armour even further while retaining comparable protection.

Elven armourers guard the secret of its creation closely; only a handful in each generation are ever taught the craft, chosen through ritual and magical testing rather than wealth or status. The process is widely believed to depend on subtle magical perception and techniques that cannot be easily replicated outside elven tradition.

For this reason, elven chain mail is never common, even among elves. It is issued to select troops, both infantry and mounted, and is most often seen in the hands of elite warriors or those on special duty. When damaged, it is always returned to its original maker for repair rather than being replaced.

Human and dwarven smiths can imitate its appearance and partial properties, but never fully reproduce its lightness and resilience. As a result, true elven chain is regarded as both a battlefield asset and a cultural treasure, more often preserved than discarded, and rarely seen outside elven lands.

**Silenced Elven Chain (AC 5)**: A rare modification of elven chain in which each link is individually wrapped in thin leather or cloth binding to reduce metal-on-metal noise. This “silencing” comes at a cost: the armour is roughly one-third heavier than standard elven chain and is even more expensive, typically exceeding the price of plate mail, while being rarer still.

It is primarily crafted for thieves or covert operatives who expect both infiltration and sudden combat. The reduction in noise improves stealth under armour, but the added bulk and stiffness interfere with fine motor tasks and climbing.

  ***Effect on Thief Skills (wearing silenced elven chain):*** Pick Pockets -25%; Open Locks -5%; Find/Remove Traps -5%; Hide in Shadows -10%; Climb Walls -25%. Move Silently, Detect Noise, and Read Languages are unaffected.

Such armour is itself a significant undertaking to commission, requiring an exceptional armourer willing to adapt elven techniques—often the focus of entire quests or patron-driven missions in its own right.

**Drow Chain (AC 4):** Drow chain is a finely crafted mesh of satiny black metal, fashioned into light tunics that offer excellent protection without restricting movement. Similar in concept to elven chain, it is forged from secret alloys that commonly include adamantine and other materials known only to drow smiths.

Drow chain is prized for its remarkable strength, lightness, and subtle enchantment. The alloys used in drow-forged armour impart the equivalent of a magical bonus (typically +1, though exceptional examples may reach +5) while the armour remains within the influence of the Underdark that empowers it; however, it does not radiate magic. If exposed to direct sunlight, the armour begins an irreversible decline. Its enchantments fail after 1d12+8 days, and after a further 1d12 days the metal crumbles to useless fragments. As a result, drow armour is exceedingly rare and short-lived on the surface world.

Among the drow, chain armour is widely used by warriors, scouts, and nobles alike, reflecting their preference for protection that does not sacrifice agility. Surface scholars often speculate that drow chain and elven chain share a common ancestral origin, though the techniques used to forge either remain among the most closely guarded secrets of their respective peoples.

**Banded Mail (AC 4)**: Banded mail consists of overlapping horizontal strips of laminated metal secured over a foundation of chain mail and leather. This construction reinforces the torso while retaining some of the flexibility of mail armour.

Compared to chain mail, the rigid bands distribute weight more evenly across the body, making the armour somewhat more comfortable to wear for extended periods. The trade-off is reduced durability at the joints, where repeated movement places greater strain on the armour's construction.

Banded mail is commonly worn by professional soldiers and experienced warriors seeking greater protection than chain mail without the expense or weight of full plate. Like other metal armours, it requires regular maintenance to prevent rust and preserve its appearance, though superficial corrosion has less effect on its protective qualities than it does on mail armour.

**Splint Mail (AC 4)**: Splint mail is a variant of banded mail in which long metal splints are attached vertically to a backing of chain, leather, or heavy cloth. While it offers protection comparable to banded mail, the arrangement is more restrictive of movement and generally less comfortable to wear.

Splint mail is often associated with older armouring traditions, regions where more advanced armour styles are uncommon, or warriors unable to obtain superior equipment. Its lower cost makes it an attractive option for adventurers seeking greater protection than chain mail without the expense of more advanced armours.

The armour's principal drawback is durability. Damaged splints can bend or break inward, posing a hazard to the wearer, and the armour requires the same regular maintenance as banded mail to remain serviceable.

**Bronze Plate (AC 4):** Bronze plate consists of large bronze plates mounted over brigandine, scale, or layered leather foundations. Lighter and less expensive than true plate mail, it provides solid protection but lacks the flexibility and durability of steel plate armour.

Bronze plate is most common in cultures where copper and tin are abundant, iron is scarce, or bronze-working traditions remain highly developed. In such societies it may serve as the standard heavy armour of professional warriors, nobles, and champions.

Because plate armour is often associated with wealth and status, bronze plate is sometimes chosen by those unable to afford a true steel harness. Unscrupulous merchants are known to exploit this prestige, passing polished or silvered bronze armour off as true plate to the inexperienced.

Well-crafted bronze plate can be highly decorative, taking embossed crests, tribal insignia, and intricate ornamentation with ease. In some cultures, masterwork bronze armour is prized as much for its beauty and symbolism as for its protection.

**Plate Mail (AC 3):** Plate mail consists of heavy steel plates mounted over a foundation of chain mail and leather, protecting the chest, abdomen, shoulders, and other vital areas. More advanced in design than bronze plate, it benefits from superior metallurgy, careful fitting, and refined construction techniques that provide excellent protection without excessive weight.

Plate mail is the standard heavy armour of professional warriors. Knights, household troops, veteran mercenaries, and elite guards commonly favour it because it offers nearly the best protection available while remaining practical for campaigning and everyday military service. Though more expensive than chain, banded, or brigandine armour, its durability, comfort, and effectiveness make it a worthwhile investment for those who can afford it.

Unlike heavier forms of plate armour, plate mail can be fitted to a new owner with relative ease and is practical to don, maintain, and travel in. Its design has been refined over generations by skilled armourers, resulting in a form of protection that balances mobility, endurance, and defence better than almost any other armour. For many adventurers, plate mail represents the pinnacle of practical battlefield equipment.

**Field Plate armour (AC 2):** Field plate consists of shaped and interlocking steel plates that enclose nearly the entire body, worn over a thick padded garment and typically accompanied by gauntlets, armoured boots, and a visored helm. Each suit is individually crafted and fitted by a master armourer, providing exceptional protection while remaining suitable for active military service.

Field plate is the armour of great nobles, renowned champions, and the wealthiest professional warriors. Though only slightly more protective than plate mail, the additional coverage comes at the cost of reduced mobility, poorer ventilation, and the considerable time required to don and remove the armour. As a result, field plate is most often worn for battle, sieges, and military campaigns rather than routine travel.

Because every suit is custom-made, captured field plate is difficult and expensive to refit for a new owner. Nevertheless, its durability and battlefield performance make it highly prized. In some campaigns, the right to wear field plate may itself be a mark of rank, nobility, or knightly status.

**Dwarven Plate Mail (AC 2):** Forged from dense black iron and built with durability foremost in mind, dwarven plate is heavier, bulkier, and has less flexible joints than comparable human armour. Though plain by the standards of other races, it is renowned for its ability to withstand punishment that would ruin lesser suits. Dwarven warriors clad in such armour are often mocked by their enemies as "waddling cauldrons," though rarely to their faces .

Dwarven plate is effectively _plate mail +1_, reflecting the superior craftsmanship and substantial protection it provides. It is 50% heavier than normal plate mail, but also gains the benefits of dwarven made high quality armour (see next sub-section).

Dwarven smiths rarely forge such armour for non-dwarves. The craft is closely guarded, and the armour's construction reflects dwarven physiques and fighting styles rather than those of taller folk. As a result, dwarven plate is most often encountered as the prized possession of clan champions, veteran warriors, and noble leaders. Unlike human armour traditions, dwarven armour is seldom decorated, its makers preferring strength, reliability, and function over ornamentation.

**Full Plate armour (AC 1):** Full plate represents the pinnacle of the armourer's craft. Constructed from precisely fitted and articulated steel plates that enclose the entire body, each suit is custom-made for its wearer and often embellished with engraving, heraldry, and other marks of status. No mundane armour offers better protection.

Full plate is exceedingly rare and is usually associated with rulers, great nobles, renowned champions, and the most prestigious knightly orders. In many campaigns, ownership of a suit is as much a symbol of rank and privilege as it is a military asset. Such armour is often bestowed as a reward for extraordinary service, inherited as a treasured heirloom, or sought as the prize of a great quest.

Compared to field plate, full plate offers superior protection through masterful craftsmanship and careful tailoring rather than additional weight. Properly fitted, it allows remarkable freedom of movement for such a heavily armoured suit. However, it remains cumbersome to don without assistance and is rarely worn except when battle or ceremony demands it.
#### High Quality Armours
Master armourers are able to produce special high-quality armour. The benefits of such armour depend on the race of the craftsman overseeing the work, reflecting the fact each culture has its own perspective of what constitutes high quality, as well as their own armouring traditions and techniques. High quality armours cost at least 10 times what normal armour of the same type does, and normally 15 times as much if the person being fitted is of a different race than the craftsman. It also takes 4 times longer to craft.

**Dwarven made**: Dwarves value durability and their armour exemplifies this. Metal armours crafted by dwarves gain a +6 to item saving throws (cumulative with any magic bonuses). They also have twice the damage points of ordinary armour if rules for damage to armour are used.

**Elven made:** Elves prefer the ability to move fluidly and consequently Elven armourers have mastered techniques of reducing the encumbrance of armour. High quality Elvish armour is half the weight of normal armour (note elven and drow chain are even lighter, and use the weights given in the armour table above).

**Gnomish made:** While high-quality gnomish versions of metal armours are unknown, their artisans have unparalleled technical skill in assembling the materials in a manner that preserves the wearer's dexterity and stealth. No penalties to thieves skills are incurred while wearing such armour.

**Halfling made**: The only high-quality armour halflings craft is standard leather armour. Their process produces leather that is exceptionally supple without losing any of its protectiveness. For the purpose of thieves' skills and any other armour-related penalty that does not relate to magic use, the wearer is treated as though they are wearing no armour.

**Human made:** All high-quality human armour is rugged, though not at the level of dwarven craftsmanship. Armours with a base AC of 4 or worse, gain a +2 to item saving throws, while those better gain a +4. They have one and a half times the damage points of ordinary armour if rules for damage to armour are used. Further armours with a base AC better than 4 also give the wearer a +2 to saving throws vs. Breath Weapons and Rods, Staffs, or Wands.

### Helms
The various helms and helmets offer differing degrees of protection to the wearer's head. Attacks that explicitly or implicitly target the head or face (called shots, saps, falling objects, etc) are made against the AC of the helm worn (if any), but otherwise the helm has no effect on AC. The wearing of helms also affects hearing and vision as given in the table below.

| Helm               | AC  | Cost  | Weight | Notes *                                          |
| ------------------ | --- | ----- | ------ | ------------------------------------------------ |
| Cap                | 7   | 4 gp  | 3 lb.  | -1 (-5%) to hearing checks                       |
| Coif               | 5   | 10 gp | 5 lb.  | -1 (-5%) to hearing checks                       |
| Open-Face Helmet   | 4   | 12 gp | 7 lb   | -2 (-10%) to hearing checks, -1 to vision checks |
| Closed-Face Helmet | 3   | 20 gp | 10 lb  | -3 (-15%) to hearing checks, -2 to vision checks |
| Great Helm         | 1   | 80 gp | 20 lb  | -4 (-20%) to hearing checks, -3 to vision checks |
* values in parentheses apply to a thief's Hear Noise ability. 

**Cap (AC 7)** : A cap is a padded, leather, or steel skullcap worn much like a close-fitted cap, beret, or bonnet. Caps are usually worn with padded armour, leather, studded leather, hide, or any other lightweight protection.

**Coif (AC 5)** : A coif is a padded chain mail hood. It fits snugly around the neck and covers the head, leaving only the face exposed. A coif is often a separately worn item though it may be directly integrated into chain mail armour like a hood. Often, a heavily armoured knight will wear a coif under a great helm. This allows him to remove his great helm for a better view of the field while maintaining a fair level of head protection. The penalty to hearing checks is not cumulative when worn under another helmet.

**Open-Face Helmet (AC 4):** This class of helmet, made of reinforced leather or metal, covers most of the head, save the face and neck, though they commonly extend down to provide protection for the nose. Open-face helmets are popular with officers and leaders, and are appropriately worn with medium-weight armours like brigandine and chain mail.

**Closed-Face Helmet (AC 3):** The closed-face helmets are identical to the open-face helmet except for the addition of a visored faceplate. This type of helmet is commonly worn by officers and sergeants-at-arms and are most commonly worn with plate mail. When the visor is up, treat as an open-face helmet.

**Great Helm (AC 1):** A great helm is any massive metal helm that covers the entire head, including the neck and often the upper shoulders. The only gaps in the protection are found in the narrow slits that allow limited vision, and some small holes to permit breathing. The visor is not normally removable. It is rare to find a great helm used with armor lighter than banded mail. Great helms are most often found in combination with plate mail or heavier armor.
### Shields
Shields add protection against attacks from the front or flanks, or alternatively shields larger than a targe may be slung providing protection against rear attacks only. Shields cannot protect both front and rear simultaneously. Shields may also be used to make certain types of attacks (see Combat Chapter). 

| Shield        | Cost  | Weight |
| ------------- | ----- | ------ |
| Buckler/Targe | 1 gp  | 3 lb   |
| Small Shield  | 3 gp  | 5 lb   |
| Medium Shield | 7 gp  | 10 lb  |
| Tower Shield  | 10 gp | 15 lb  |

**Buckler (or Targe):** A buckler is a very small shield, usually round and strapped to the forearm rather than gripped in the hand. Its compact size allows it to be worn while using missile weapons, making it popular among archers, crossbowmen, scouts, and other lightly equipped warriors.

A buckler does not provide a constant armour Class bonus. Instead, once per round the wearer may declare its use against a single incoming attack before the attack roll is made. Against *that attack only*, the buckler improves the wearer's armour Class by 1 (or more if the buckler is magical).

**Small Shield:** A small shield is typically a round shield about 2 feet in diameter, carried on the forearm and gripped with the shield hand. Its relatively light weight allows the wearer to carry small items in that hand (a torch, lantern, map, etc), though not to wield another weapon.

A small shield may be used to protect against up to two frontal attacks per round. The wearer must declare which attacks are being blocked before the attack rolls are made. Against those attacks, the shield grants its normal armour Class bonus of +1.

**Medium Shield:** A medium shield is carried on the forearm and gripped in the shield hand. Larger and heavier than a small shield, it occupies the hand completely and prevents the wearer from carrying or using other items with that arm. Medium shields are commonly constructed of wood reinforced with metal or entirely of metal, and appear in a wide variety of shapes and styles.

A medium shield provides protection against any number of frontal and flanking attacks. For game purposes, the shield's +1 armour Class bonus is applied directly to the wearer's armour Class, however this protection does not apply against attacks from the rear.

**Tower Shield:** Also known as a kite shield or body shield, a tower shield is a massive shield designed to protect much of the wearer's body. Typically reaching from shoulder or chin to shin, it is strapped firmly to the forearm and requires the full use of the shield arm to carry.

A tower shield grants a +1 armour Class bonus against melee attacks and a +2 armour Class bonus against missile attacks. As with other shields, these bonuses apply only against frontal and flanking attacks.
### Donning and Doffing Armour
There are occasions when a character must know how quickly armour can be put on or removed. Night attacks, ambushes, accidents, and similar emergencies rarely allow the luxury of full preparation.

Armour that consists primarily of a single garment or piece (such as leather armour, padded armour, hide armour, or chain mail) requires **1 round** to don with assistance, or **2 rounds** without assistance. Metal armours of similarly simple construction require **2 rounds** with assistance, or **4 rounds** without assistance.

More complex armours composed of multiple fitted pieces (such as plate mail, field plate, and full plate) require **1d6+4 rounds** to don with assistance, or **three times as long** without assistance. These times assume any necessary padding or undergarments are available and are donned as part of the process.

Characters may choose to don armour hastily. Simple armours can be donned in **half the normal time** (minimum 1 round), but until properly adjusted they provide protection **1 AC worse than normal** (to a maximum of AC 8) and impose a **–1 penalty on attack rolls**.

Complex armours may be donned piecemeal. Each round spent dressing improves the wearer's Armour Class by 1, beginning from **AC 10**, until the armour's normal AC is reached. For example, a warrior who spends 3 rounds hastily donning plate mail would enter battle with **AC 7**.

Removing armour is considerably faster. Simple armours can be removed in **1 round**. Complex armours require **1d4+1 rounds** to remove. If the wearer is willing to cut straps, bend fittings, or otherwise damage the armour, this time is halved (round fractions up).

### Creatures with Natural Armour
Some creatures possess a natural Armour Class equal to or better than that provided by manufactured armour. Such creatures may still benefit from armour, barding, or similar protection.

Rather than replacing the creature's existing Armour Class, armour improves its natural Armour Class by an amount determined by the type of armour worn, up to a maximum Armour Class based on that armour's protective value.

| Armour or Barding Type           | AC Bonus | Maximum AC |
| -------------------------------- | -------- | ---------- |
| Leather or Padded                | +1       | 4          |
| Ring, Studded, Scale, Brigandine | +2       | 3          |
| Chain                            | +3       | 2          |
| Banded or Splint                 | +4       | 1          |
| Plate                            | +5       | 0          |
| Field Plate                      | +6       | -1         |
| Full Plate                       | +7       | -2         |
For example, a horse with a natural AC of 7 wearing leather barding improves to AC 6. If fitted with chain barding its AC improves to 4. A creature with a natural AC of 1 would gain no benefit from chain barding, as chain cannot improve Armour Class beyond AC 2.
### Damage to Armour (Optional Rule)
For campaigns that wish to track armour condition, each suit of armour, shield, and helm has a number of **Damage Points (DP)** representing its structural integrity.

Ordinary armour has DP equal to **50 - (AC × 5)**, helms have DP equal to **40 - (Helm AC × 5)**, and shields have DP equal to **5 + (Weight × 2)**.

For magical armour, use the AC of the underlying armour type when calculating DP; enchantment bonuses do not increase an item's Damage Points. Thus a suit of _plate mail +3_ has the same DP as ordinary plate mail. However, armour suitable for enchantment is often high-quality armour and may therefore benefit from any additional DP granted by superior craftsmanship.
#### Taking Damage
Armour normally suffers damage only when its wearer is struck by a **critical hit**. A critical hit automatically inflicts **1 DP** of damage, regardless of the amount of damage caused by the attack.

Damage is applied as follows:

- If the attack was specifically directed at the head (such as a called shot or sap attack), the **helm** loses 1 DP instead of the armour.
- If the attack was blocked, parried, or otherwise intercepted with a shield, the **shield** loses 1 DP instead of the armour.
- Particularly devastating attacks, significant accidents, or deliberate attempts to damage equipment may inflict additional DP loss at the DM's discretion.

When an item is reduced to **0 DP**, it is ruined and no longer provides protection. Any item with at least **1 DP** remaining can be repaired.
#### Repairing Damage
Repairing armour typically requires access to a suitable craftsman, forge, workshop, or similar facilities. Each point of damage repaired generally costs 5% of the armour's original value, though costs may vary depending on circumstances.

**Adze:** An adze is a woodworking tool with a blade mounted perpendicular to the haft, resembling an axe turned sideways. Though primarily a tool, short-handled adzes are sometimes adapted for combat and used as chopping weapons. Adzes are commonly found wherever wood is worked.

**Ankus:** An ankus is a staff about three to four feet long fitted with a metal hook and spike at one end. Originally designed for handling large animals like elephants, it can also serve as a weapon. Some examples are made as ceremonial or decorative pieces.

**Atl-Atl:** A short throwing lever with a shallow groove or hook used to propel spears with increased force and efficiency. It extends the effective range of thrown spears and allows a higher rate of successive throws compared to hand-thrown weapons.

**Awl Pike:** Often called simply a pike, the awl pike is a very long infantry spear, typically 16 to 22 feet in length. Its head is usually leaf-shaped or lozenge-shaped and mounted on a sturdy wooden shaft. Many examples reinforce the head with steel strips running down the shaft to prevent it from being cut away. The butt is commonly capped with metal and the grip wrapped for better handling.

**Bagh Nakh:** Also called tiger claws, the bagh nakh consists of several curved blades mounted on a bar worn across the palm. It is used with slashing, claw-like strikes and is often worn in pairs.

**Bardiche:** A bardiche is a poleaxe with a long, curved axe blade mounted on a shaft five to eight feet long. The upper portion of the blade can be used for thrusting, while the lower section is secured to the shaft by a reinforcing metal strip called a langet.

**Bastard Sword:** Also known as a hand-and-a-half sword, the bastard sword bridges the gap between the longsword and the two-handed sword. It features a double-edged blade and an extended grip that can be used with either one or two hands. Most examples measure between four and five feet in overall length. A bastard sword may be used either one-handed or two-handed.

**Battle Axe:** The battle axe is a heavy fighting axe designed for use in one hand. Most have a haft about four feet long and a broad cutting blade, though many include spikes, hammers, or secondary blades opposite the main edge. The weapon's long handle provides leverage and striking power while still allowing the use of a shield. 

A thrust with an axe spike, if present, inflicts 1d3 points of damage.

**Bec de Corbin:** The bec de corbin is a pole weapon fitted with a beak-like spike designed to punch through or tear armor. Most examples also feature a hammer or axe face opposite the beak. The shaft is typically about eight feet long.

**Belaying Pin:** A belaying pin is a short wooden or metal rod used to secure rigging lines aboard a ship. Though not intended as a weapon, it can serve as an improvised club when needed. If thrown, a belaying pin uses the same statistics as a club.

**Bill-Guisarme:** Often shortened to bill, the bill-guisarme developed from the agricultural bill hook. Its head combines a thrusting spike, a hooked blade, and a cutting edge mounted on a pole approximately eight feet long.

**Blowgun:** A blowgun is a hollow tube used to fire light projectiles by means of a strong breath. Most blowguns are six to seven feet long, although smaller versions exist. Blowguns may fire blowgun darts or blowgun needles.

Blowgun ammunition is too light to penetrate heavy armor effectively. Attacks against targets wearing any form of brigandine, mail, or plate armor suffer an additional –4 penalty to hit.

**Bo Stick:** A bo is a hardwood staff, usually six to seven feet long and slightly tapered toward the ends. Though simple in appearance, it is a durable and effective weapon. A wielder suffers a –2 penalty to attack rolls against opponents wearing any form of plate armor.

**Boku-To:** A boku-to is a wooden practice sword shaped like a short sword. Although incapable of cutting, it can still be used as a bludgeoning weapon.

**Bolas:** Bolas consist of two or more weighted ends connected by cords or thongs. When thrown, the weights wrap around a target, tangling limbs and hindering movement. 

A character must be proficient with bolas to use them effectively. If the attack hits, the target automatically faces a knockdown check. A victim who fails must spend a full round and succeed at a Strength check before standing and moving normally.

A called shot to the arms prevents the target from using weapons or shields until freed. A called shot to the head wraps the cords around the neck, inflicting 1d3 points of strangulation damage each round until removed. A sharp blade can be used to cut the cords free automatically, provided the victim has a free arm.

**Boomerang:** A boomerang is a shaped throwing club designed for stable flight. Most do not automatically return to the thrower. A returning throw requires a successful called shot and only returns if the attack misses.

**Bottle:** A glass bottle is a common improvised weapon. Whenever a bottle strikes a target, it must succeed at a saving throw vs. normal blow or shatter. A broken bottle may thereafter be used as a knife.

**Brandistock:** A brandistock is an iron-shod walking staff that conceals three folding blades. When extended, the blades form a small trident. If drawn in circumstances where opponents do not expect it, the DM may grant a +1 bonus to surprise or initiative.

**Broad Sword:** A broad sword is a heavy military sword with a double-edged blade designed primarily for cutting. Most are equipped with a basket hilt or shell guard that protects the hand.

A basket-hilted broad sword grants a +1 bonus on Parry maneuvers. Punching attacks made with the basket hilt are treated as attacks with a metal gauntlet.

**Caltrop:** A caltrop is a small spiked object designed so that one point always faces upward when placed on the ground.

At least 10 caltrops must cover a 25-square-foot area to be fully effective. Any character entering the area must save vs. paralyzation. Failure indicates the character has stepped on a caltrop, suffers 1d4 damage, and moves at half normal speed until it is removed. A second failed save indicates a more serious injury that reduces movement to one-third normal for 24 hours or until magically healed. Removing a caltrop requires one round.

Five caltrops in the same area grant a +4 bonus on the saving throw. Every additional five caltrops beyond the first ten impose a –2 penalty, to a maximum of –6. Characters moving at less than one-third speed can avoid caltrops without a saving throw if they can see the ground.

**Cestus:** A cestus is a reinforced fighting gauntlet fitted with spikes, blades, metal plates, or weighted inserts. It is worn over the fist and used to enhance punching attacks.

Damage inflicted by the cestus replaces normal punching damage and is treated as normal weapon damage rather than temporary damage. There is no separate proficiency required for the cestus, although warriors may specialize in its use. A cestus suffers a –2 penalty to attack rolls against opponents wearing any form of plate armor.

**Chain:** A fighting chain consists of a weighted length of chain used for striking, entangling, and controlling opponents. When used to perform a Pull/Trip maneuver, the wielder gains a +4 bonus to the Strength check. This bonus also applies against mounted opponents.

**Chakram:** A chakram is a circular throwing weapon with a sharpened outer edge. It is thrown with a rapid spinning motion.

The chakram is less effective against heavily armored targets and suffers a –2 penalty to attacks against opponents wearing any form of mail, scale, or plate armor.

**Chijikiri:** A chijikiri combines a spear with a length of chain attached to the butt of the shaft. It can be used as a normal spear or reversed and swung with the chain end. When using the chain end, the weapon inflicts damage as a chain and is treated as a Bludgeoning weapon. The wielder gains a +4 bonus to effective Strength for pull/trip maneuvers, but the weapon cannot be used to pull or trip mounted opponents.

**Cho-Ku-No:** The cho-ku-no is a repeating crossbow fitted with a magazine that holds up to ten bolts. A lever mechanism allows bolts to be loaded and fired rapidly without individually drawing and nocking each shot. It uses light quarrels.

**Claymore:** A claymore is a large two-handed sword with a broad, double-edged blade, long crossguard, and extended grip. It is slightly shorter than a typical two-handed sword while retaining much of the same reach and striking power.

**Club:** A club is a sturdy stick, branch, or shaped length of wood used as a bludgeoning weapon. Among the oldest weapons known, clubs range from simple improvised cudgels to carefully crafted fighting weapons. Clubs are common and easily obtained. Any suitably balanced length of wood may serve as a club, though purpose-made weapons are often reinforced, weighted, or decorated.

**Composite Short Bow:** A composite short bow is constructed from multiple materials laminated together, commonly horn, wood, or bone. Its compact design makes it suitable for use while mounted. Composite short bows fire flight arrows or stone arrows.

Characters with Strength bonuses must use bows built for their Strength to gain the full benefit of those bonuses. Custom bows cost three to five times the normal price.

**Composite Long Bow:** A composite long bow is a large laminated bow constructed from multiple materials. It combines the range of a long bow with the resilience of composite construction. Composite long bows fire any type of arrow.

Specially constructed composite long bows may be used from horseback at twice the normal cost.

Characters with Strength bonuses must use bows built for their Strength to gain the full benefit of those bonuses. Custom bows cost three to five times the normal price.

**Cutlass:** A cutlass is a short, heavy sword with a broad, single-edged curved blade and a protective basket hilt. Its compact design makes it well suited to fighting in confined spaces.

A basket-hilted cutlass grants the same benefits to Parry maneuvers as a broad sword.

**Dagger.** A dagger is a short thrusting weapon with a pointed blade, usually double-edged and slightly longer than a knife. It serves equally well as a sidearm, utility tool, or concealed weapon.

Daggers are made from a wide variety of materials. Primitive versions may be fashioned from bone or stone, while more advanced examples are typically metal. Decorative daggers often feature carved hilts, engraved blades, or other ornamentation.

**Daikyu:** A daikyu is a large asymmetrical bow approximately seven feet long. The hand grip is positioned below the center of the stave, allowing the bow to be used from horseback or while kneeling.  

**Dart.** A dart is a small thrown missile consisting of a short shaft fitted with a pointed head and stabilizing flights. Easy to conceal and quick to throw, darts are used for hunting, sport, and warfare.

Darts may be coated with poison or other substances. Their high rate of fire allows a thrower to make several attacks in rapid succession.

**Dirk.** A dirk is a large knife that combines the qualities of a dagger and a utility blade. It features a single-edged blade with a sharpened back edge near the point and a distinctive hilt with a flared grip and rounded guard. Many dirks are carried in specialized sheaths that include small compartments for eating utensils or utility tools.

**Elbow and Knee Spikes:** Retractable or fixed spikes integrated into elbow and knee protection. Normally concealed as reinforced fittings, they deploy under pressure and lock into place. When grappled or engaged in wrestling, the wearer may substitute a spike attack for standard unarmed options, at –2 to hit. These items are effectively disarm-resistant due to their integration with worn protection.

**Estoc:** An estoc is a long thrusting sword with a rigid, edgeless blade designed for piercing armor. Many examples feature triangular or square blade cross-sections.

An estoc gains a +2 bonus to attack rolls against opponents wearing any form of mail armor and a +1 bonus against opponents wearing any form of plate armor.

**Falchion.** A falchion is a heavy, single-edged sword with a curved cutting edge and a broadening blade near the tip. Its forward-weighted design delivers powerful chopping blows.

**Fauchard.** A fauchard is a polearm derived from agricultural cutting tools. It consists of a long, curved blade mounted on a shaft approximately eight feet long, often fitted with a small rear hook or fluke. The weapon is used primarily for sweeping cuts rather than thrusts.

**Fauchard-Fork.** This variation of the fauchard adds a forward-facing hook or fork intended to improve thrusting attacks. Despite the modification, it remains a predominantly cutting weapon.

**Footman's Flail.** The footman's flail consists of a long shaft fitted with a hinged, spiked striking head. Designed for two-handed use, it delivers powerful crushing blows.

A footman's flail gains a +1 bonus to attack rolls against opponents wearing any form of plate armor.

**Footman's Mace.** The footman's mace is a two-handed mace with a long haft and a heavy striking head, often flanged or studded. Crude battlefield versions assembled from improvised materials suffer a –1 penalty to attack and damage rolls.

**Footman's Pick.** The footman's pick is a two-handed military pick with an extended haft that allows powerful, armor-piercing strikes.

**Fork:** A fork is a common agricultural tool consisting of a long handle fitted with two or more tines. Though intended for farm work, it can serve as an improvised weapon.

**Gaff.** A gaff is a hooked tool used for landing large fish. It consists of a metal hook attached to a short handle and is commonly found aboard fishing vessels and in waterfront communities.

Though intended as a tool, a gaff can serve as an improvised weapon. Some individuals permanently mount a gaff to a prosthetic arm (this configuration costs 2 gp), though this provides no special combat advantage.

**Gladius.** The gladius is a short sword with a double-edged blade and a reinforced thrusting point. It is designed for close combat and combines effective cutting and thrusting attacks in a compact weapon.

**Drusus.** A drusus is a finely crafted form of gladius noted for superior workmanship and careful maintenance requirements.

**Glaive.** A glaive is a polearm consisting of a large knife-like blade mounted on a shaft eight to ten feet long. Some examples include a rear hook or fluke. The weapon combines the cutting power of a sword with the reach of a polearm.

**Glaive-Guisarme.** A glaive-guisarme adds a rear hook or fluke to the glaive's blade. The additional fittings make the weapon somewhat heavier and slower than a standard glaive.

**Glove Nail:** A reinforced gauntlet fitted with a forward spike designed for close-quarters strikes, typically aimed at exposed areas. Often used as a paired set. On a successful piercing hit, the wielder may apply a follow-up pull effect as part of the same action (subject to normal combat resolution).

**Grain Flail.** The grain flail is an agricultural threshing tool consisting of a wooden striking bar attached to a longer handle by a flexible strap. Though not designed as a weapon, it can be used in combat and inspired the development of military flails.

**Grapple:** A grapple consists of several iron hooks attached to a rope. It is commonly used for climbing, securing lines, and similar tasks.

A grapple may be thrown a distance of 5 feet horizontally or 3 feet vertically per point of Strength. A grapple and rope may also be used for certain Pull/Trip maneuvers at the DM's discretion.

**Great Club.** A great club is an oversized two-handed club, often reinforced with iron bands, spikes, or metal studs. Its greater mass allows it to strike with considerably more force than a standard club.

**Guisarme.** A guisarme is a polearm with a long, curved blade mounted on a staff approximately six feet long. Many examples include a thrusting spike at the top of the shaft. The weapon is well suited to sweeping cuts and hooking attacks.

**Guisarme-Voulge.** This development of the guisarme combines a curved axe-like blade with a rear spike or hook. The blade narrows toward the tip, allowing both cutting and thrusting attacks. Some guisarme-voulges replace the rear spike with a hook designed for pulling riders from the saddle.

**Gunsen:** A gunsen is a reinforced folding fan that can be used as both a defensive implement and a bludgeoning weapon. A character proficient with a gunsen gains a +2 bonus on Block maneuvers made with the weapon.

**Halberd:** A halberd is a polearm combining an axe blade, a thrusting spike, and a rear hook or fluke mounted on a shaft about six feet long. The hook is commonly used to pull riders from the saddle, while the axe and spike allow both cutting and thrusting attacks.

**Hand Crossbow:** A hand crossbow is a compact crossbow small enough to conceal beneath a cloak or coat. It fires short bolts and is designed for one-handed use.

**Hand Axe:** Also called a throwing axe or hatchet, the hand axe consists of a compact axe head mounted on a short haft. Many examples include a rear fluke or top spike. A hand axe may be used in melee or thrown.

**Harness Knife:** A harness knife is a small utility blade carried as part of a wilderness harness. It is intended primarily for cutting rope, leather, and similar materials, but may also serve as a weapon.

**Harpoon:** A harpoon is a heavy throwing spear fitted with a barbed or detachable head attached to a line. It may be wielded one- or two-handed without affecting speed factor.

If a creature struck by a harpoon has an Intelligence of 2 or greater and a means of freeing itself, it may attempt a saving throw vs. poison. Success frees the creature. Failure leaves the harpoon embedded, inflicts 1 additional point of damage, and allows the wielder to draw the target up to 10 feet closer if its size permits.

**Hatchet:** A hatchet is a one-handed woodsman's axe with a broad blade mounted on a short haft. Though primarily a tool, it may also be used as a weapon.

**Head Spike:** A helmet-mounted spike used in charging attacks. The wearer drives forward in a lowered stance to impale an opponent. Attack is made at –3 and the attacker loses Dexterity-based AC bonuses for that attack. On a successful hit, damage is doubled.

**Heavy Crossbow:** A heavy crossbow is built around a sturdy tiller and heavy bow stave. It fires large bolts and often incorporates a stirrup or similar device to aid spanning. Heavy crossbows may be used underwater.

**Holy Symbol:** Large personal holy symbols may be used as improvised bludgeoning weapons. If a creature suffers damage from contact with a holy symbol, this damage is inflicted in addition to any bludgeoning damage caused by the attack. Creatures requiring magical weapons to be harmed are affected only by enchanted holy symbols.

**Hook Fauchard:** A hook fauchard is a fauchard fitted with a rear-facing hook intended for pulling riders from the saddle. The additional hook makes the weapon heavier and slower than a standard fauchard.

**Horseman's Flail:** A horseman's flail consists of a short haft connected by chain to one or more weighted striking heads.

Horseman's flails gain a +1 bonus to attack rolls against shield users and a +2 bonus on attempts to trap or offensively disarm an opponent's weapon.

**Horseman's Mace:** A horseman's mace is a one-handed mace with a shortened haft intended for mounted use. The head is typically flanged, ridged, or otherwise reinforced.

**Horseman's Pick:** A horseman's pick is a one-handed military pick fitted to a shortened haft for mounted combat. It features a narrow piercing beak mounted opposite a hammer face.

**Ice Pick:** An ice pick is a metal awl mounted on a wood or bone handle. Intended as a tool for working ice, it may also be used as a simple thrusting weapon.

**Iuak:** Also called a snow blade, the iuak is a broad bone tool resembling a blunt-ended machete. It is commonly used to cut and shape snow and may also serve as a weapon.

**Jambiya:** A jambiya is a curved dagger distinguished by its pronounced sweep and decorative hilt. Many examples are elaborately ornamented.

**Javelin:** A javelin is a light spear balanced for both throwing and melee combat. Its relatively small head allows it to be thrown effectively while remaining useful in hand-to-hand combat. A javelin may be used either one-handed or two-handed without affecting speed factor.

**Jousting Lance:** A jousting lance is a heavy lance designed for tournament use. It has a long wooden shaft fitted with a blunted or specially designed head intended to reduce penetration. **Kama:** A kama is a straight-bladed sickle mounted on a short handle. Because it resembles a common tool, a kama may be carried openly without immediately appearing to be a weapon.

**Katana:** A katana is a curved sword with a single-edged blade and a sharply pointed tip. The grip is long enough to accommodate either one- or two-handed use.

**Kau Sin Ke:** A kau sin ke consists of several iron bars linked together by short lengths of chain. A kau sin ke gains a +1 bonus to attack rolls against opponents using shields.

**Kawanaga:** A kawanaga consists of a grapple attached to a weighted rope. Either end may be used to strike an opponent. When used to perform a Pull/Trip maneuver, the wielder gains a +4 bonus to the Strength check.

**Khopesh:** A khopesh is a heavy sword with a straight hilt section that curves forward into a sickle-shaped blade. Most examples are made of bronze or iron.

**Knife:** A knife is a short, single-edged blade mounted to a handle. Knives vary widely in size and construction, and may be made from bone, stone, bronze, iron, or steel. They may be used as tools or weapons. Small decorative knives suffer a –1 penalty to attack and damage rolls.

**Kusari-Gama:** A kusari-gama combines a kama with an attached length of chain. When used to perform a Pull/Trip maneuver, the wielder gains a +4 bonus to the Strength check.

**Lajatang:** A lajatang is a double-ended pole weapon fitted with crescent-shaped blades at both ends. A proficient wielder Threatens flank spaces as well as front spaces.

**Lance:** A lance is a cavalry spear designed for use from horseback. It consists of a long wooden shaft fitted with a steel head. Lances are normally gripped near the butt for extended reach.

Lances are available in three types:

- Light Lance: 10 feet long, 5 pounds
- Medium Lance: 12 feet long, 10 pounds
- Heavy Lance: 14 feet long, 15 pounds

A rider on a heavy warhorse may use any lance. Smaller mounts are limited to lighter types.

**Lantern:** A lantern may be used as an improvised club. If the lantern is lit, roll an item saving throw for glass against a normal blow. If the lantern breaks, the target suffers an additional 1d4 points of damage from burning oil and may catch fire.

**Lasso/Lariat:** A lasso is a length of rope with a sliding loop at one end. It is thrown to entangle a target and may be used to restrain, trip, pin, dismount, or choke. A successful hit however causes no direct damage.

**Light Crossbow:** A light crossbow is a compact crossbow mounted on a tiller and fired using quarrels. It is smaller and faster to handle than a heavy crossbow, but fires shorter bolts. Unlike ordinary bows, crossbows may be used underwater.

**Long Sword:** A longsword is a double-edged sword with a blade typically 35 to 47 inches in length. It is fitted with a crossguard and pommel and is designed for cutting and thrusting.

**Sapara:** A sapara is a short curved sword resembling a smaller version of a khopesh. It is comparable in size to a short sword.

**Short Sword:** A short sword is a double-edged blade approximately two feet in length. It is designed for one-handed use and serves as a secondary weapon.

**Shuriken:** A shuriken is a small thrown weapon with multiple pointed projections. It is easily concealed and thrown by hand.

**Sickle:** A sickle is a crescent-shaped blade mounted on a short handle. It is primarily an agricultural tool but may also be used as a weapon.

Its weight and leverage demand a two-handed grip, but allow powerful, sweeping strikes with sustained momentum. The opposing blades reduce the need to adjust orientation between blows, making it effective in continuous arcs of attack.

**Two-Handed Sword:** A two-handed sword is a large double-edged sword designed for use with both hands. It has a long blade and a counter-weighted pommel. It cannot be used with a shield. It is commonly used by foot soldiers.

**Tulwar:** A tulwar is a curved single-edged sword designed for powerful chopping attacks.

**Vial:** A vial may be thrown as a grenade-like missile. When thrown, only Dexterity and magical adjustments to Armor Class apply; armor is ignored. If the vial strikes the target, the contents inflict their normal effects. Missed attacks may still produce splash effects as described under grenade-like missiles.

**Voulge:** A voulge is a polearm consisting of a large blade mounted on a shaft approximately eight feet in length. The blade narrows toward a thrusting point at the tip and may include a rear hook or fluke.

**Wakizashi:** A wakizashi is a short curved sword similar in construction to a katana.

**War Club:** A war club is a purpose-built fighting club reinforced with spikes, stone inserts, metal bands, or similar additions. Unlike an ordinary club, a war club is not balanced for throwing.

**War Hammer:** A war hammer is a one-handed weapon consisting of a metal head mounted on a short haft, typically about eighteen inches long. Many examples include a rear spike. Some include reinforcing rondels along the grip.

**Whip:** A whip is a long braided lash made of leather or rawhide. It is thicker near the handle and tapers to a narrow cord at the tip. Length commonly ranges from fifteen to twenty-five feet and requires at least 10 feet of space between wielder and target. A whip may be used to attempt disarm or entangle attacks with a –4 penalty to attack rolls. A successful hit may entangle a limb or weapon. Entanglement effects are determined randomly.
### Ammunition

**Barbed Dart:** A blowgun dart is a light barbed projectile intended for hunting and general use.

**Flight Arrow:** A flight arrow is a light general-purpose arrow designed for maximum range. It is commonly used for hunting and warfare.

**Needle:** A blowgun needle is a slender projectile commonly used to deliver poison or similar substances.

**Pellets:** Pellets are small clay projectiles fired from a pellet bow.

**Pile Arrow:** A pile arrow is fitted with a narrow, dense head designed to penetrate armor. A pile arrow fired at short range reduces the target's Armor Class from physical armor by 2 points. Dexterity, cover, and magical adjustments are unaffected.

**Quarrels:** Quarrels are the standard ammunition used by crossbows. They are shorter and heavier than arrows, with thick shafts and sturdy heads designed to withstand the force of a crossbow's release.

**Sheaf Arrow:** A sheaf arrow is a heavier arrow fitted with a broad, weighty head. It sacrifices range in exchange for greater striking power.

**Sling Bullet:** A sling bullet is a cast missile made from lead, bronze, iron, or similar materials. Sling bullets inflict greater damage and achieve greater range than ordinary stones.

**Sling Stone:** A sling stone is a smooth rounded stone selected for use with a sling. Suitable ammunition can often be gathered in rocky terrain or streambeds.

**Stinkpot:** A stinkpot is a fragile container filled with foul-smelling substances and thrown by a staff-sling. When it breaks, it creates a cloud affecting creatures within a 20-foot diameter area. Creatures remaining within the cloud for one full round must save vs. poison or suffer penalties to attack rolls and movement. The cloud persists for 1d3+1 rounds.

**Yanone:** The arrows used with the Daikyu are subtantially larger than other arrows, sometimes exceeding 3 feet in length, and larger in diameter.
