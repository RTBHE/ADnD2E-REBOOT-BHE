# Light, Vision, and Detection
Before a character can meaningfully interact with the world, he must first perceive it. Combat, exploration, navigation, stealth, and countless other actions depend upon the ability to see, hear, or otherwise detect surrounding creatures, objects, and events.

The ability to detect things is influenced by distance, lighting, weather, terrain, concealment, movement, noise, and the capabilities of the observer’s senses. Spotting a distant figure is far easier than identifying it clearly, and hearing movement is often possible long before its source can be seen.

The rules below provide general guidelines for illumination, vision, visibility, and audibility under typical adventuring conditions. The DM should adjust these guidelines whenever unusual environments, supernatural effects, or extraordinary circumstances are involved.
## Light and Illumination
Many creatures rely upon light to see clearly. In areas of insufficient illumination, visibility rapidly deteriorates.

Three general lighting conditions are recognized:

- **Bright Light** allows normal vision.
- **Dim Light** restricts visibility and may reduce identification or detail ranges.
- **Darkness** renders ordinary vision impossible.

Even minimal illumination such as moonlight, distant fires, or phosphorescence may permit limited visibility.

### Light and Exposure
Light sources allow characters to see, but they also make those characters easier to detect.

Creatures outside an illuminated area may often see the light source and those carrying it before the illuminated characters themselves can clearly distinguish what lies beyond the edge of the light.

Torches, campfires, lanterns, magical illumination, and similar light sources therefore make stealth, ambush, concealment, and inconspicuous travel significantly more difficult.
## Vision
Under ordinary conditions, creatures perceive the world primarily through normal sight. Darkness, concealment, weather, distance, and physical obstruction may all reduce or prevent effective vision.

Certain creatures possess unusual visual capabilities that allow them to perceive their surroundings under conditions impossible for ordinary sight.
### Infravision
Some creatures possess the supernatural sensory ability known as **infravision**.

Infravision allows perception in darkness beyond the capabilities of normal sight. The exact nature of this sense varies between worlds and magical traditions. Some scholars describe it as a physiological sensitivity to unseen emanations or radiations beyond normal vision, while others regard it as an inherently magical form of perception. Regardless of its underlying nature, infravision allows creatures to navigate, detect obstacles, and perceive forms in darkness within its listed range.

Infravision does not function identically to normal sight. Fine colour distinctions, written text, intricate detail, and certain illusions may remain difficult or impossible to perceive through infravision alone at the DM’s discretion. 

If not otherwise specified, infravision has a maximum range of 60 feet.
### Using Mirrors
Characters sometimes attempt to observe creatures indirectly through mirrors or reflected surfaces. This is particularly common when confronting creatures whose direct gaze is dangerous, such as a medusa. 

A usable reflection requires sufficient illumination to see clearly. Attempting to fight, move, or perform delicate actions while relying entirely upon a reflected image is highly disorienting. Characters attacking or performing actions solely through a mirror suffer a –2 penalty to attack rolls, ability checks, and proficiency checks. In addition, the character loses all Dexterity bonuses to Armour Class against opponents viewed only indirectly through reflections.
## Visibility and Identification
The ability to perceive distant objects depends greatly upon environmental conditions and the amount of detail being observed.

Under ideal conditions on open ground, a moving man-sized figure may be noticed at great distance, though little useful information can be determined. As distance decreases, increasingly specific details become perceptible.

The categories below describe different levels of visual perception:

- **Movement** indicates the maximum distance at which motion may be perceived.
- **Detection** indicates the maximum distance at which a stationary or moving figure can be *reliably* noticed.
- **Recognition** indicates the maximum distance at which broad characteristics such as size, species, equipment, or general appearance become distinguishable.
- **Identification** indicates the maximum distance at which specific individuals, heraldry, or precise identities may be recognized.
- **Detail** indicates the maximum distance at which small actions, facial expressions, or fine manipulation can be clearly observed.

These ranges assume open terrain, unobstructed sightlines, and approximately human-sized creatures. Terrain does not normally alter atmospheric visibility itself, but it may greatly reduce the likelihood of detection due to concealment, cover, and interrupted lines of sight.

Thus, a creature hidden within dense woods may remain unseen until very close range even on a perfectly clear day.
### Size and Visibility
Creature size strongly affects visibility. When observing creatures of Size Small, halve all visibility ranges.

Large creatures double their Movement, Detection, and Recognition ranges. Exceptionally large creatures, fortifications, ships, dragons, towers, mountains, fires, or armies may be visible at vastly greater distances.
### Environmental Conditions
The ranges above assume broadly Earth-like conditions and ordinary atmospheres. Strange worlds, planar environments, magical darkness, supernatural weather, glowing skies, perpetual twilight, or other unusual circumstances may alter visibility dramatically. The DM should freely adjust visibility when environmental conditions warrant it.
## Audibility
The distances at which sounds may be heard depend heavily upon terrain, weather, background noise, wind, elevation, and the listener’s attention. Sound often travels farther at night or across still water, while forests, rain, urban environments, strong winds, or battlefields may greatly reduce effective hearing distances.

The ranges below assume relatively open terrain and ordinary ambient conditions.

| Sound                                                        | Audible  | Comprehensible |
| ------------------------------------------------------------ | -------- | -------------- |
| Whispering                                                   | 10 ft    | 5 ft           |
| Quiet conversation                                           | 60 ft    | 20 ft          |

| Standard movement in adventuring gear <br/>with metal armour | 200 ft   | —              |
| Raised voice                                                 | 300 ft   | 150 ft         |
| Loud shout or battlefield call                               | 1,000 ft | 300 ft         |
| Horn, bell, or whistle                                       | 1 mile+  | —              |
| Combat                                                       | 1 mile+  | —              |
| Thunder or large battles                                     | 5 miles+ | —              |

The DM should freely modify these distances according to conditions. Strong wind, heavy rain, dense forest, underground passages, crowds, or active combat may sharply reduce hearing range. Still night air, elevated positions, tunnels, mountain valleys, or open water may greatly increase it.

At night, particularly under calm conditions, sound often seems to carry much farther than during the day. This effect is real enough that distant voices, movement, or activity may sometimes be heard at several times their normal daytime range.

Hearing a sound however does not necessarily reveal its precise source, distance, numbers, or intent.
