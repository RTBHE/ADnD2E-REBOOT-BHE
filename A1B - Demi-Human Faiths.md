# Appendix 1B - The Demi-Human Pantheons

Unlike many human faiths which are unique to a specific campaign setting, the deities of the demi-human races presented herein are understood to exist across the multiverse in a stable and continuous form. While local interpretation, mythic emphasis, and minor names may vary from culture to culture, the gods themselves are not typically seen as regional or isolated powers unless a Dungeon Master chooses otherwise. A halfling, elf, dwarf, or gnome invoking their gods is therefore likely to call upon these same divine presence regardless of location, even if the expression of that faith differs widely between communities.

This appendix is not exhaustive. Each pantheon includes its principal deities, but most cultures also recognize minor powers, regional spirits, demigods, and local cult-figures that may not be universally known like the deities presented here. Dungeon Masters are encouraged to develop additional divine figures where appropriate, including community-specific patrons or specialized gods tied to local traditions, professions, or environments, provided they remain consistent with the broader cultural themes of the pantheons.

Players are also reminded that not all deities presented are appropriate for player characters. As outlined in the alignment chapter, evil-aligned play is generally discouraged in standard campaigns, and some gods in these pantheons are thus normally unsuitable for player devotion. Any selection of deity should be made in consultation with the Dungeon Master and grounded in the tone and expectations of the campaign.

Each pantheon is presented in a consistent structure. A general overview establishes the cultural and theological character of the faith as a whole, followed by a description of the relationships within the pantheon and how its deities are understood in relation to one another. Individual deity entries then detail their role in dwarven, elven, gnomish or halfling society, including cultural interpretation, worship practices, and the expectations placed upon their followers. Where relevant, priesthoods, shamans, or other clerical structures are described in mechanical terms following each deity’s cultural section.

#### Priests of Different Races

The racial pantheons described in this appendix are primarily tied to the peoples who created and sustain them. Dwarven gods are shaped by dwarven values and history, elven gods by elven memory and perception, and so forth. As a result, priests who serve a deity outside their own race are uncommon and are often regarded as unusual figures within both cultures.

Such priests, when allowed, must still obey all the expectations, obligations, and behavioural norms of the faith they serve. In most cases, however, they advance more slowly within that tradition than members of the deity’s own race. Rather than being absolutely barred from higher attainment, these “cross-racial” priests face slower progression.

A priest of a different race than the deity they serve uses an advancement threshold (See 'Class Restrictions and Advancement Thresholds' in the *Player Character Races* Chapter) that is the lower of:

- the threshold normally allowed for their own race and class, or
- a threshold 3 levels lower than that associated with the deity’s priesthood or shamans (as appropriate to the character's class)

The above rule applies to humans that choose to serve a demihuman deity, an exception to the rule that humans may advance in any class without restriction. 

This reflects the reality that nonhuman religions are deeply rooted in the psychology, traditions, and social structures of their parent cultures. A human priest of a dwarven forge god, for example, may sincerely embrace dwarven values, but will rarely be fully integrated into the spiritual and cultural framework from which the faith emerged.

Some deities make broader allowances than others. Gods shared between closely related peoples may treat multiple races as spiritually equivalent for purposes of advancement and priesthood. Half-elves, for example, are usually accepted fully within elven priesthoods, while goblin and hobgoblin gods often recognize both peoples equally. Such exceptions are always determined by the deity, the culture of the faith, and ultimately the Dungeon Master.

### Clerics and Racial Faiths

While demi-human religions are typically structured around specialty priesthoods closely aligned with divine portfolios, some campaign settings may permit the use of the cleric class as a more generalist expression of religious service. In such cases, clerics represent broad servants of a racial pantheon rather than narrowly defined specialists of a single deity.

This approach is not the norm within demi-human societies, where religious service is usually more focused and culturally integrated. However, it may be appropriate where party composition, campaign balance, or player needs require a more flexible divine class—particularly when access to essential functions such as healing magic and undead turning would otherwise be unavailable.

If allowed, clerics of demi-human faiths function as generalist priests of the pantheon as a whole rather than devotees of a single deity (though they may still individually prefer one above others). They are expected to respect and uphold the collective values of the pantheon, and their behaviour should reflect the dominant cultural ethos of their people rather than the strict doctrines of one god. In mechanical terms, such clerics do not gain specialty priest granted powers beyond turning undead and do not benefit from exceptional Wisdom-based advancement. Their role is primarily practical rather than doctrinal, and they are more commonly found among adventuring companies than within formal temple hierarchies.

#### Priests and Priesthoods

Priests of the demi-human faiths are not solely ritual specialists but active participants in the life of their communities. Their duties extend beyond worship into governance, education, and practical stewardship. They are expected to advance the goals of their deity through daily action, embodying its principles in both behavior and responsibility.

Depending on the faith, priests may be called upon to maintain social harmony, defend communities, guide agricultural cycles, oversee contracts and rites of passage, or expand the influence of their people through teaching and example. Senior clergy often hold administrative authority within temples or settlements, while junior priests are expected to serve both religious and civic functions as part of their training and obligation.

Even among adventuring priests, religious duty does not end with travel. Such individuals are still expected to act as representatives of their faith, to reinforce its values through conduct, and to contribute resources, knowledge, or wealth back to their communities when possible. In this sense, priesthood is never separate from society, but a continuous expression of it wherever the faithful may go.

## The Morndinsamman (Dwarven Pantheon)

The gods of the dwarves are collectively known as the Morndinsamman, the “Council of Forge and Stone.” Though many deities are honored among different clans and strongholds, all dwarves acknowledge Moradin as Father of the Dwarves, Soul Forger, and first smith of the People.

Dwarven myth teaches that Moradin forged the first dwarves deep beneath the roots of the world. From iron, stone, and mithral drawn from the earth’s heart, he shaped the Dwarf-Fathers upon a great anvil, breathing life into them as hot metal cools beneath the smith’s hammer. Many traditions hold that the first dwarves then fought their way upward through the dark places of the earth, overcoming monsters, fire, collapsing stone, and ancient horrors before reaching the mountains that would become their homes.

These myths establish the central virtues of dwarven religion: endurance, strength, craftsmanship, discipline, and loyalty to kin. Dwarven theology is practical rather than mystical. The gods are revered not through abstract philosophy but through labor well done, oaths faithfully kept, and enemies stoutly resisted. Holy symbols are commonly tools, hammers, anvils, axes, shields, and crafted works rather than celestial or abstract icons.

The Morndinsamman do not merely govern aspects of dwarven life; together they embody the enduring struggle of the People to preserve what is eternal while surviving in a changing world.

#### Moradin the Soul Forger

Moradin is said to have taught the first dwarves the arts of smithing, metalcraft, masonry, and warfare. To the dwarves, these are not merely trades but sacred gifts that separate civilization from chaos. The forge is therefore both workshop and temple, and many dwarves invoke Moradin’s name during crucial acts of craftsmanship: the tempering of steel, the casting of an axe-head, or the laying of a fortress cornerstone.

Unlike the gods of elves or humans, the dwarven deities concern themselves little with beauty for its own sake, mystical revelation, or celestial mysteries. Their power is rooted in earth, fire, labor, and permanence. Even dwarven celebrations tend toward feats of endurance, craftsmanship, oath-making, and remembrance of ancestors rather than frivolity or excess.

#### The Nature of the Dwarven Gods

Though the Morndinsamman are overwhelmingly lawful in outlook, dwarven myths rarely depict them dwelling together in harmony. Instead, many maintain separate halls across the Outer Planes, ruling distinct domains much as powerful clan-lords govern isolated strongholds. Dwarven sages often interpret this as reflecting the dwarven desire for territory, independence, and self-sufficiency. Just as mortal dwarves carve citadels from the earth, so too do their gods maintain separate realms and responsibilities.

The dwarven pantheon is also markedly masculine. Female deities exist, but most traditions place greater emphasis upon gods of smithing, war, stonecraft, and endurance than upon fertility, beauty, or nature. Berronar Truesilver, wife of Moradin and guardian of home and clan, is the most widely revered goddess among the People.

The concerns of the Morndinsamman commonly include:

- Smithing, metalwork, and stonecraft
- Mountains, caver-ns, and the deep earth
- Warfare and defense of the clan
- Fire, forges, and volcanic power
- Physical strength and endurance
- Oaths, tradition, and lawful conduct
- Ancestors, burial, and the honored dead

Few dwarven deities govern seas, forests, agriculture, weather, revelry, or arcane magic. Such matters are generally regarded as distant from the proper concerns of the People. The notable exception is Dugmaren Brightmantle, patron of invention, curiosity, and uncommon learning.

#### Relations Within the Morndinsamman

The gods of the dwarves are united by their concern for the survival and endurance of the People, but they are far from harmonious. Dwarven priests often describe the Morndinsamman as a council of powerful clan-lords: bound by shared ancestry and mutual obligation, yet divided by temperament, priorities, and ancient grudges.

Moradin stands at the center of the pantheon as creator and lawgiver, and most dwarven faiths acknowledge his authority even when they disagree with one another. Berronar’s clergy generally act as mediators among the priesthoods, seeking to preserve unity and prevent disputes from harming the clan as a whole.

Clangeddin’s followers respect Moradin’s order and discipline, though they often view the clergy of Berronar and Dumathoin as overly cautious. Priests of Dugmaren and Muamman are tolerated by more traditional faiths because their curiosity and adaptability ultimately benefit dwarvenkind, though many conservatives regard them as unconventional or unreliable.

Vergadain’s clergy maintain uneasy relations with the rest of the pantheon. Their skill in trade and negotiation is valuable, but their comfort with trickery causes many priests—especially those of Clangeddin and Berronar—to distrust them. Even so, Vergadain’s faith remains accepted within most dwarven societies so long as its followers place dwarven interests above personal gain.

Only Abbathor stands truly outside the trust of the Morndinsamman. His worship is widely condemned among honorable dwarves, and his clergy are viewed as corrupters who place greed above clan, oath, and kinship. Priests of Berronar and Dumathoin in particular are known enemies of Abbathor’s cults.

The duergar god Laduguer is regarded by most dwarven faiths as the patron of a tragic schism among the People: a stern and bitter deity whose followers abandoned kinship for isolation and resentment. Diirinka, patron of the derro, is considered wholly alien and corrupted, his faith representing madness, betrayal, and the perversion of dwarven nature itself.

#### Avatars and Manifestations

A curious feature of dwarven mythology is that many dwarven gods may manifest in gigantic form, appearing as towering dwarves twenty feet tall or more. Priests teach that these manifestations symbolize the immensity of divine strength and the indomitable spirit of the People, rather than any shame regarding dwarven stature. In other tales, the gods appear as ordinary dwarves so they may walk unnoticed among their folk or travel the narrow halls beneath the mountains.

#### Priests and Worship

Priests of the Morndinsamman are expected to place clan and community above personal ambition. Their duties include maintaining ancestral shrines, blessing forges and strongholds, preserving records of lineage and oath, and defending dwarven realms against their ancient enemies.

All dwarven priests are expected to remain vigilant against orcs, goblins, hobgoblins, and their kin. Conflict against such foes is not viewed as mere warfare but as an ongoing sacred duty handed down since the first ascent from the deep places of the world.

### Moradin (Greater God)
Moradin is the creator and chief deity of the dwarven peoples, revered as the Soul Forger, Father of the Dwarves, and master of forge, stone, and fire. Dwarven myth teaches that Moradin shaped the first dwarves deep beneath the roots of the world, forging them upon a great anvil from iron, stone, and mithral drawn from the earth’s heart. In many traditions, he breathed fire into their forms to grant them life, and this divine flame remains central to his worship.

Moradin embodies the virtues most prized by dwarves: endurance, discipline, craftsmanship, strength, loyalty, and the fulfillment of duty. He is stern rather than compassionate, valuing honest labor, perseverance, and the quality of one’s deeds above mere good intentions. To followers of Moradin, civilization is built through effort and maintained through vigilance. A wall properly raised, an oath faithfully kept, or a weapon carefully forged are all acts of devotion.

Forges occupy a sacred place in Moradin’s faith. Nearly every temple maintains an eternal forge-fire representing the divine flame that first gave life to the People. Allowing such a fire to die is considered a terrible omen and spiritual failure. Smithing itself is regarded as a sacred act, and many dwarves quietly invoke Moradin during moments of important craftsmanship or labor.

Moradin’s worship is strongest among hill and mountain dwarves, though he is honored by nearly all dwarven peoples except many duergar. His clergy oversee marriages, clan oaths, coronations, funerary rites, the consecration of halls and strongholds, and the preservation of genealogies and history. Priests are also expected to safeguard the welfare of dwarven communities and encourage the founding, defense, and prosperity of dwarven settlements.

Moradin’s faith teaches that dwarves must ultimately secure their own place in the world through effort and resolve. Divine aid is possible, but should never be expected without worthy struggle.

#### Roleplaying a Worshipper of Moradin
Followers of Moradin tend to value reliability, craftsmanship, and personal discipline. They respect skill earned through labor and often judge others by the quality of their actions rather than their words. Patience, practicality, and endurance are considered virtues, while laziness, wastefulness, and oath-breaking are deeply shameful.

Many worshippers maintain some craft or trade, even if adventuring is their primary profession. Weapons and armor are commonly cared for personally, and tools are treated with respect. Important decisions are rarely made hastily, and traditions are generally viewed as safeguards won through the experience of generations.

Though stern, Moradin’s faithful are not joyless. Feasting, strong drink, storytelling, and celebration are all valued, particularly after hardship honestly overcome or labor successfully completed.

#### Priests of Moradin
Priests of Moradin serve as spiritual leaders, keepers of tradition, judges, historians, and guardians of dwarven communities. They are expected to place clan and community above personal ambition, and are often called upon to settle disputes, oversee important ceremonies, and preserve records of ancestry and oath.

Adventuring priests are encouraged to pursue goals that directly benefit dwarvenkind, such as reclaiming lost strongholds, recovering ancestral relics, defending dwarven lands, or opposing ancient enemies such as orcs, goblinoids, giants, and the duergar.

Moradin’s clergy traditionally forge or help forge the weapons they wield. Even a simple weapon crafted with care is considered spiritually superior to one merely purchased.

**Alignment:** Lawful Good  
**Worshippers:** Primarily dwarves; especially hill and mountain dwarves  
**Areas of Concern:** Creation, smithing, craftsmanship, stone, fire, protection, warfare, clan, law  
**Symbol:** Hammer and anvil

#### Shamans of Moradin
Moradin’s shamans are uncommon among the more traditional dwarven peoples, but may be found among isolated clans or frontier communities where formal temples are absent. They emphasize endurance, ancestral tradition, sacred fire, and the protective aspects of the Soul Forger.

**Ability Requirements:** Strength 10  
**Alignment:** Lawful Good, Lawful Neutral, or Neutral Good  
**Armor:** Must wear metal armor  
**Weapons:** Warhammer required as first weapon proficiency  
**Hit Die:** d6  
**Maximum Level:** 6

### Berronar Truesilver (Intermediate Goddess)
Berronar Truesilver is the dwarven goddess of home, healing, truth, protection, and clan loyalty. Wife of Moradin and guardian of the hearth, she is revered throughout dwarven society as the protector of family, lineage, and the bonds that hold the People together. While often associated with domestic life and marriage, Berronar is no passive deity; her protection extends across all dwarven civilization, from the smallest clanhold to the greatest mountain kingdom.

Dwarven tradition teaches that Berronar preserves the memory and continuity of the People. She watches over births, marriages, oaths, reconciliations, and funerary rites, and her priests maintain genealogies, legal records, and clan histories alongside those of Moradin. To many dwarves, a clan without memory is a clan already lost.

Berronar embodies mercy, generosity, steadfastness, and vigilance. She teaches that strength alone cannot preserve a people without trust, honesty, and mutual obligation. Her worship places great emphasis upon loyalty to kin, the defense of the vulnerable, and the sacred nature of hospitality and sworn bonds.

The exchange of rings in dwarven marriage traditions is closely associated with Berronar, whose symbol is a pair of silver rings. Among many dwarves, gifting a ring signifies profound trust and enduring loyalty, not merely romantic attachment.

#### Roleplaying a Worshipper of Berronar
Followers of Berronar value honesty, reliability, and the protection of family and community. They tend to distrust needless cruelty, selfish ambition, and deceit, particularly when such behavior harms one’s kin or allies. Hospitality is considered a sacred duty, especially toward fellow dwarves and trusted companions.

Many worshippers see themselves as caretakers of tradition and memory. They often preserve family heirlooms, maintain records of ancestry, or feel responsible for protecting vulnerable members of their community. Disputes among kin are viewed as tragic necessities to be resolved carefully rather than opportunities for domination or revenge.

Berronar’s faithful are often more compassionate and diplomatic than many other dwarves, though they remain fully capable of stern judgment against oathbreakers, traitors, and those who threaten the safety of the clan.

#### Priests of Berronar
Priests of Berronar serve as healers, mediators, protectors, archivists, and guardians of clan law and tradition. They oversee marriages, births, funerals, inheritance disputes, and reconciliation ceremonies, and are often entrusted with maintaining genealogical and historical records.

They are expected to defend dwarven communities, care for the sick and injured, preserve truth in legal and social matters, and shelter those under their protection. Many accompany caravans, refugees, or isolated settlements in times of danger.

Unlike the harsher clergy of some dwarven gods, Berronar’s priests are generally expected to temper justice with mercy, though never at the expense of clan safety or sacred obligation.

**Alignment:** Lawful Good  
**Worshippers:** Good-aligned dwarves  
**Areas of Concern:** Protection, healing, home, family, truth, loyalty, clan tradition  
**Symbol:** Two silver rings

##### Priesthood
**Ability Requirements:** Standard  
**Alignment:** Lawful Good  
**Armor:** Any armor  
**Weapons:** Battle axe, club, crossbow, mace, morning star; mace always granted as first weapon proficiency

**Hit Die:** d6

##### Shamans of Berronar
Shamans devoted to Berronar are most often found among smaller clans, frontier settlements, or isolated dwarven communities lacking formal temples. They focus on healing, reconciliation, ancestral guidance, and the protection of hearth and family.

**Ability Requirements:** Standard  
**Alignment:** Lawful Good or Neutral Good  
**Hit Die:** d3+1  
**Maximum Level:** 5

### Clangeddin Silverbeard (Intermediate God)
Clangeddin Silverbeard is the Father of Battle, patron of martial courage, discipline, and honorable warfare. Where Moradin teaches that warfare is one duty among many necessary to preserve the People, Clangeddin exalts battle itself as the truest test of dwarven virtue. To his followers, courage under arms reveals character more clearly than any oath or boast.

Clangeddin embodies the dwarven ideal of the warrior who leads from the front. He values valor, endurance, and straightforward combat, and despises cowardice, treachery, and deceit in war. Victory won through ambush, betrayal, or dishonorable trickery is regarded as hollow and shameful. His priests teach that a dwarf should meet danger openly, hold the line beside their kin, and never abandon comrades while strength remains.

Though stern, Clangeddin is not reckless. His faith places great emphasis upon preparation, discipline, and martial readiness. Weapons must be maintained, formations drilled, and grudges remembered. War is treated as a sacred craft no less worthy of mastery than smithing or stonework.

Clangeddin is especially revered among dwarven warriors defending strongholds against orcs, goblinoids, trolls, giants, and other ancient enemies of the People. Tales of his battles against giant-kin are common throughout dwarven halls, and hill giants in particular are regarded as favored foes of his priesthood.

His worship is direct and communal. Ceremonies often involve martial songs, oath-swearing, weapon blessings, and recounting famous battles or heroic last stands. Priests commonly oversee military training, organize defenses, and accompany dwarven warbands into battle.

#### Roleplaying Followers of Clangeddin
Followers of Clangeddin value bravery, honesty, and martial competence. They are expected to face threats directly and to stand firm even in difficult circumstances. Caution is respected, but cowardice is unforgivable.

Many worshippers are soldiers, guards, caravan defenders, mercenaries of strict reputation, or dwarves who see the defense of clan and stronghold as the highest calling. Even non-warriors often honor Clangeddin before dangerous undertakings or when swearing serious oaths.

His followers tend to:

- Value directness and personal courage
- Respect warriors who earn victory honorably
- Distrust deceitful tactics and treachery
- Maintain weapons and armor with ritual care
- Place loyalty to companions above personal safety
- Treat military discipline as a virtue rather than a burden

#### Priests of Clangeddin
Priests of Clangeddin form a respected warrior-priest tradition within many dwarven communities. Many are fighter/clerics who serve as battlefield leaders, champions, and instructors in the arts of war. They are expected to maintain physical readiness at all times and to train continually in weaponcraft and tactics.

Clangeddin’s clergy often lead defensive campaigns against the enemies of dwarvenkind, organize militia forces, and oversee the martial training of younger dwarves. They are particularly hostile toward giant-kin, whom tradition holds as ancient foes of the Father of Battle.

Unlike many dwarven priests, they place relatively little emphasis upon scholarship, diplomacy, or craftsmanship outside the needs of warfare. Martial excellence is itself considered a sacred offering to their god.

##### Priesthood
- **Ability Score Minimum:** Strength 15
- **Alignment:** Lawful Good
- **Prime Requisite:** Wisdom
- **Weapons:** Any (battle axe required as first proficiency)
- **Armor:** Any metal armor; shields are forbidden
- **Major Spheres:** All, Combat, Guardian, Law, Protection, War
- **Minor Spheres:** Charm, Elemental (Earth), Healing, Wards
- **Turn Undead:** No
- **Hit Die:** d8
- **Shamans:** Not permitted

**Granted Powers**
- **1st Level:** May use _command_ twice per day during combat.
- **5th Level:** May cast _strength_ once per day, gaining the fighter version of the spell (+1d8 Strength).
- **9th Level:** Once per day may deliver a single battle axe attack that inflicts triple damage on a successful hit.

### Dugmaren Brightmantle (Lesser God)
Dugmaren Brightmantle is the dwarven god of curiosity, discovery, invention, and uncommon learning. Unlike most dwarven deities, whose concerns center upon tradition, duty, and established craft, Dugmaren embodies inquiry for its own sake. He encourages experimentation, exploration, and the pursuit of knowledge even when no immediate practical value is apparent.

Among the Morndinsamman, Dugmaren is regarded as an eccentric but ultimately valuable figure. Conservative dwarves often view his followers with mild suspicion or amusement, considering them impractical, overly curious, or dangerously interested in strange ideas. Yet even the most traditional clans acknowledge that many innovations in engineering, mining, and craft first arose from the unconventional thinking he inspires.

Dugmaren teaches that knowledge is itself a worthy treasure. His followers collect maps, books, mechanisms, stories, forgotten histories, unusual minerals, and strange devices simply because such things expand understanding. He favors invention, travel, experimentation, and the recovery of lost lore, especially knowledge forgotten or ignored by more orthodox dwarven traditions.

Despite his unorthodox nature, Dugmaren is neither rebellious nor malicious. He values creativity, optimism, and open-mindedness rather than destruction of tradition. His faith tends to attract dwarves who feel constrained by rigid custom, as well as scholars, engineers, explorers, inventors, cartographers, and travelers.

Worship of Dugmaren is informal by dwarven standards. Shrines are often attached to libraries, workshops, observatories, or experimental forges cluttered with unfinished projects and curious mechanisms. His holy symbol, the open book, represents both recorded knowledge and the willingness to seek what is unknown.

#### Roleplaying Followers of Dugmaren
Followers of Dugmaren are unusually inquisitive for dwarves. They value learning, travel, and discovery, and are often more willing than other dwarves to engage with foreign peoples, strange ideas, and unfamiliar technologies or magic.

Many are inventors, engineers, sages, explorers, architects, historians, or wandering scholars. Others are simply dwarves who believe tradition should guide inquiry rather than restrict it.

His followers tend to:

- Ask questions others consider unnecessary
- Pursue obscure or forgotten knowledge
- Value innovation alongside tradition
- Travel more readily than most dwarves
- Collect unusual objects, texts, or mechanisms
- Approach problems creatively rather than conventionally

Though generally good-natured, followers of Dugmaren may frustrate more conservative dwarves through absent-mindedness, risky experimentation, or fascination with impractical subjects.

#### Priests of Dugmaren
Priests of Dugmaren are expected to seek knowledge actively rather than waiting for it to come to them. Travel, research, experimentation, and scholarly exchange are central duties of the faith. They often act as wandering sages, inventors, chroniclers, or advisors on unusual matters.

Unlike many dwarven priesthoods, Dugmaren’s clergy maintain relatively friendly relations with non-dwarven scholars and are more tolerant of unconventional thinking. While still wary of dangerous magic or reckless ambition, they are among the few dwarven priests willing to study foreign philosophies, ancient ruins, or unfamiliar technologies without immediate suspicion.

Many temples of Dugmaren function partly as libraries, archives, or workshops. Young dwarves showing unusual curiosity are often encouraged by his clergy even when their interests seem impractical.

##### Priest Requirements
- **Ability Score Minimum:** Wisdom 13, Intelligence 10
- **Alignment:** Chaotic Good, Neutral Good, Neutral, or Chaotic Neutral
- **Prime Requisite:** Wisdom
- **Weapons:** Axe (any), bows, broadsword, crossbow, dagger, knife, sling
- **Armor:** Any allowed by class
- **Major Spheres:** All, Charm, Divination, Guardian, Numbers, Protection, Thought
- **Minor Spheres:** Astral, Chaos, Creation, Healing, Wards
- **Turn Undead:** No
- **Hit Die:** d6
- **Shamans:** Not permitted
**Granted Powers**
- **1st Level:** Gains one additional language for every two priest levels attained.
- **3rd Level:** May cast _augury_ once per day.
- **7th Level:** May cast _divination_ once per day.
- **10th Level:** May cast _commune_ once per week.

### Dumathoin (Intermediate God)
Dumathoin, the Keeper of Secrets Under the Mountain, is the dwarven god of mines, hidden wealth, underground exploration, and the mysteries of the deep earth. He is revered as the silent guardian of buried treasures and the protector of those who delve beneath the mountains in search of them.

Dwarven tradition teaches that Dumathoin conceals the riches of the earth until worthy dwarves are prepared to uncover them through patience, skill, and diligence. Veins of ore, hidden caverns, gemstone deposits, and forgotten halls are often believed to exist by his will. To his followers, the deep places of the world are not merely resources to exploit, but sacred mysteries deserving respect.

Unlike Moradin, who embodies the forge and the ordered labor of civilization, Dumathoin represents discovery, endurance, and the unknown spaces beneath the earth. His faith emphasizes caution, perseverance, and reverence for the hidden beauty of stone and darkness. Miners, explorers, tunnel scouts, and deep delvers commonly offer prayers to him before descending underground.

Dumathoin is especially important among mountain and deep dwarves, though his worship is widespread throughout dwarvenkind. Small gemstones carried for luck or protection are commonly associated with his favor.

His temples are often quiet subterranean sanctuaries carved directly into living stone, decorated with natural crystals, polished gems, and carefully preserved caverns rather than elaborate ornamentation. Silence and contemplation are regarded as virtues within many of his holy places.

#### Roleplaying Followers of Dumathoin
Followers of Dumathoin value patience, caution, and quiet determination. They tend to speak little, observe carefully, and avoid wasteful destruction of the underground world. Many believe the earth reveals its secrets only to those willing to listen and endure.

His worshippers are commonly miners, engineers, scouts, gemcutters, tunnel wardens, explorers, and dwarves who spend much of their lives below the surface. They often develop a deep respect for subterranean environments and may regard reckless mining or needless collapse of tunnels as both foolish and impious.

His followers tend to:
- Value patience over haste
- Respect the hidden beauty of stone and gems
- Treat mining and excavation as sacred duties
- Avoid greed and reckless exploitation
- Show caution in unknown underground places
- Prefer quiet competence to boasting

Many also serve as intermediaries with non-hostile underground peoples, believing unnecessary conflict beneath the earth threatens all who dwell there.

#### Priests of Dumathoin
Priests of Dumathoin oversee mines, subterranean roads, excavations, and underground defenses. They are expected to safeguard dwarven delvers, maintain the stability of tunnels and strongholds, and ensure that the earth’s riches are gathered responsibly rather than greedily.

Their clergy frequently accompany expeditions into unknown depths, acting as guides, wardens, and protectors against the dangers of the deep earth. They are also charged with recovering lost dwarven treasures, tombs, and strongholds whenever possible.

Unlike many dwarven priesthoods, Dumathoin’s clergy are often pragmatic negotiators with neutral underground races when conflict can be avoided. Preservation of the deep realms is considered more important than pride or needless warfare.

### Muamman Duathal (Lesser God)
Muamman Duathal is the dwarven god of travelers, wanderers, expatriates, and those who live far from the traditional strongholds of their people. He watches over caravans, distant settlements, mountain roads, and the scattered dwarven communities that survive beyond the halls of their ancestors.

Among the Morndinsamman, Muamman represents an increasingly important aspect of dwarven life: adaptation to a changing world. While many dwarven gods are closely tied to clan strongholds and the permanence of stone, Muamman concerns himself with dwarves who must journey, settle abroad, or build lives among other races.

His faith is especially common among merchants, caravan guards, explorers, mercenaries, engineers, sailors on inland waters, and dwarves dwelling in human cities or frontier settlements. Sundered dwarves in particular often revere him, seeing in his teachings a way to preserve dwarven identity while surviving beyond the old halls beneath the mountains.

Muamman values resilience, adaptability, and fellowship among dwarves far from home. He teaches that the strength of the People does not reside solely in ancient strongholds, but also in the ability of dwarves to endure hardship wherever fate carries them.

Lightning is closely associated with his worship, symbolizing sudden insight, warning, and guidance through dangerous journeys. Storms, rockfalls, and flashes upon distant peaks are commonly interpreted as signs of his attention.

His shrines are often practical rather than grand: roadside sanctuaries, wayhouses, mountain shelters, caravan chapels, or modest urban temples where traveling dwarves may find safety, food, and news from distant kin.

#### Roleplaying Followers of Muamman

Followers of Muamman tend to be more open to travel and contact with outsiders than most dwarves. They value endurance, self-reliance, and adaptability, while still maintaining strong attachment to dwarven customs and kinship.

Many worshippers are traders, scouts, guides, caravan guards, adventurers, or dwarves raised far from traditional clan strongholds. Others are simply dwarves who believe the People must survive and prosper beyond the mountains as the world changes.

His followers tend to:

- Respect travelers and those far from home
- Value practical cooperation with outsiders
- Preserve dwarven traditions even in foreign lands
- Maintain roads, trails, and safe shelters
- See adaptability as strength rather than weakness
- Offer aid to stranded or isolated dwarves whenever possible

Among conservative dwarves, devotees of Muamman are sometimes viewed as overly worldly or insufficiently attached to clan tradition, though their usefulness is rarely denied.

#### Priests of Muamman
Priests of Muamman are wandering clergy who maintain roads, mountain passes, tunnels, caravan routes, and wayhouses. They commonly serve scattered dwarven communities lacking ties to larger strongholds, acting as spiritual leaders, mediators, and messengers between distant clans.

Their temples often function as hostels, shelters, or gathering places for traveling dwarves. Priests are expected to aid those in need upon the road and to maintain safe passage wherever possible.

Unlike many dwarven priesthoods, Muamman’s clergy frequently travel for long periods and are comfortable living among non-dwarven peoples. Many become adventurers or guides, and some spend their entire lives moving between isolated communities.

### Vergadain (Intermediate God)
Vergadain is the dwarven god of wealth, trade, luck, and clever dealings. He is the patron of merchants, traders, and those dwarves who rely on wit, timing, and opportunity as much as strength or endurance. Among the Morndinsamman, he is both respected and regarded with caution, for he embodies the use of cunning and negotiation in ways that sit uneasily beside more rigid dwarven virtues.

Vergadain teaches that wealth is not merely gold or gemstones, but the ability to recognize opportunity, secure advantage, and ensure that resources ultimately benefit dwarvenkind. He is associated with fortune—both favorable and ill-timed—and with the belief that luck rewards those who are attentive, adaptable, and bold.

Unlike most dwarven deities, Vergadain is comfortable with deception, provided it is clever, non-destructive, and ultimately harmless to dwarven interests. His followers distinguish between honest trade, necessary trickery, and dishonorable theft, though outsiders often find these distinctions blurred. He is also regarded as a patron of skilled negotiators, explorers of opportunity, and those who recover lost or hidden valuables.

Temples of Vergadain are often richly appointed halls, counting houses, guild lodges, or merchant sanctuaries. Wealth is displayed as a sign of divine favor, though excessive waste is discouraged. His holy symbol, the gold coin, represents both material wealth and the ever-turning nature of fortune.

#### Roleplaying Followers of Vergadain
Followers of Vergadain are pragmatic, opportunistic, and socially adept. They value cleverness, negotiation, and the ability to turn circumstance to advantage. While not necessarily dishonest, they rarely pass up an opportunity where skill and timing can yield benefit.

Many worshippers are merchants, caravan masters, negotiators, treasure-hunters, artisans, smugglers (of a socially tolerated kind), and adventurers who rely on luck and quick thinking. Some are thieves by profession, though usually those who operate within a strict personal or cultural code.

His followers tend to:
- Value wealth as a measure of success and opportunity
- Prefer negotiation over conflict when possible
- Respect intelligence and clever problem-solving
- Take advantage of favorable circumstances when they arise
- Maintain personal prosperity as a religious expectation
- View luck as something influenced by wit and boldness

Among more traditional dwarves, followers of Vergadain are sometimes regarded as untrustworthy or overly flexible in matters of ethics, though their success in trade often earns grudging respect.

#### Priests of Vergadain
Priests of Vergadain oversee trade guilds, markets, caravan operations, and financial dealings involving dwarves and other peoples. Many act as merchants, agents, or facilitators of exchange between dwarven and non-dwarven communities.

Some priests also serve as treasure seekers or recoverers of lost valuables, especially those believed to have fallen into unworthy hands. Others operate more subtly, ensuring that dwarven interests are advanced through commerce, negotiation, or carefully managed “opportunity.”

Temples of Vergadain are often both religious and commercial institutions, combining worship with trade, accounting, and storage of valuable goods. Wealth is expected, but priests are judged by their ability to increase it responsibly rather than simply hoard it.

### Abbathor (Intermediate God)
Abbathor is the dwarven god of greed, avarice, and the destructive hunger for wealth. He represents one of the deepest flaws in dwarven nature: the desire to possess and hoard treasure beyond reason, loyalty, or need. Where Vergadain embodies clever acquisition and opportunity, Abbathor embodies obsession, envy, and the compulsion to take for oneself regardless of cost.

Among the Morndinsamman, Abbathor is neither trusted nor forgiven. His actions in ancient myth show that he has fought alongside the dwarven gods when it suited him, but his motivations are always self-serving. He is tolerated only because even destructive forces may occasionally serve dwarven interests, though other gods—particularly Berronar and Dumathoin—are said to watch his influence closely and deny him access to the treasures of the deep whenever possible.

His faith teaches that wealth is power and that possession is proof of superiority. Unlike Vergadain, who values exchange and advantage, Abbathor demands accumulation without restraint. Treasure must be taken, hidden, and guarded, and the success of any act is measured only by what is gained. This philosophy makes his worship deeply antisocial and widely condemned among mainstream dwarven society.

Abbathor is associated with theft, betrayal, and the corruption of otherwise honorable dwarves through greed. His followers often rationalize their actions as rightful claim or necessary strength, but their path frequently leads to isolation and conflict with their own kin.

His shrines are secretive, hidden, and often poorly maintained, sometimes located in abandoned tunnels, forgotten vaults, or deep caverns lined with hoarded wealth. His holy symbol, the jeweled dagger, reflects both the allure and violence of stolen treasure.

#### Roleplaying Followers of Abbathor
Followers of Abbathor are driven by greed, ambition, and possessiveness. They view wealth as something to be taken rather than earned through craft or shared through community. Even among evil dwarves, they are often regarded with suspicion due to their tendency toward betrayal and excessive hoarding.

Many worshippers are thieves, corrupt merchants, treasure hunters, mercenaries, or dwarves who have abandoned clan loyalty in pursuit of personal gain. Some begin as ordinary dwarves who gradually fall into obsession with wealth or status.

His followers tend to:
- Prioritize personal gain over clan loyalty
- View wealth as the ultimate measure of worth
- Distrust cooperation unless it serves their interests
- Hoard valuables obsessively, often beyond practical use
- Justify theft or betrayal if it results in profit
- Compete constantly with rivals, including other worshippers

Even among evil dwarves, Abbathor’s faithful are often feared for their willingness to betray allies or escalate conflict over relatively minor treasure.

#### Priests of Abbathor
Priests of Abbathor actively cultivate greed in themselves and others, viewing accumulation of wealth as both sacred duty and proof of devotion. They often serve as corrupting influences within dwarf society, undermining rival priesthoods and spreading obsession with treasure.

Many act as secret thieves, hoarders, or manipulative agents who seek out valuable relics, hidden caches, and opportunities for exploitation. They are particularly hostile toward priests of Dumathoin, whose protection of subterranean wealth directly opposes Abbathor’s desires.

Despite their reputation, Abbathor’s clergy are not necessarily disorganized; many maintain hidden networks for trading, theft, and redistribution of stolen goods among themselves.

### Laduguer (Greater God, Duergar Patron)
Laduguer is the stern and uncompromising patron of the duergar, a god of endurance, toil, and grim self-sufficiency. He teaches that strength is proven through hardship, and that survival in a hostile world is its own form of victory. To his followers, suffering is not a curse but a measure of worth, and weakness is something to be purged through discipline.

Duergar tradition holds that Laduguer was wrongfully denied recognition among the greater dwarven gods and led his people into the deepest reaches of the world rather than submit to injustice. In this telling, isolation is not exile but chosen sovereignty, and the duergar are defined by loyalty to their god above all other claims of kinship.

Laduguer’s faith emphasizes obedience, austerity, and the rejection of unnecessary comforts. Wealth is valued only insofar as it supports survival and strength; excess is seen as indulgence that invites ruin. His followers are expected to be self-reliant, suspicious of outsiders, and unwavering in duty.
#### Roleplaying Notes
Worshippers of Laduguer are grim, disciplined, and highly pragmatic. They respect strength and competence in others but rarely offer trust freely. Cooperation with non-duergar is possible, but always conditional and temporary. Priests of Laduguer often serve as enforcers of discipline within duergar society or as envoys sent into hostile environments where endurance matters more than diplomacy.

Omens are harsh and literal: cracking stone, extinguished light, tools breaking at critical moments, or the sudden failure of poorly prepared plans.

- **Alignment**: lawful neutral (duergar only; non-evil duergar cultures vary by setting tone)
- **Areas of Concern**: endurance, discipline, survival, isolation, duty, harsh environments
- **Symbol**: a clenched fist within a stone circle

### Priests and Worship
Priests of Laduguer are expected to embody extreme discipline and self-sufficiency. They oversee labor, punishment, and training within duergar communities and often lead expeditions into the deepest and most dangerous parts of the Underdark.

Adventuring priests serve as survivalists and hard leaders rather than healers or diplomats. They are expected to endure conditions others cannot and to enforce duergar laws without hesitation.

### Diirinka (Lesser God)
Diirinka is the patron of the derro, a god of distorted genius, hidden knowledge, and unstable magic. Derro faith teaches that Diirinka shaped their race through forbidden insight and experimentation, granting them their unnatural gifts and separating them from lesser dwarvenkind. His teachings emphasize cunning, secrecy, and superiority through intellect rather than strength.

Derro mythology is fragmented and contradictory, but consistently portrays Diirinka as a divine innovator who elevated his chosen people beyond ordinary dwarven limits. Among the derro, madness and brilliance are often seen as closely linked, and Diirinka is believed to reward those who push thought and magic beyond safe boundaries.

Diirinka is also associated with betrayal, hidden truths, and the dangers of knowledge taken too far. Even among his worshippers, he is not trusted so much as feared and obeyed. His influence is credited with both their magical talents and their instability.

#### Roleplaying Notes
Worshippers of Diirinka are secretive, erratic, and intensely self-focused. They value personal insight above all authority and rarely cooperate unless it serves their own designs. Even then, alliances are unstable and often temporary.

Derro priests and savants act as interpreters of Diirinka’s will, pursuing experiments, magical study, and psychological manipulation. They are often driven by obsession rather than doctrine, and consider restraint a form of weakness.

Omens are interpreted as intrusive thoughts, shifting symbols, hallucinations, or sudden “clarity” about hidden truths. Reality is often assumed to be negotiable.

- **Alignment**: chaotic neutral (derro only; most interpretations trend toward evil behavior in practice)
- **Areas of Concern**: aberrant magic, hidden knowledge, transformation, obsession, forbidden insight
- **Symbol**: a fractured rune or broken crystal sphere

### Priests and Savants
Diirinka’s clergy do not follow conventional priestly traditions. Instead, selected derro become **savants**, whose abilities are innate and shaped through divine alteration rather than formal worship.

Savants are expected to pursue experimentation, arcane development, and the expansion of derro understanding of magic at any cost. Failure is often treated as corruption or discard rather than correction. Their “service” is measured by discovery, not devotion.

Derro communities may also recognize minor combat-oriented or death-aspected cult figures, but these are subordinate expressions of Diirinka’s will rather than true independent deities.

## The Seldarine (Elven Pantheon)

Elven religion centres upon the Seldarine, the “fellowship of brothers and sisters of the wood,” a pantheon bound by kinship, artistry, memory, and reverence for the living world. Though myths differ in detail across realms and ages, all elves acknowledge Corellon Larethian as creator, protector, and first champion of the People. The gods of the elves dwell together in Arvandor, the High Forest of the Outer Planes, and are portrayed not as distant rulers but as exemplars of elven ideals: beauty without vanity, freedom without cruelty, and mastery without domination.

Elven theology is subtle and symbolic. Contradictory myths are accepted without concern, for elves hold that truth may wear many faces. Most traditions describe the Seldarine as children or emanations of Corellon, while others claim they arose from the primal spirits of forests, seas, winds, moonlight, and memory itself. The gods are deeply intertwined with the natural world, often appearing as both person and landscape simultaneously. Thus a deity may be a great tree, a storm wind, a moonlit pool, and an elf-shaped being all at once.

The Seldarine are notable for their fluid and androgynous nature. Many deities may assume male or female forms freely, though each favors certain aspects in myth and worship. Corellon is invariably depicted as possessing an unearthly beauty blending masculine and feminine qualities alike, embodying the totality and mutability of elven creation.

The drow gods, sometimes known as the Dark Seldarine, are understood in some traditions as a fractured reflection of the same divine pattern rather than a separate pantheon. Where the Seldarine represent balance between elven aspects, the drow gods represent those same aspects unbalanced, isolated, or driven to extremity.

#### Corellon and Gruumsh

The defining myth of the elves is the battle between Corellon and Gruumsh, the great enemy of the People. In the oldest tales, the two fought from dawn until dusk while armies clashed beneath them. Gruumsh wielded spear and burning darkness, while Corellon answered with moonlit blade and sorcery. As night approached, the orc god grew stronger in the gathering gloom, and defeat seemed certain.

At dusk, Corellon looked to the rising moon and struck a final blow, blinding Gruumsh in one eye and driving him from the field. Some traditions say the moon goddess aided Corellon with illusions and moonlight; others insist the victory was won by Corellon alone. All agree that the triumph preserved the elven peoples from destruction and established the eternal enmity between elves and orcs.

To elves, this tale is not merely history but instruction. However refined elven culture may appear, the People remember that beauty, memory, and artistry survive only when defended with courage and vigilance.

#### The Fall of the Drow
Another foundational myth recounts the corruption of the dark elves by Lolth. Elven tradition teaches that evil cannot take root without pride, ambition, cruelty, or the desire to dominate already present within the heart. Thus the existence of the drow is regarded with sorrow and shame as much as hatred.

In the great wars against Lolth, Corellon cast the traitorous elves into the depths beneath the earth. As the Spider Queen’s clouds of darkness dispersed, sunlight returned to the battlefields above and the drow fled into the Underdark. The tale serves as a warning repeated throughout elven culture: arrogance, possessiveness, and the pursuit of mastery without harmony are the first steps toward corruption.

#### Themes of Elven Faith
The Seldarine concern themselves with:

- Nature, memory, and living beauty
- Magic, artistry, and disciplined mastery
- Love, joy, and fellowship
- Dance, music, poetry, and fleeting experience
- Moonlight, stars, seasons, and the passage of time
- Freedom, individuality, transformation, and playful mischief
- Harmony between peoples and with the natural world

Most elven faiths place little emphasis upon conquest, rigid hierarchy, tyranny, or martial glory for its own sake. Even warrior deities are defenders and guardians rather than conquerors.

Different elven peoples naturally emphasize different aspects of the Seldarine. High elves most often venerate Corellon as patron of balance, diplomacy, and refined mastery; sylvan elves emphasize the immediacy of the living world and the spirits of forest and hunt; grey elves focus upon memory, scholarship, magical discipline, and the preservation of ancient traditions; while sea elves revere the shifting rhythms and hidden depths of the oceans. Despite these differences, all recognize the Seldarine as a single divine fellowship.

Elven religion also teaches tolerance toward other goodly peoples, especially those who dwell within forests and wild places. The gods favor cooperation with sylvan races and command vigilance against the enemies of the People, most notably orcs and drow.

#### Relations Within the Seldarine
The gods of the Seldarine are bound less by rigid hierarchy than by shared kinship, memory, and purpose. Though Corellon Larethian is acknowledged as first among them, the elven deities are rarely portrayed as rulers and subjects. Instead, they are companions, rivals, lovers, teachers, and kindred spirits whose relationships shift like seasons, music, or moonlight.

Most elven faiths overlap freely, and few elves devote themselves exclusively to a single deity. An elf may honor Corellon in war, Sehanine beneath the moon, Rillifane within the forest, and Hanali during festivals of love and beauty without contradiction. Priests of different gods generally cooperate easily, seeing themselves as guardians of different aspects of the same people and world.

The Seldarine value harmony, but not uniformity. Erevan Ilesere’s mischief frustrates more disciplined deities, Fenmarel Mestarine’s bitterness isolates him from the fellowship, and Deep Sashelas is often regarded as distant by surface elves. Yet these divisions are viewed as natural tensions within elven existence rather than causes for schism.

Only Lolth stands wholly outside the fellowship. Her betrayal and corruption of the drow remains the great spiritual wound of elven mythology, and opposition to her influence unites even otherwise distant elven faiths. The eternal struggle against Gruumsh and the powers of destructive hatred likewise binds the Seldarine together despite their differences.

#### Priests and Worship
Priests of the Seldarine are expected to preserve sacred groves, defend elven lands, maintain ancient knowledge, and oppose corruption wherever it appears. Worship is rarely solemn. Ceremonies often include music, dance, storytelling, feasting, and the creation of art beneath open skies. Holy days are commonly tied to celestial events, seasonal changes, tides, migrations, or other natural phenomena rather than rigid calendars.

All priests of the Seldarine are expected to remain watchful against the schemes of drow and orcs, while maintaining friendship with other sylvan folk and defenders of the natural world.

### Corellon Larethian (Greater God)
Corellon Larethian is the creator, protector, and first champion of the elven peoples, revered throughout the Seldarine as the source of elven life, artistry, magic, and grace. Elven myth teaches that the People were born from Corellon’s blood during the ancient war against Gruumsh, and that the beauty, creativity, and sorrow of the elves all flow from that divine origin.

Corellon embodies the highest ideals of elvenkind: mastery without tyranny, beauty without vanity, freedom without cruelty, and strength guided by wisdom. Though often depicted in masculine form, Corellon is understood to transcend mortal distinctions of gender and appearance, reflecting the fluid and ever-changing nature of elven creation itself.

To the elves, Corellon is both artist and guardian. He is patron of magic, music, poetry, craftsmanship, swordplay, and disciplined mastery of every refined art. Yet he is also remembered as the eternal defender of the People against the forces that would destroy or corrupt them. Elven tradition teaches that beauty and civilization survive only through vigilance and courage.

The wars against Gruumsh and Lolth remain central to Corellon’s worship. His defeat of Gruumsh established the ancient enmity between elves and orcs, while the banishment of Lolth and the drow serves as a warning against pride, cruelty, and the desire to dominate others. Priests commonly teach that corruption begins not with monstrous acts, but with arrogance and the abandonment of harmony.

Despite his martial role, Corellon is not a conqueror. He values creativity, growth, discovery, and refinement above domination or rigid authority. Elven myths often portray him wandering unseen among his people, observing their deeds, inspiring artists and heroes, and quietly guiding those who strive toward wisdom and beauty.

Shrines to Corellon are commonly found in sacred groves, open-air temples, moonlit glades, and halls adorned with living art, flowing water, silver light, and finely crafted works. His symbol, the crescent moon, represents beauty, transformation, vigilance, and the enduring spirit of the elves.

#### Roleplaying Followers of Corellon
Followers of Corellon strive to embody grace, discipline, creativity, and compassion. They believe skill should be cultivated not for personal glory, but to elevate both oneself and the world around them.

Many worshippers are warriors, wizards, artists, diplomats, bladesingers, lorekeepers, or protectors of elven lands. Others simply seek balance between beauty and strength in their daily lives.

His followers tend to:

- Value artistry and disciplined mastery
- Defend beauty, memory, and civilization from corruption
- Seek harmony rather than domination
- Respect learning, creativity, and self-improvement
- Oppose cruelty, tyranny, and needless destruction
- Remain vigilant against the influence of drow and orcs

Corellon’s faithful are often patient and thoughtful, but can become fierce and relentless when defending their people or sacred places.

#### Priests of Corellon

Priests of Corellon serve as spiritual leaders, teachers, artists, diplomats, warriors, and guardians of elven communities. They are expected to cultivate excellence in at least one discipline—commonly martial skill, magic, music, poetry, craftsmanship, or scholarship—and to encourage such growth in others.

Their duties include preserving sacred groves and ancient lore, mediating disputes among elves and allied peoples, maintaining harmony within elven communities, and defending the People against external threats and internal corruption alike.

Corellon’s clergy often work closely with priests of other Seldarine deities, seeing themselves as protectors not merely of elven lands, but of the ideals embodied by elven civilization itself.

- **Armor:** Any
- **Major Spheres:** All, Charm, Combat, Creation, Guardian, Healing, Necromantic, Protection, Sun, War, Wards

### Sehanine Moonbow (Intermediate Goddess)
Sehanine Moonbow is the elven goddess of the moon, dreams, mysticism, journeys, illusion, and the passage of the spirit beyond mortal life. Among the Seldarine, she is most closely associated with transcendence, hidden wisdom, intuition, and the quiet mysteries that lie beyond ordinary understanding.

Though Corellon’s symbol is the crescent moon, it is Sehanine whom elves most strongly associate with moonlight itself, particularly the full moon and the pale silver radiance that reveals truths softly rather than harshly. Her worship emphasizes mystery, reflection, imagination, and the unseen paths connecting memory, spirit, and destiny.

Sehanine governs both physical and spiritual journeys. She is revered by travelers, mystics, diviners, dreamers, and those elves approaching the final transition from the mortal world. Elven tradition teaches that when an elf’s long life nears its close, Sehanine guides the spirit toward hidden sanctuaries, distant shores, or otherworldly realms beyond mortal lands. These places are said to be concealed behind veils of moonlight, mist, dream, and illusion.

Because of this role, Sehanine is also closely associated with death—not as destruction or terror, but as peaceful passage and transformation. Funeral rites among many elves invoke her protection so the spirit may travel safely onward. Her priests are among the foremost opponents of undead creatures, which are regarded as violations of the natural transition between life, memory, and transcendence.

Sehanine’s worship often involves trance, meditation, dance, dreams, poetry, music, and altered states of awareness. Elves commonly believe she speaks through visions, intuitions, symbolic dreams, fleeting moonlit revelations, and moments of profound emotional clarity.

Her holy symbol, the full moon marked by a pale crescent “moonbow,” represents guidance through darkness, spiritual transformation, and hidden truth revealed gently rather than forcefully.

#### Roleplaying Followers of Sehanine
Followers of Sehanine are reflective, intuitive, and spiritually minded. They value mystery, imagination, empathy, and emotional understanding, and often see reality as layered with symbols, dreams, and hidden meanings.

Many worshippers are seers, mystics, travelers, lorekeepers, illusionists, healers, poets, astronomers, or guardians of sacred burial sites. Others simply feel drawn toward solitude, moonlit contemplation, or the search for deeper truths beneath appearances.

Her followers tend to:

- Trust intuition and dreams alongside reason
- Respect mystery rather than demanding certainty
- View death as transition rather than ending
- Oppose undead and spiritual corruption
- Value beauty, subtlety, and emotional insight
- Seek hidden wisdom through reflection and experience

Followers of Sehanine are often calm and compassionate, though they may appear distant or enigmatic to more practical-minded folk.

#### Priests of Sehanine
Priests of Sehanine serve as mystics, seers, caretakers of the dead, interpreters of dreams, and guardians of hidden lore. They oversee funeral rites, protect sacred burial places, aid elves preparing for final journeys, and preserve magical and historical knowledge associated with visions, illusion, divination, and spiritual insight.

Many also act as guides for travelers through dangerous or unfamiliar lands, especially under moonlight or across hidden paths known only to the elves. Temples of Sehanine are commonly found in moonlit groves, quiet towers, secluded gardens, crystal caverns, or sacred places overlooking water or open skies.

Her clergy are expected to oppose undead creatures actively, seeing them as unnatural imprisonments of spirit and memory.

- **Weapons:** Bow, javelin, quarterstaff, sickle, sling, staff sling

### Aerdrie Faenya (Intermediate Goddess)
Aerdrie Faenya is the elven goddess of air, weather, freedom, birds, and the open sky. She embodies the restless joy of wind over forest canopies, the exhilaration of flight, and the untamed beauty of storms. Among the Seldarine, she is often regarded as one of the most free-spirited and least bound by custom or settlement, preferring wandering skies to courts or halls.

Elven tradition associates Aerdrie with rain, renewal, and the life-giving movement of air across the world. Gentle rains, high mountain winds, migrating birds, and sudden summer storms are all considered expressions of her presence. Though capable of fierce tempests and lightning, she is not a destructive deity by nature; storms in her mythology are symbols of freedom, change, cleansing, and the refusal to remain confined.

Aerdrie values independence, movement, curiosity, and compassion toward all creatures of the sky. Her faith is especially common among elves who travel widely, dwell in high forests or mountains, or feel constrained by rigid traditions. Rangers, scouts, messengers, fliers, musicians, and wanderers often honor her.

Unlike many elven deities deeply tied to memory or ancient custom, Aerdrie represents spontaneity and emotional immediacy. Her worshippers are encouraged to experience the beauty of the world directly rather than preserving it only through remembrance.

Birds hold special significance in her faith, especially eagles, hawks, swans, and other creatures associated with grace and freedom. Friendly relations with avian peoples and magical flying creatures are regarded as sacred obligations by many of her clergy.

Her shrines are commonly located in exposed natural places: hilltops, forest clearings open to the sky, sea cliffs, bridges, towers, and mountain ledges where wind and weather can be felt directly. Music played upon flutes, whistles, horns, and other wind instruments often accompanies her ceremonies.

#### Roleplaying Followers of Aerdrie

Followers of Aerdrie tend to value freedom, emotional honesty, curiosity, and movement. They dislike confinement, unnecessary hierarchy, and stagnant routines. Many are adventurous, expressive, and unusually open-minded by elven standards.

Her worshippers are commonly scouts, messengers, musicians, travelers, sailors, rangers, weather-watchers, fliers, and elves who feel more comfortable beneath open skies than within enclosed cities or courts.

Her followers tend to:
- Value freedom over rigid obligation
- Prefer movement, travel, and exploration
- Feel strong emotional connections to weather and sky
- Respect birds and flying creatures
- Distrust oppressive authority and confinement
- Embrace spontaneity and emotional expression
- See storms as cleansing rather than frightening

Though generally gentle in temperament, followers of Aerdrie can become fierce when defending freedom or resisting tyranny.

#### Priests of Aerdrie

Priests of Aerdrie serve as wanderers, weather-watchers, scouts, messengers, and intermediaries between elven communities and avian races. Many spend little time in permanent temples, traveling instead between sacred natural sites beneath the open sky.

Unlike many elven priesthoods, Aerdrie’s clergy place relatively little emphasis upon formal scholarship or ancient ritual. Direct experience of the living world is considered the truest form of devotion.

### Erevan Ilesere (Intermediate God)
Erevan Ilesere is the elven god of mischief, change, trickery, rogues, and freedom from constraint. Among the Seldarine, he embodies spontaneity, irreverence, cleverness, and the refusal to allow life to become rigid or joyless. He is beloved by pranksters, wanderers, thieves, shapeshifters, and those elves who see excessive order as a danger to the spirit.

Elven mythology portrays Erevan as endlessly unpredictable. In one tale he appears as a wandering minstrel, in another a fox, a child, a beggar, or a laughing spirit in the trees. He delights in confusion, harmless deception, and turning certainty upside down. Yet beneath the humor and chaos lies a deeper purpose: Erevan opposes tyranny, stagnation, cruelty, and pride wherever they become too entrenched.

Unlike darker trickster figures, Erevan’s mischief is rarely malicious for its own sake. He humiliates arrogance, disrupts oppressive authority, frees the trapped, and reminds the People that joy and adaptability are necessary for survival. To his followers, excessive seriousness and rigid tradition can become prisons no less dangerous than chains.

Erevan is also associated with transformation and shapeshifting, both literal and symbolic. His faith teaches that identity is fluid, that change is natural, and that cleverness often succeeds where force fails. Many myths describe him escaping impossible situations through wit, disguise, or sheer audacity.

He is especially popular among younger elves, wanderers, rogues, scouts, and members of sylvan races such as sprites, pixies, and brownies. Many small forest folk regard him as a friend and protector against larger and more powerful beings.

His shrines are rarely permanent or formal. A hidden glade, a laughing brook, a hollow tree, or a campsite where stories and wine are shared beneath the stars may all serve as places of worship. Symbols associated with him often include asymmetrical stars, masks, foxes, laughter, wine, and shifting reflections.

#### Roleplaying Followers of Erevan

Followers of Erevan value freedom, cleverness, adaptability, and humor. They dislike excessive hierarchy, rigid law, and anyone who takes themselves too seriously. Many delight in harmless pranks, wordplay, disguises, and unsettling the expectations of others.

His worshippers are commonly rogues, scouts, illusionists, wanderers, performers, smugglers, tricksters, and adventurers who prefer improvisation to careful planning. Some are genuinely irresponsible, but many use humor and unpredictability to resist cruelty or undermine oppressive power.

His followers tend to:

- Value freedom over rigid order
- Distrust excessive authority and tradition
- Use wit and deception rather than brute force
- Enjoy harmless pranks and social confusion
- Protect weaker folk from bullies and tyrants
- Embrace change rather than fear it
- Treat life as something to be experienced playfully

Though often lighthearted, followers of Erevan can become surprisingly serious when confronting cruelty, oppression, or those who abuse power.

#### Priests of Erevan

Priests of Erevan are among the least structured clergy within the Seldarine. Many wander freely rather than maintaining fixed temples, and some combine priestly training with the skills of thieves, scouts, entertainers, or illusionists.

They are expected to challenge rigid authority, embarrass the arrogant, and defend weaker folk against domination. Their methods are indirect and unpredictable: trickery, infiltration, disguise, confusion, and social disruption are all considered acceptable tools.

Many priests cultivate skills in stealth, disguise, storytelling, acrobatics, or shapeshifting magic. Ceremonies are informal and joyous, often involving feasts, music, wine, dancing, and elaborate practical jokes.

Despite their reputation, Erevan’s clergy are not nihilistic. Their chaos is intended to preserve freedom, creativity, and vitality rather than destroy society outright.

### Fenmarel Mestarine (Lesser God)

Elven tradition portrays Fenmarel as the protector of those abandoned, forgotten, hunted, or forced to survive at the edges of the world. He watches over scattered woodland tribes, hidden forest enclaves, nomadic elves, and communities living in lands too dangerous or remote for stronger powers to defend. His worship is especially common among feral elves, reclusive sylvan peoples, and isolated clans struggling against encroaching enemies.

Fenmarel teaches caution, self-reliance, and survival through secrecy. His followers avoid unnecessary attention, conceal their homes, and trust outsiders slowly, if at all. Camouflage, ambush, woodland craft, tracking, and the quiet observation of threats are regarded as sacred skills. To his worshippers, survival itself is a victory in a hostile world.

Though withdrawn from much of elven society, Fenmarel is not evil and does not side with the enemies of the People. He bears a deep hatred for the drow and opposes Lolth relentlessly, often acting as a hidden guardian against dangers emerging from the depths or advancing into isolated forests.

Unlike many elven gods associated with beauty, artistry, or celebration, Fenmarel’s faith is austere and practical. His shrines are concealed places deep within forests, swamps, jungles, or hills, often marked only by subtle symbols known to his followers. Silence, caution, and concealment are themselves considered forms of reverence.

Fenmarel has no formal priesthood in the traditional sense. Instead, his worship is guided by shamans, hunters, scouts, and spiritual leaders who combine practical survival skills with intuitive spiritual traditions.

Followers of Fenmarel tend to be cautious, self-reliant, and highly observant. They distrust centralized authority and often feel uncomfortable in crowded settlements or formal courts. Many believe civilization inevitably weakens vigilance and draws unwanted attention.

### Labelas Enoreth (Intermediate God)

Labelas is especially associated with history, philosophy, education, genealogy, astronomy, and the preservation of knowledge. Labelas is closely associated with Sehanine Moonbow in matters of life’s passage. - Preserve records, traditions, and oral histories

Through these roots flows the rhythm of seasons and the pulse of woodland life.

Rillifane’s concern is the balance and continuity of natural life within forests. He watches over woodland realms to ensure that all living things may fulfill their place in the natural order without being abused or destroyed beyond necessity. His faith emphasizes preservation, restraint, and reverence for living ecosystems rather than domination of them.

This philosophy brings him into harmony with Corellon Larethian, but into philosophical tension with Solonor Thelandira. Hunting is not forbidden within Rillifane’s domains, but it is strictly bounded by necessity. Hunting for survival is permitted; hunting for sport or excess is an affront to his will, and woodlands under his deep presence are often treated as sanctuaries even by other elven gods.

Rillifane is closely allied with Emmantiensien, god of treants, and the two are said to engage in long, deliberate communion that reflects the slow, enduring nature of the forests themselves. Among sylvan powers, he is widely respected, and he is a favored guest of Titania’s Seelie Court. Skerrit of the centaurs is among his closest friends.

Unlike many elven deities, Rillifane is deliberate, contemplative, and unchanging over vast spans of time. He is among the least impulsive of the Seldarine, preferring reflection and natural unfolding over direct intervention.

#### Roleplaying a Worshipper of Rillifane
Followers of Rillifane are deeply attuned to woodland life and tend to view forests as living communities rather than resources. They value patience, balance, and restraint, and they are often uncomfortable with unnecessary destruction of natural environments even when it serves practical ends.

Most worshippers are wood elves, rangers, druids, or forest guardians. Some voadkyn and other sylvan creatures also honor him in isolated regions of deep wilderness.

His followers tend to:

- Treat forests as sacred, living systems
- Prefer preservation over exploitation of nature
- Hunt only for necessity, never for sport
- Avoid unnecessary alteration of natural landscapes
- Respect the cycles of growth, death, and renewal
- Maintain quiet vigilance rather than overt dominance

Even among other elves, Rillifane’s faithful are sometimes seen as solemn and intensely conservative in matters of ecology, though they are respected for their deep understanding of natural balance.

#### Priests of Rillifane

Priests of Rillifane are druidic guardians of woodland realms. They serve as caretakers, mediators, and protectors of forest ecosystems, ensuring that hunting, settlement, and travel within sacred woods do not disrupt the natural balance.

They are responsible for maintaining harmony between elves and sylvan creatures, overseeing rites of passage in wood-elf society, and safeguarding forests from disease, corruption, and destructive intrusion. Death rites are typically handled by Sehanine Moonbow or Labelas Enoreth, leaving Rillifane’s clergy focused on life, growth, and continuity.

Rillifane’s priests often act as quiet wardens rather than political leaders, intervening only when natural balance is threatened.

### Lolth (Intermediate Goddess)

Lolth is the principal deity of the drow and the unquestioned center of their divine hierarchy. She embodies chaos given structure through fear, ambition, and domination. To the drow, she is both creator and predator: the source of their strength and the eternal test of their survival.

Drow myth holds that Lolth shaped their society through suffering, trial, and relentless internal competition. Weakness is not merely punished in her doctrine—it is harvested. Strength is proven not through peace or stability, but through the ability to endure betrayal, upheaval, and constant internal threat.

Lolth does not seek disorder for its own sake. Rather, she cultivates controlled chaos: a society in which drow are forced to struggle against one another in ways that sharpen intelligence, cruelty, and adaptability. Excessive stagnation is seen as decay; uncontrolled conflict is seen as waste. The ideal drow society is one in which ambition is constant but always directed through decisive, devastating acts rather than prolonged instability.

Her ultimate goals for the drow are twofold:

- The domination of the Underdark and all weaker subterranean peoples
- The eventual humiliation or destruction of surface elf civilizations, even if symbolic rather than practical

Lolth’s influence extends beyond religion into every aspect of drow governance. Her priesthood functions as both religious authority and political architecture, shaping noble houses, succession, punishment, and war.

#### Roleplaying a Worshipper of Lolth

Followers of Lolth are defined by ambition, suspicion, and calculated ruthlessness. Faith is not comforting; it is a framework for survival and advancement in a hostile society where trust is a liability.

Drow who worship Lolth are expected to:

- Pursue personal advancement without hesitation or sentimentality
- View alliances as temporary instruments, not moral commitments
- Accept betrayal as a normal condition of power
- Demonstrate strength through decisive action, not prolonged conflict
- Recognize hierarchy as both necessary and constantly contestable

Even devout worshippers are not “safe” in their devotion. Lolth’s favor is unstable, and certainty is itself a weakness. Her followers do not seek peace with their goddess—they seek not to be noticed as failures.
### Kiaransalee (Demigoddess)

Kiaransalee is the drow goddess of vengeance and the restless dead. She is a being of obsession and retribution, defined by the persistence of remembered wrongs. Where Lolth encourages ambition and dominance, Kiaransalee demands repayment—absolute, final, and often disproportionate.

Her theology centers on the idea that death is not an ending but a tool. Those who die unjustly may be reclaimed, reshaped, and made instruments of revenge. Hatred, in her doctrine, is not a flaw but a sustaining force that binds the dead to purpose.

Kiaransalee is not a stabilizing force in drow society. She is a pressure point—appearing most often in contexts of betrayal, assassination, or the aftermath of failed ambition. Her influence is strongest where resentment is allowed to endure and calcify into purpose.

Even among the drow, her worship is feared. It is one thing to kill an enemy; it is another to ensure they do not remain at rest.

#### Roleplaying a Worshipper of Kiaransalee

Followers of Kiaransalee are driven by grievance, memory, and fixation. They do not forget slights, and they do not consider forgiveness a virtue.

They tend to:

- Preserve injuries—real or perceived—as sacred motivation
- Treat death as a resource rather than an end state
- Prefer long-term vengeance over immediate retaliation
- Maintain secrecy and emotional detachment
- View emotional restraint as a tool for precision rather than morality

Among drow, they are often used—and feared—as specialists in post-conflict resolution: the ones who ensure that enemies do not remain “merely defeated.”

### Vhaeraun (Lesser God)
Vhaeraun is the drow god of thievery, stealth, and the expansion of drow influence upon the surface world. He is also the patron of male drow, teaching that males are not inherently inferior to females and may pursue power, skill, and authority in their own right. This doctrine places him in quiet opposition to the dominant teachings of Lolth’s priesthood, though Vhaeraun lacks the strength to openly challenge her rule and therefore relies on secrecy, subversion, and avoidance rather than confrontation.

Vhaeraun’s faith is built around infiltration, disguise, and long-term manipulation. His followers believe that drow supremacy should extend beyond the Underdark through subtle conquest rather than open war. Surface infiltration, theft of knowledge and power, and the gradual undermining of enemies are all considered sacred acts.

Unlike more overtly destructive powers, Vhaeraun favors patience and concealment. His doctrine emphasizes that victory belongs to those who are unseen until the moment they choose to strike.

#### Roleplaying a Worshipper of Vhaeraun

Followers of Vhaeraun are secretive, prideful, and highly independent. They often present themselves as pragmatists rather than ideologues, but their underlying goal is always the advancement of drow power and the weakening of Lolth’s dominance over drow society.

Many worshippers are male drow who reject traditional roles, but the faith also attracts spies, thieves, surface infiltrators, scouts, and dissidents within Lolthite cities. Most operate in secrecy, even from other drow, due to the extreme danger posed by discovery.

Vhaeraun’s followers tend to:

- Favor stealth, disguise, and indirect action
- Value personal competence regardless of gender
- Prefer infiltration over open confrontation
- Operate in small, tightly controlled cells
- Seek influence in surface societies
- Distrust Lolthite authority structures

Even among evil drow, Vhaeraun’s worshippers are often regarded as dangerously subversive, since their goals undermine established power structures rather than simply competing within them.

#### Priests of Vhaeraun

Priests of Vhaeraun serve as agents of infiltration, rebellion, and covert expansion. In drow society, they typically conceal their faith, operating under false identities or within criminal and mercantile networks. Exposure is usually fatal, as Lolth’s priesthood treats them as heretics and traitors.

Their duties include spreading dissent among male drow, undermining Lolthite authority where possible, and establishing hidden footholds in surface civilizations for future drow influence. Some also pursue a broader ideological goal of unity among elven races under drow leadership, though this is generally secondary to survival and long-term positioning.

Unlike many drow priesthoods, Vhaeraun’s clergy are not centralized. They operate in autonomous cells, communicating through secret signs, coded messages, and rare trusted intermediaries.

## The Athanor (Gnome Pantheon)
The gnomish pantheon, formally known as the Athanor, is a closely interwoven fellowship of gods concerned with the hidden structure of the world. Unlike the pantheons of more hierarchical peoples, the Athanor is not defined by rigid authority or cosmic law, but by overlapping domains of observation, transformation, and survival through understanding.

Gnomish myth holds that Garl Glittergold first uncovered the gnomes within a hidden cavern of living crystal, where stone, mineral, and latent magic formed a system waiting to be understood rather than conquered. His act of discovery—opening, examining, and refining what already existed—became the model for all gnomish religious thought. Creation, in this sense, is not the imposition of will upon the world, but the revelation of what the world is already doing beneath the surface.

From this perspective, the Athanor is less a family of gods than a collection of investigative principles given will and personality. Each deity represents a particular mode of understanding: through craft, through concealment, through observation of nature, through disciplined survival, or through experimental invention.

#### Garl Glittergold and the Shape of Gnomish Faith

Garl Glittergold stands at the center of the Athanor as the primary point of reference rather than absolute ruler. Most gnomes acknowledge his pre-eminence, but more importantly, they see him as the clearest expression of gnomish cognition: curiosity refined into insight, and insight expressed through controlled action.

Gnomish religion does not treat humour as entertainment alone, but as a cognitive tool. Misdirection, irony, and well-timed absurdity are ways of revealing assumptions in how others interpret reality. A joke that “works” is one that exposes structure, error, or hidden expectation.

Craft, in this context, is not simply production. It is investigation through transformation. A mechanism is valuable not only for what it does, but for what it reveals about material behaviour, interaction, and failure states.

Gnomish faith therefore places equal weight on success and controlled failure. Both are sources of knowledge.

#### The Trick of Kurtulmak

The tale of Garl and Kurtulmak remains one of the defining expressions of gnomish mythic thought. In it, deception is not presented as chaos or cruelty, but as structured misalignment of expectation—used to expose weakness, impatience, and overconfidence.

Garl’s escape is less important than the method: observation of his captor, exploitation of narrative structure, and the use of competing interests to destabilize a hostile system. Gnomish tradition interprets this not as a victory of wit over strength alone, but of understanding over assumption.

#### Companions and Fellowship

A defining feature of gnomish mythology is the constant presence of companions. Garl is rarely depicted alone; his axe Arumdina is both weapon and confidant. Other deities likewise travel with animal companions, spirits, or enchanted constructs. For example, Baervan Wildwanderer is accompanied by his raccoon companion Chiktikka, and Segojan Earthcaller is often associated with crafted guardians and living stone.

Heroic gnomish tales mirror this pattern. No significant journey is undertaken in isolation. Instead, success depends on cooperation, shared insight, and the willingness to accept help from unlikely sources. This reflects a central cultural belief: wisdom is distributed, and even the smallest companion may hold the key to survival.

#### Nature of the Gnomish Gods

The gnomish deities are almost exclusively male in mythic tradition, though this carries little cultural weight in terms of authority or hierarchy. The Athanor is not structured around dominance, lineage, or cosmic kingship, but around specialization and shared curiosity. Its gods are generally portrayed as approachable, practical, and engaged with the material and perceptible world rather than distant or abstracted from it.

Unlike dwarven gods, who are often envisioned as clan-lords of distinct realms and domains, the gods of the Athanor are less territorially defined. Their distinctions are conceptual rather than geographic: different ways of observing, testing, and interacting with the same underlying reality. While they may be associated with particular planes or mythic locations, gnomish tradition places greater emphasis on their methods than their domains.

Unlike elven gods, they are not remote embodiments of idealized beauty or cosmic principles. Instead, they are active participants in processes of discovery, transformation, and survival, often depicted as working alongside or through their followers rather than apart from them.

The concerns of the Athanor commonly include:

- Craft, transformation, and experimental workmanship
- Subterranean systems, surface ecology, and hidden natural processes
- Protection of communities through vigilance, concealment, or preparedness
- Healing, adaptation, and preservation of life under changing conditions
- Small-scale natural complexity: burrows, roots, mineral growth, and living systems
- Perception, misdirection, and the controlled use of illusion or ambiguity
- Invention, refinement, and the systematic testing of ideas through failure and iteration

What unifies these concerns is not subject matter, but attention to structure: how things function, how they fail, and how understanding can be derived from careful interaction with them.

Gnomish deities rarely concern themselves with conquest, rigid law, grand cosmic order, or large-scale destruction. Even when war or conflict is present in their myths, it is typically framed in terms of system integrity, protection of small communities, or the correction of imbalance rather than expansion or domination.

Similarly, concepts such as agriculture, rulership, and large-scale empire-building are largely absent from their divine focus, except where they intersect with engineering, survival, or hidden processes. Even trickery and humor, where present, are usually treated as tools of perception—methods for revealing assumptions, testing understanding, or exposing error rather than ends in themselves.

The Athanor is therefore best understood not as a court of gods, but as a distributed model of gnomish thought: each deity representing a different disciplined way of noticing, testing, and refining the hidden workings of the world.

#### Relations Within the Athanor
The gods of the gnomes are united by their concern for understanding and survival within complex systems, but they are not without tension. Gnomish priests often describe the Athanor as a fellowship of specialists: bound by shared purpose, yet divided by method, emphasis, and interpretation.

Garl Glittergold stands at the center of the pantheon as first among equals, and most gnomish faiths acknowledge his interpretive authority even when they focus on narrower domains. His clergy often act as mediators between differing traditions, not by enforcing doctrine, but by reframing conflict as complementary perspective.

Baervan Wildwanderer and Segojan Earthcaller are closely aligned in their study of natural systems, though they differ in scale and focus: Baervan observing surface ecology and movement, Segojan studying subterranean structure, pressure, and burrowing life. Their followers often cooperate in mapping and environmental understanding.

Flandal Steelskin and Nebelun share a focus on invention and transformation, but differ in methodology. Flandal emphasizes refinement, durability, and controlled process, while Nebelun treats instability, error, and iteration as essential tools of discovery.

Baravar Cloakshadow and Gaerdal Ironhand both protect gnomish communities, but through different models of system defense. Baravar relies on concealment, misinformation, and pre-emptive disruption of hostile perception, while Gaerdal relies on vigilance, structural defense, and overt preparedness. Their followers often cooperate under pressure despite philosophical disagreement.

Callarduran Smoothhands is respected by all gnomish traditions but remains narrowly focused on survival within the Underdark. His clergy are seen as specialists in extreme environmental conditions rather than general authorities.

Urdlen stands outside the Athanor’s interpretive structure. Where the other gods represent methods of understanding systems, Urdlen represents their collapse—when observation becomes obsession, craft becomes destruction, and curiosity becomes consumption without comprehension.

#### Priests and Worship

Gnomish priests are not separated from experimentation, craft, or inquiry. Religious practice is integrated into daily activity: mining, tinkering, mapping, observing, and testing are all forms of worship when done with awareness.

Temples are therefore rarely isolated structures. They are workshops, observatories, mines, laboratories, and communal spaces of shared work. Ritual is often indistinguishable from practice.

Worship among gnomes is seldom exclusive. Individuals commonly align themselves with multiple aspects of the Athanor depending on profession and circumstance, reflecting the belief that no single method of understanding is sufficient on its own.

The Athanor is thus not a hierarchy of divine authority, but a model of gnomish thought itself: distributed, experimental, and constantly refining its understanding of the hidden mechanisms of the world.

### Garl Glittergold (Greater God)
Garl Glittergold is the creator and chief deity of the gnomish peoples, revered as the patron of wit, protection, craft, humor, and subtle understanding. To gnomes, Garl represents the belief that intelligence and perception are greater strengths than brute force, and that survival depends as much upon adaptability, curiosity, and social insight as upon weapons or walls.

Gnomish traditions portray Garl as endlessly inquisitive, difficult to deceive, and almost impossible to intimidate. Many stories describe him overcoming stronger foes through illusion, misdirection, or careful manipulation of circumstance rather than direct confrontation. Such tales are not merely entertainment within gnomish culture, but moral instruction. They reinforce the belief that arrogance, rigidity, and excessive seriousness make one vulnerable, while awareness and flexibility preserve both individuals and communities.

Unlike many racial creator gods, Garl is viewed as unusually attentive to the daily lives of his people. Gnomish religion teaches that the gods watch closely over mortal affairs, sending signs, coincidences, subtle warnings, or moments of embarrassment to redirect followers away from foolishness, conflict, or pride. This belief contributes to the deeply practical and socially observant character of gnomish culture.

Though strongly associated with humor and trickery, Garl is not a frivolous deity. In gnomish thought, humor serves social and intellectual purposes: defusing tension, exposing pride, testing perception, and revealing hidden truths indirectly. A clever joke, subtle illusion, or carefully timed prank may be regarded as both entertainment and instruction.

Garl is also associated with gemcutting, smithing, and skilled craftwork, though less as acts of industry than as forms of understanding. His faith teaches that making and examining things reveals the hidden structures underlying the world itself.

His worship is widespread among nearly all non-evil gnomes, particularly rock gnomes. Shrines to Garl are often integrated into workshops, communal halls, libraries, or shared gathering spaces rather than isolated temples. Gold, polished stone, reflective metals, intricate carvings, and subtle visual illusions are common features in his holy places.

#### Roleplaying Followers of Garl Glittergold
Followers of Garl value curiosity, adaptability, patience, and social intelligence. They tend to distrust excessive pride, rigid thinking, and displays of brute authority. Many believe problems are best solved indirectly whenever possible, and often use humor, misdirection, or careful observation to navigate difficult situations.

His worshippers commonly include artisans, illusionists, scouts, gemcutters, storytellers, inventors, negotiators, and teachers. Even among adventurers, followers of Garl are often more interested in understanding a situation than dominating it directly.

His followers tend to:

- Value wit and perception over physical intimidation
- Use humor to ease tension or expose foolishness
- Prefer clever solutions to direct confrontation
- Maintain curiosity about unfamiliar people, objects, or systems
- Distrust arrogance and unnecessary displays of power
- Treat craftsmanship as a form of understanding rather than mere labor
- View cooperation and mutual reliance as essential virtues

Many also enjoy practical jokes and subtle deceptions, though within gnomish culture such acts are generally expected to reveal insight, encourage humility, or strengthen social bonds rather than simply humiliate others.

#### Priests of Garl Glittergold

Priests of Garl serve as teachers, mediators, protectors, artisans, and keepers of communal knowledge. They are expected to remain actively involved in ordinary gnomish life rather than separating themselves from it. Most continue to practice some form of craftwork throughout their lives, commonly gemcutting, smithing, engraving, alchemy, or mechanical tinkering.

Education is regarded as one of the priesthood’s most important duties. Young gnomes are taught through stories, demonstrations, puzzles, and carefully constructed mistakes intended to develop independent thought rather than obedience alone. Humor is commonly employed as a teaching tool, though usually with deliberate purpose.

Priests are also expected to monitor tensions within gnomish communities and quietly intervene before disputes become destructive. Open authority is generally avoided when subtler methods will suffice. Many priests cultivate skills associated with illusion, stealth, observation, or negotiation for this reason.

The clergy maintains careful vigilance against kobolds, goblinoids, and hostile Underdark forces, all of whom appear frequently in gnomish religious traditions as examples of cruelty, tyranny, or destructive cunning untempered by wisdom.

### Baervan Wildwanderer (Intermediate God)
Baervan Wildwanderer is the gnomish god of forests, travel, woodland communities, and the quiet mischief of the natural world. Among the gnomish pantheon, he is closely associated with forest gnomes, wandering hunters, scouts, herbalists, and those who prefer life beneath the boughs of ancient trees rather than within burrows or workshops. Though loyal to Garl Glittergold and the gnomish people as a whole, Baervan is markedly less formal than many other gnomish deities, embodying curiosity, freedom, and harmony with the living world.

Gnomish folklore portrays Baervan as a tireless wanderer who travels hidden woodland paths accompanied by animal companions and forest spirits. Tales of his exploits often involve embarrassing cruel hunters, outwitting monsters, rescuing lost travelers, or humbling arrogant intruders through harmless but memorable tricks. Unlike harsher nature gods, Baervan rarely seeks vengeance for its own sake. Instead, he prefers to confuse, redirect, or discourage those who threaten the balance of the forest.

Baervan maintains close friendships with many sylvan races and deities, and his worship encourages peaceful relations with good-aligned woodland beings whenever possible. Forest animals are regarded as favored creatures under his protection, particularly raccoons, which appear frequently in stories and religious symbolism. Ancient oak trees are especially sacred to his faith, symbolizing endurance, shelter, and the quiet wisdom of the natural world.

Though lighthearted in temperament, Baervan is fiercely protective of natural places and gnomish woodland communities. His myths often emphasize that the forests shelter the small and overlooked from larger and more destructive powers. Evil creatures that despoil nature or prey upon peaceful woodland folk are treated as enemies to be driven away without hesitation.

Shrines to Baervan are usually modest outdoor sanctuaries rather than formal temples. Sacred groves, hollow trees, woodland springs, and hidden clearings commonly serve as places of worship. His holy symbol, the raccoon’s face, represents cleverness, adaptability, curiosity, and skill in surviving unnoticed.

#### Roleplaying Followers of Baervan

Followers of Baervan tend to value freedom, humor, adaptability, and harmony with nature. They are often more relaxed and less rigidly structured than many other gnomes, preferring practical cooperation and good-hearted cleverness over strict authority or formal hierarchy.

Many worshippers are scouts, foresters, hunters, herbalists, wanderers, guides, druids, trappers, explorers, or protectors of woodland communities. Others simply feel more comfortable beneath trees than within crowded settlements.

His followers tend to:
- Respect forests and natural places as living sanctuaries
- Prefer misdirection and cleverness over direct confrontation
- Protect small communities and vulnerable creatures
- Enjoy harmless pranks and practical jokes
- Value personal freedom and wandering
- Distrust needless destruction of nature

Though generally good-natured, followers of Baervan can become surprisingly serious when confronting those who exploit forests recklessly or prey upon peaceful woodland folk.

#### Priests of Baervan

Priests of Baervan serve as wardens, guides, scouts, healers, and protectors of forest gnome communities. Their duties resemble those of druids in many respects, though they place greater emphasis upon defending gnomish settlements and maintaining ties between woodland peoples.

Baervan’s clergy are expected to safeguard forests from destructive monsters, unnatural corruption, and reckless exploitation. They frequently mediate disputes between woodland races, guide travelers safely through dangerous terrain, and preserve hidden paths, shrines, and sanctuaries known only to trusted allies.

Many priests maintain close bonds with animal companions, especially raccoons and other small woodland creatures. Temples are uncommon; instead, priests travel between scattered forest communities or dwell within hidden groves and natural sanctuaries.

### Baravar Cloakshadow (Lesser God)
Baravar Cloakshadow is the gnomish god of illusions, secrecy, deception, traps, and hidden defense. Among the gnomish pantheon, he represents the belief that cleverness and subtlety are often the only reliable protection available to small folk living beside larger and more aggressive powers. While other gnomish gods celebrate humor, craftsmanship, or community, Baravar embodies caution, vigilance, and the use of misdirection against dangerous enemies.

Gnomish folklore portrays Baravar as a shadowy wanderer who survives through wit rather than strength. His stories frequently involve goblinoids, bugbears, kobolds, or other enemies being lured into elaborate traps, deceived by convincing illusions, or manipulated into fighting one another while gnomes escape unharmed. Unlike Garl Glittergold, whose tricks are often playful or humiliating, Baravar’s deceptions are practical and sometimes ruthless. His pranks are intended to protect the People rather than simply entertain them.

Baravar bears a particular hatred toward goblinoid deities and their followers, whom he regards as perpetual threats to gnomish survival. Many myths depict him harassing goblin tribes long before they become dangerous, sabotaging their efforts through theft, illusion, and quiet sabotage. Among his faithful, there is a common saying that “an enemy confused is an enemy already half-defeated.”

Though associated with thievery, Baravar is not usually worshipped as a patron of greed or selfish criminality. Instead, theft is viewed pragmatically: stealing dangerous weapons, secret plans, magical tools, or strategic advantages from enemies is considered entirely honorable if it protects gnome communities. His faith places strong emphasis upon preparation, caution, and maintaining hidden advantages.

Temples and shrines to Baravar are often concealed behind illusions, hidden doors, false walls, or underground passages. Many double as safehouses, archives of magical research, or hidden refuges during times of danger. His holy symbol, the cloak and dagger, represents secrecy, vigilance, and concealed protection.

#### Roleplaying Followers of Baravar
Followers of Baravar value caution, intelligence, preparation, and subtlety. They tend to distrust direct confrontation when clever alternatives exist, believing survival depends upon using one’s mind rather than competing physically with stronger foes.

Many worshippers are illusionists, scouts, spies, investigators, burglars, trappers, infiltrators, or guardians of hidden settlements. Others simply believe dangerous enemies should never be given a fair opportunity to strike first.

His followers tend to:

- Prefer deception and ambush over open battle
- Value secrecy and hidden knowledge
- Distrust displays of arrogance or overconfidence
- Use illusions and trickery defensively
- Maintain hidden escape routes, caches, and safehouses
- Treat vigilance as a necessary virtue

Though often suspicious or secretive around outsiders, followers of Baravar are usually deeply loyal to their communities and allies. Many see themselves as protectors willing to do uncomfortable or morally ambiguous work so others may live safely.

#### Priests of Baravar
Priests of Baravar serve as spies, scouts, magical researchers, investigators, and hidden defenders of gnomish communities. They are expected to travel widely, gather intelligence on potential threats, and develop new magical techniques involving illusion, concealment, and deception.

Many members of the clergy are multiclass illusionists or maintain extensive knowledge of magical traps, disguises, and infiltration techniques. Their temples commonly function as hidden refuges or intelligence centers where dangerous information, magical secrets, and emergency supplies are concealed from enemies.

Baravar’s clergy are particularly active against goblinoids, kobolds, and hostile underground races. They often sabotage enemy movements before open conflict begins, believing prevention is preferable to costly warfare.

### Flandal Steelskin (Intermediate God)
Flandal Steelskin is the gnomish god of mining, smithing, metalcraft, and physical endurance. Among the gnomish pantheon, he is revered as the master craftsman who taught the People how to shape metal drawn from the deep earth into tools, weapons, machines, and works of lasting skill. Though less jovial than Garl Glittergold, Flandal embodies many of the practical virtues most admired among gnomes: diligence, ingenuity, resilience, and pride in honest craftsmanship.

Gnomish myths describe Flandal as both artisan and warrior, equally comfortable at the forge or upon the battlefield. Tales often portray him forging impossible devices, recovering rare ores from dangerous depths, or bargaining with elemental powers of fire and earth for secret knowledge of metalworking. Tradition holds that he aided in the forging of Arumdina, Garl’s legendary battle axe, and for this reason his counsel is considered especially valued among the gnomish gods.

Unlike dwarven smith deities, whose teachings often emphasize permanence and solemn duty, Flandal’s faith celebrates invention and adaptability alongside craftsmanship. His followers believe tools and devices should not merely endure, but improve the lives and defenses of the People through clever design and practical innovation.

Mining and smithing are regarded as sacred responsibilities within his faith. Ore drawn from the earth is considered a gift requiring skill and respect to shape properly. Flandal is also associated with physical training and endurance, teaching that the body must be strengthened alongside the craftsperson’s skill if difficult labor is to be mastered safely.

His worship is especially common among miners, blacksmiths, engineers, armorers, metalworkers, locksmiths, and inventors. Temples to Flandal are usually built around active forges or workshops, where prayer and labor occur side by side. Sacred flames are maintained continuously whenever possible, and many rituals involve the heating, shaping, or tempering of metal.

Fire is strongly associated with Flandal’s faith, though it is viewed as a tool to be mastered rather than a destructive force. Omens attributed to him often involve unusual activity within hearths or forges: sudden bursts of flame, sparks behaving unnaturally, or glowing heat appearing where no fire should exist. His holy symbol, the flaming hammer, represents both creation through labor and strength through endurance.

#### Roleplaying Followers of Flandal

Followers of Flandal value hard work, practical skill, physical resilience, and craftsmanship. They tend to judge others by competence and effort rather than status or appearance, and often become frustrated with laziness, wastefulness, or poorly maintained equipment.

Many worshippers are smiths, miners, engineers, armorers, builders, metalworkers, or adventurers who maintain their own gear personally. Others simply believe that labor itself is honorable when done skillfully and honestly.

His followers tend to:

- Respect practical skill over empty theory
- Treat tools and crafted objects with care
- Maintain strong physical conditioning
- Value invention and technical problem-solving
- Regard mining and smithing as sacred labors
- Distrust wastefulness and poor workmanship

Though usually good-natured, followers of Flandal can become stubborn or highly competitive regarding craftsmanship, especially when their skill is challenged.

#### Priests of Flandal

Priests of Flandal serve as smiths, engineers, mining overseers, artisans, and protectors of gnomish industry. They are expected to master practical crafts alongside their religious duties and commonly spend as much time working at forges or within mines as conducting ceremonies.

The clergy oversee mine safety, train apprentices, recover new ore deposits, and maintain relations with creatures or powers associated with fire and the deep earth. Many priests become experts in underground navigation, metallurgy, and engineering.

Unlike many priesthoods, Flandal’s clergy place strong emphasis upon physical conditioning. Strength and endurance are regarded as essential virtues for those who labor beneath the earth or work long hours at the forge. Among outsiders, the sight of intensely determined gnomes engaging in strenuous physical training is sometimes considered unintentionally comical, though rarely by the priests themselves.

### Gaerdal Ironhand (Lesser God)

Gaerdal Ironhand is the stern guardian of the gnomish pantheon, patron of vigilance, defense, discipline, and righteous combat. Among the gods of the gnomes, he is unusual for his severity. Where Garl delights in wit and clever humiliation, and Baravar embraces deception and trickery, Gaerdal values preparedness, duty, and direct protection of the People against their enemies.

Gnomish tales often portray Gaerdal as the immovable shield standing beside the laughter of Garl. Though he rarely participates in the practical jokes and merriment beloved by other gnomish deities, he is respected for his unwavering dedication to the safety of gnome communities. Stories describe him standing watch through endless nights beneath the hills, hammer in hand, while other gods feast or celebrate.

Gaerdal teaches that humor and cleverness are valuable only when protected by vigilance and strength. He distrusts recklessness, unnecessary deception, and complacency, believing that the enemies of gnomekind—particularly goblinoids and subterranean threats—must never be underestimated. His worship therefore emphasizes discipline, readiness, and the defense of hearth and settlement.

Despite his stern reputation, Gaerdal is not cruel or joyless. His followers believe his seriousness reflects deep concern for the survival of the People rather than contempt for happiness. Many gnomes claim that Gaerdal secretly appreciates humor in private, though no reliable witness has ever confirmed this.

Small statues or engraved iron markers dedicated to Gaerdal are commonly placed near gates, tunnels, bridges, and defensive walls in gnomish settlements. Such symbols are believed to warn of danger and strengthen the resolve of defenders.

#### Roleplaying Followers of Gaerdal

Followers of Gaerdal tend to be disciplined, practical, and protective. They value preparedness, reliability, and moral responsibility, often acting as defenders, guards, militia leaders, judges, or organizers within their communities.

Many worshippers are less outwardly whimsical than other gnomes, though they usually retain the race’s characteristic warmth and intelligence beneath their serious demeanor. They are often frustrated by irresponsible behavior, poorly planned schemes, or trickery that places others at risk.

His followers tend to:

- Value vigilance and preparation
- Protect vulnerable communities and companions
- Distrust needless deception or recklessness
- Approach danger cautiously and methodically
- Respect courage tempered by discipline
- Believe humor should never undermine safety or duty

Followers of Gaerdal frequently take personal responsibility for the safety of others and may become deeply troubled by failures of protection or leadership.

#### Priests of Gaerdal

Priests of Gaerdal serve as defenders, wardens, judges, military organizers, and guardians of gnomish settlements. Many are fighter/clerics who train regularly in combat and defensive tactics, and are expected to remain physically prepared for conflict at all times.

Their clergy oversee watches, fortifications, tunnel defenses, militia drills, and emergency preparations in gnomish communities. They are particularly vigilant against goblinoids, kobolds, and other traditional enemies of gnomekind.

Unlike many gnomish priesthoods, Gaerdal’s clergy place relatively little value upon pranks, illusion, or elaborate trickery. Many openly distrust the followers of Baravar Cloakshadow, believing excessive reliance upon deceit encourages carelessness and moral compromise.

Temples of Gaerdal are typically austere by gnomish standards, often doubling as guardhouses, armories, or fortified shrines. Defensive architecture and practical engineering are considered sacred expressions of devotion.

### Nebelun, the Meddler (Lesser God)
Nebelun is the gnomish patron of invention, experimentation, improvisation, and inspired problem-solving. Among the gnomish gods, he represents the belief that understanding emerges through curiosity, trial, failure, and adaptation rather than through rigid tradition or established authority. His followers regard mistakes not as shameful, but as necessary steps toward insight.

Gnomish stories portray Nebelun as endlessly occupied with new devices, mechanisms, theories, and improbable schemes. Few of his creations function exactly as intended, yet many produce unexpected discoveries or reveal useful principles through their failures. For this reason, he is closely associated with ingenuity, accidental innovation, and the productive value of experimentation.

Unlike more disciplined deities such as Flandal Steelskin or Gaerdal Ironhand, Nebelun encourages flexible thinking and unconventional solutions. His worship appeals particularly to inventors, alchemists, engineers, artificers, locksmiths, puzzle-makers, and gnomes fascinated by unusual systems or mechanical complexity.

Though often associated with humor and mischief, Nebelun’s faith treats these qualities as expressions of curiosity rather than chaos. Clever disruption, unexpected approaches, and practical improvisation are valued because they expose hidden assumptions and encourage adaptation. His clergy commonly teach that a failed experiment may still contain useful knowledge if examined carefully.

Shrines to Nebelun are often attached to workshops, laboratories, observatories, or cluttered communal spaces filled with unfinished devices, diagrams, notes, and half-disassembled mechanisms. Such places are usually noisy, active, and perpetually under construction.

#### Roleplaying Followers of Nebelun
Followers of Nebelun tend to be inquisitive, inventive, and intellectually restless. They are often drawn toward unfamiliar systems, unusual tools, obscure mechanisms, or problems others consider impractical or unsolvable.

His followers tend to:

- Treat failure as part of learning
- Improvise readily under pressure
- Pursue unusual or unconventional solutions
- Collect tools, diagrams, mechanisms, or prototypes

Followers of Nebelun are often patient with accidents and mistakes, but may become frustrated by excessive caution, intellectual stagnation, or refusal to question established methods.

#### Priests of Nebelun

Priests of Nebelun serve as inventors, researchers, engineers, experimental craftsmen, and keepers of technical knowledge. Their temples function partly as religious centers and partly as communal workshops where experimentation and collaborative invention are encouraged.

Unlike many priesthoods, Nebelun’s clergy are expected to actively pursue new ideas rather than preserve established practices unchanged. Experimentation itself is considered a form of devotion, particularly when it expands understanding or improves the lives of gnome communities.

Many priests travel widely in search of unusual materials, forgotten mechanisms, rare knowledge, or unfamiliar technologies. Adventuring clergy often become explorers, dungeon delvers, or wandering troubleshooters motivated as much by curiosity as by duty.

### Segojan Earthcaller (Intermediate God)

Segojan is also associated with communication between gnomes and burrowing animals, a gift that reflects the gnomish belief that even small and overlooked forms of life can provide essential insight into the structure of the world. His relationship with the svirfnebli (deep gnomes) is particularly significant. Segojan is seen as a bridge between surface gnomes and their deep-dwelling kin, encouraging cautious cooperation where mutual survival depends on shared knowledge of the Underdark.

Temples and shrines dedicated to Segojan are typically integrated into natural cave systems or carefully excavated chambers that preserve geological features rather than obscuring them. Smooth stone, mineral formations, and preserved root structures are treated as sacred elements of the space rather than decoration.

#### Cultural and Religious Context

Segojan’s worship reflects a gnomish understanding of the subterranean world as structured, intelligible, and responsive to careful study. His faith emphasizes observation, patience, and the interpretation of subtle environmental change rather than forceful intervention.

Unlike more doctrinally complex faiths, Segojan’s religion is notably pragmatic. Communication from the deity is understood as direct and unambiguous, often interpreted through environmental cues or the behavior of subterranean wildlife rather than formal revelation or elaborate ritual symbolism.

Segojan’s faithful generally avoid unnecessary disruption of underground environments. Reckless excavation, collapse-inducing methods, or disregard for subterranean life are viewed as both dangerous and disrespectful.

### Urdlen, the Burrower Below (Intermediate God)

Urdlen is the embodiment of subterranean malice, destructive impulse, and instinctive cruelty turned inward upon the gnomish people. Within gnomish religious understanding, he represents not a balanced aspect of nature or society, but a corruption of subterranean existence—where curiosity becomes compulsion, and exploration becomes destruction.

Unlike other gnomish deities, Urdlen is not integrated into any constructive cultural role. He is treated as an anomaly within the moral and spiritual structure of gnomish belief: a presence that reflects what the gnomish psyche fears most in itself—unchecked greed, violence, and the abandonment of communal responsibility.

Gnomes do not preserve coherent myths explaining his origin. Instead, he is understood as something that exists outside the normal moral and cultural development of the People. His influence is associated with irrational sabotage, sudden cruelty, and the destruction of carefully maintained structures—both physical and social.

Urdlen is especially feared because his corruption is not always external. Gnomish tradition holds that his influence can emerge subtly within individuals, expressed as obsessive greed, destructive curiosity, or hostility toward communal order. For this reason, his presence is treated as both an external threat and an internal danger.

His worship is universally condemned in mainstream gnomish society, and even mentioning his name in certain communities is considered dangerous or taboo.

#### Cultural and Religious Context

Urdlen’s “faith,” where it exists, is not structured religion so much as a collection of secretive, unstable cults formed around greed, resentment, or destructive impulse. These groups tend to fragment quickly, as internal trust is minimal and cohesion is maintained only through fear or coercion.

Within gnomish society, Urdlen is associated with:

- Sabotage of mining, engineering, and crafted systems
- Violent or obsessive greed directed at subterranean resources
- Corruption of trust within tightly connected communities
- Undermining of tunnels, foundations, and carefully built structures
- Psychological instability expressed through violence or hoarding behavior

His influence is most often discussed in practical rather than theological terms. Miners, engineers, and tunnel workers may describe “Urdlen’s touch” as unexplained collapses, sudden structural failures, or inexplicable acts of destructive negligence.

Shrines are not recognized or sanctioned in any formal sense. Where they exist, they are hidden, crude, and often associated with abandoned tunnels, collapsed chambers, or sites of prior disaster.

#### Roleplaying Followers of Urdlen

Followers of Urdlen are defined less by doctrine than by behavior patterns centered on compulsion, mistrust, and destructive fixation. They tend to reject communal responsibility and instead prioritize acquisition, domination, or the expression of internal instability.

Common traits include:

- Obsessive attachment to wealth, gems, or subterranean resources
- Willingness to sabotage shared efforts for personal gain or satisfaction
- Distrust of gnomish social structures and cooperative systems
- Preference for secrecy and isolation
- Impulsive or emotionally driven decision-making
- Rationalization of harm as necessity or entitlement

Many identified followers are not formally religious in a conventional sense, but individuals whose behavior aligns with the destructive impulses attributed to Urdlen’s influence. For this reason, accusations of his worship are often socially and politically charged within gnomish communities.

#### Priests of Urdlen

Those who serve Urdlen act as agents of destruction, corruption, and destabilization within gnomish society. Their activities frequently target mining operations, communal resource distribution, and cooperative engineering efforts, where they introduce failure, mistrust, or direct sabotage.

Ritual practice centers on the symbolic rejection of communal value systems. Wealth is often destroyed, buried, or rendered unusable, reflecting a belief that possession is more meaningful than use or exchange. Violence is frequently framed as purification or reclamation.

Priests of Urdlen are typically opposed by nearly all other gnomish religious traditions, and are treated as enemies of social order rather than merely theological dissenters.

## Yondalla’s Children (Halfling Pantheon)

The halfling pantheon is small, close-knit, and deeply communal. Its deities are often referred to collectively as “Yondalla’s Children,” a name used interchangeably for the gods themselves, the halfling people, and in some traditions even the priesthood. Halfling religion places exceptional emphasis upon kinship, fellowship, continuity, and the sanctity of home, and these values are reflected in every aspect of their theology.

At the head of the pantheon stands Yondalla, the Protector and Provider, revered as mother of the halfling race. Halfling myths differ in the details of creation, but all agree that Yondalla fashioned the People through acts of care, cultivation, and belonging rather than conquest or craft. Some tales claim she gave birth to the first halflings directly, while others say she shaped them from fertile earth, tended fields, and warm hearth-fire. A few rare traditions tell of Yondalla transforming a lonely and forgotten sylvan creature into the first halfling, creating a people for herself as much as for the mortal she uplifted.

Whatever the tale, halflings view their connection to Yondalla as intimate and immediate. To them, the goddess is not distant or abstract but present in every meal shared, every field maintained, every friendship preserved, and every hearth kept warm against the dark.

#### The Gift of the Hearthlands
Halfling myths hold that Yondalla secured the fertile lands of the world for her children through wisdom, patience, and persuasion rather than force. She praised Moradin so lavishly for his mastery of metal that he withdrew deep beneath the mountains in search of richer veins. She intrigued Garl Glittergold with tales of hidden gems and buried wonders, sending the gnomes below the hills. With Corellon Larethian she negotiated peaceful division of the green places between elves and halflings alike. Humans, she supposedly observed, were adaptable enough to flourish anywhere, and thus claimed no singular homeland at all.

These myths reveal a central halfling ideal: stability and prosperity are best secured through accommodation, careful stewardship, and the avoidance of unnecessary conflict. Halflings admire those who preserve peace and continuity without domination or bloodshed whenever possible.

#### Yondalla’s Mercy and Wrath
Yondalla is regarded as the source of every blessing cherished by halflings—comfort, friendship, abundance, peace, and security. Few deities are loved with such uncomplicated devotion by their people. Yet halfling myths also stress that beneath the goddess’s generosity lies terrible power.

As giver of fertility and growth, Yondalla may also withdraw those gifts. When halfling homes or communities are threatened, she becomes merciless. Tales describe barren fields, withering orchards, sudden age, and wasting sickness inflicted upon invaders who sought to despoil halfling lands. This duality is reflected among halflings themselves, who are famously peaceable until cornered. When defending home, kin, or the continuity of their way of life, they are capable of astonishing resolve and ferocity.

To halflings, this is not contradiction but sacred duty. Home is not merely shelter, but the foundation of a whole and enduring life.

#### Fellowship and Shared Fortune
Halfling hero tales rarely celebrate solitary greatness. Their champions succeed because of generosity shown earlier in the story: a kindness offered to a stranger, shelter shared with a traveller, or aid given without expectation of reward. In moments of greatest danger, these relationships and obligations return to preserve the hero from ruin.

Thus halfling religion teaches that trust, hospitality, and mutual reliance are practical virtues as much as moral ones. “A happy home has happy neighbours” is among the most common sayings attributed to halfling priests.

The halfling gods themselves reflect this unity. Their responsibilities often overlap, and they are frequently depicted travelling together or acting in concert. Even Brandobaris, patron of wanderers, luck, and mischief, readily aids the other gods when called upon. Halfling myths contain little divine rivalry or jealousy; cooperation and coexistence are regarded as the natural order of things.

#### Nature of the Halfling Gods
Most major halfling deities are female, especially those governing the central concerns of halfling life: hearth, kinship, agriculture, trust, and continuity. Sheela Peryroyl, goddess of weather, plants, and rural wisdom, is second only to Yondalla in affection among the People.

The halfling pantheon concerns itself with:

- Home, hearth, and family
- Friendship, trust, and fellowship
- Agriculture, gardens, and cultivated lands
- Peace, security, and mutual aid
- Youth, play, humor, and good fortune
- Hospitality, continuity, and shared abundance

Halflings possess no gods of cruelty, conquest, or destructive ambition. Even Arvoreen, their closest equivalent to a war god, concerns himself solely with defense, vigilance, and protection rather than aggression. Likewise, halfling religion places little emphasis upon arcane lore, occult mysteries, or grand works of artifice. Such pursuits are viewed as secondary to the preservation of community, stability, and everyday well-being.

#### Relations Among Yondalla’s Children
The halfling gods are understood as a close, familial pantheon rather than a strict hierarchy. Halfling faith emphasizes cooperation and shared purpose over doctrine or rivalry, and the deities are often described as kin whose domains overlap naturally in service of halfling life.

Yondalla stands at the center as Protector and Provider, and while her authority is widely acknowledged, it is not seen as absolute command over the other gods. Instead, she is understood as the source of stability from which the others extend more focused aspects of halfling experience.

Cyrrollalee and Sheela Peryroyl are most closely aligned with Yondalla’s day-to-day influence, and their clergy work in frequent cooperation regarding homes, families, and agriculture. Their spheres overlap so extensively that distinctions between them are often practical rather than theological.

Arvoreen’s followers maintain a more disciplined and security-focused outlook, sometimes in tension with the more communal or adaptive instincts of Cyrrollalee’s and Sheela’s clergy. Nevertheless, he is fully accepted as an essential expression of Yondalla’s protective aspect when stability is under threat.

Brandobaris is viewed with a mix of fondness and caution. His clergy are useful and often effective, but rarely entrusted with authority, as his influence encourages risk and disruption even when ultimately harmless. He is tolerated as a necessary counterbalance to over-caution.

Urogalan is respected but distant, acknowledged as the inevitable end of life and the guardian of what lies beneath it. His priests are granted authority in matters of burial and foundation, but remain outside the rhythm of everyday halfling worship.

Overall, tensions within the pantheon are mild and practical rather than ideological. Differences between the gods are understood as differences in emphasis rather than conflict, and halfling clergy are expected to cooperate regardless of patron. The pantheon functions as a single interdependent system: home, protection, growth, trust, risk, and remembrance held in careful balance.

#### Priests and Worship

Halfling priests are expected to place the welfare and continuity of their communities above all else. Unlike many clergy of other races, they rarely wander for long periods, preferring instead to serve as guardians, mediators, healers, and keepers of local custom within their homelands.

All priests of the halfling gods are expected to maintain close friendship with one another regardless of differing patron deities. Hospitality between temples is considered sacred obligation, and any halfling priest in need must be offered food, shelter, and aid without hesitation.

Halfling worship is informal, communal, and rooted in ordinary life. Festivals, shared meals, storytelling, planting feasts, weddings, seasonal migrations, and harvest gatherings all serve religious as well as social functions. To a halfling, a secure home, reliable companions, and a life lived in balance with one’s surroundings are among the clearest signs of divine favor.

### Yondalla (Greater Goddess)
Yondalla stands at the heart of the halfling pantheon as its Protector and Provider, and is widely regarded as the divine source of halfling continuity, safety, and prosperity. Among halflings, she is not treated as a remote sovereign but as an ever-present guardian of ordinary life—felt in the steadiness of a well-tended home, the safety of familiar roads, and the quiet assurance that a community will endure beyond a single generation.

She represents two inseparable truths in halfling thought: that life must be sustained, and that what is sustained must also be defended. Her authority is not expressed through domination or conquest, but through care, vigilance, and the capacity to preserve what is already whole. Halflings understand her not as a distant judge, but as the divine expression of belonging itself—life at its most stable and secure.

#### Cultural and Religious Context
Yondalla governs the foundational aspects of halfling life: home, family, fertility, protection, and the continuity of settled communities. Her influence is understood to extend into both the physical and social fabric of halfling existence—fields that grow reliably, homes that remain safe, and kinship networks that hold together under strain are all seen as signs of her favor.

Worship of Yondalla is inseparable from daily life. Her reverence is expressed through maintenance rather than spectacle: repairing a roof before it leaks, ensuring neighbors are fed, keeping pathways clear, and maintaining harmony within a household or village. Major festivals in her honor are communal in nature, centered on shared meals, seasonal harvests, and the reaffirmation of kinship bonds.

Temples of Yondalla are typically modest structures or expanded homes rather than monumental buildings. Her clergy serve as anchors of community life—coordinating aid during hardship, mediating disputes, overseeing seasonal planning, and ensuring that no member of the community is left unsupported. Her worship is especially strong among settled agrarian halflings, though all subraces acknowledge her as the ultimate protector of halflingkind.

Yondalla’s relationship to other halfling deities is one of coordination rather than hierarchy. Her portfolio overlaps naturally with gods of agriculture, home, and protection, reflecting a pantheon that functions as an interdependent system rather than a set of competing divine authorities. She is understood to act first in support of stability, while allowing more specialized deities to address narrower concerns.

#### Roleplaying Followers of Yondalla
Followers of Yondalla tend to embody the values of stability, care, and practical responsibility. They are rarely abstract in their faith; instead, their devotion is measured through action within their immediate surroundings.

Typical behaviors and values include:

- Prioritizing the safety and well-being of family and community over personal ambition
- Maintaining homes, tools, and shared spaces in good repair
- Acting as mediators in disputes, especially within kin or village groups
- Preferring cooperation and compromise over confrontation
- Taking responsibility for those who are vulnerable or displaced
- Valuing continuity, tradition, and established social bonds

Even when adventuring, followers of Yondalla tend to frame their actions in terms of protection, preservation, or return—seeking not glory, but the restoration or safeguarding of something valued.

#### Priests of Yondalla
Priests of Yondalla serve as the primary stabilizing force within halfling communities. They function less as distant theological authorities and more as active participants in communal life, taking responsibility for the health, cohesion, and continuity of the people they serve.

Their duties commonly include:

- Mediating disputes and maintaining social harmony
- Coordinating care for the sick, young, and elderly
- Overseeing agricultural cycles and communal resource planning
- Blessing homes, families, and seasonal gatherings
- Guiding rites of passage such as births, marriages, and funerals
- Advising community leaders on matters of long-term stability

In times of crisis, Yondallan priests are expected to act decisively in defense of their community’s survival, often coordinating directly with local defenders or representatives of Arvoreen. However, their first response is typically preventive: maintaining cohesion and addressing problems before they escalate.

Among adventuring priests, motivation usually arises from the need to protect dispersed kin, restore endangered communities, or establish safe conditions for halfling settlement in unstable regions. Even when far from home, they tend to form surrogate communities wherever they travel.

### Arvoreen (Intermediate God)

Arvoreen, known as _the Defender_, is the primary divine patron of halfling vigilance, preparation, and martial protection. Within the halfling pantheon he represents the principle that peace is preserved not through aggression, but through readiness—the disciplined awareness that danger is always possible and must be anticipated rather than merely reacted to.

Halflings do not view Arvoreen as a god of conquest or war in the broader sense. Instead, he embodies _active defense_: the commitment to ensure that threats are identified early, confronted decisively, and prevented from spreading into the heart of halfling life. His concern is not glory in battle, but the survival of home, kin, and continuity under pressure.

#### Cultural and Religious Context

Arvoreen governs vigilance, defense, and organized resistance to intrusion. He is especially revered in communities that lie near dangerous frontiers, borderlands, or regions where halfling settlements exist alongside more volatile neighbors.

Worship of Arvoreen is practical and disciplined rather than ceremonial. His influence is most visible in the routines of daily life: maintaining watch schedules, reinforcing boundaries, training community defenders, and ensuring that escape routes and warning systems exist even in peaceful times. Shrines to Arvoreen are often placed at village edges, road junctions, and other transitional spaces where threats might first appear.

His clergy are closely integrated with local defense structures. They do not replace secular authority but instead reinforce it, providing training, coordination, and spiritual assurance that preparation is itself a moral obligation. Among halflings, this is not viewed as paranoia, but as responsible stewardship of safety.

Arvoreen’s role overlaps with Yondalla’s protective aspect, but where Yondalla preserves stability through cohesion and care, Arvoreen emphasizes readiness, structure, and immediate response. The two are understood as complementary forces: one maintaining the home, the other ensuring it cannot easily be broken.

#### Roleplaying Followers of Arvoreen
Followers of Arvoreen are defined by alertness, discipline, and a strong sense of responsibility for communal safety. They tend to see preparedness not as anxiety, but as an expression of care for others.

Common traits include:

- Maintaining awareness of surroundings and potential threats
- Preferring preparation and planning over improvisation in crises
- Taking responsibility for the safety of neighbors and kin
- Practicing or supporting defensive training and drills
- Valuing reliable equipment, clear communication, and contingency planning
- Treating complacency as a moral failing when others are at risk

Even outside combat, followers of Arvoreen tend to organize themselves and others efficiently, ensuring that groups are not caught unprepared or divided in dangerous situations.

#### Priests of Arvoreen
Priests of Arvoreen serve as coordinators of defense and preparedness within halfling society. They are not purely martial figures, but community organizers who ensure that halflings are never without structure when faced with uncertainty or threat.

Their responsibilities commonly include:

- Training local militias or defense groups
- Designing and maintaining warning systems and signal networks
- Constructing or advising on fortifications, barriers, and safe points of retreat
- Identifying risks to settlements and organizing preventative measures
- Coordinating responses during raids, disasters, or incursions
- Ensuring weapons and supplies are available and maintained

In many communities, Arvoreen’s priests are the first to respond to signs of danger and the last to stand down after a threat has passed. Their authority is typically respected even in peacetime, as they are seen as guardians of the conditions that allow peace to persist.

Adventuring priests often travel to eliminate emerging threats before they reach halfling lands, recover lost defensive knowledge or equipment, or assist distant halfling communities facing instability.

### Brandobaris (Lesser God)
Brandobaris occupies a distinctive place within the halfling pantheon as the patron of adventure, misadventure, and the unplanned consequences that follow from curiosity acted upon too quickly. He represents the halfling recognition that not all stability is preserved through caution alone; sometimes it is tested, disrupted, and ultimately reaffirmed through risk.

Within halfling religious thought, Brandobaris is not a moral counterweight to the more structured deities, but a necessary pressure within the system of communal life. He embodies the impulse to step beyond the familiar boundary, to test limits, and to discover whether preparation and good fortune can sustain one through uncertainty.

He is widely regarded as amiable rather than dangerous, though never entirely predictable. His stories consistently affirm a central cultural idea: that survival is often less about avoiding risk entirely and more about navigating its consequences with wit, adaptability, and luck.

#### Cultural and Religious Context
Brandobaris is especially associated with halfling adventurers, scouts, messengers, and those who live at the edges of settled life. His worship is informal, often indistinguishable from storytelling traditions about clever escapes, fortunate mistakes, and narrow survivals.

His influence appears most clearly in:

- Tales of unexpected success born from poor planning
- Stories of theft or mischief that resolve without lasting harm
- Accounts of exploration, travel, and boundary-crossing
- Cultural acceptance of “necessary rule-bending” when circumstances demand it

Temples are rare; shrines tend to be improvised or symbolic rather than formal, often consisting of small tokens, footprints carved into stone or wood, or objects associated with travel and concealment.

Brandobaris differs from other halfling deities in that he is not primarily concerned with maintaining stability, but with what happens when stability is briefly abandoned and then re-established. His role in the pantheon is therefore not oppositional, but exploratory.

#### Roleplaying Followers of Brandobaris
Followers of Brandobaris tend to be pragmatic risk-takers rather than reckless individuals. They value competence under pressure and tend to trust improvisation backed by experience.

Common traits include:

- Comfort with uncertainty and shifting plans
- Preference for indirect solutions over confrontation
- Curiosity about places, systems, and restricted knowledge
- Acceptance of minor rule-bending when outcomes justify it
- Strong focus on personal skill, timing, and perception
- Tendency to view failure as instructive rather than defining

They are often the halflings most likely to travel, but not necessarily the least rooted—many return home with experience that strengthens their communities rather than abandoning them.

#### Priests of Brandobaris

Priests of Brandobaris function less as traditional clergy and more as sanctioned practitioners of applied risk. They are expected to engage directly with the world, often in roles that blur the line between religious practice and adventuring profession.

Their responsibilities include:

- Acquiring knowledge through travel, infiltration, or exploration
- Testing boundaries of settlements, ruins, and social systems
- Retrieving or recovering items of value or significance
- Acting as intermediaries in situations requiring discretion
- Sharing accounts of failure, success, and adaptation for communal learning

They are not exempt from halfling communal expectations; rather, they interpret those expectations through lived experience beyond the home.

### Cyrrollalee (Intermediate Goddess)
Cyrrollalee is the halfling goddess of home, trust, and fellowship. She embodies the quiet, everyday duties that bind halfling communities together: caring for households, keeping promises, maintaining social bonds, and observing oaths. While Yondalla oversees protection and providence on a grand scale, Cyrrollalee is focused on the intimate sphere of domestic and interpersonal harmony.

She is widely invoked in minor rituals, often daily, to safeguard the household, ensure fair dealings among neighbors, and maintain trust between friends. Her moral framework emphasizes fidelity, honesty, and conscientious care for both people and property. Halflings understand Cyrrollalee’s guidance as a practical force: small acts of attentiveness, fairness, and vigilance collectively preserve the community.

#### Cultural and Religious Context

Cyrrollalee governs aspects of life that are personal, domestic, and social:

- Protection of the home and family
- Maintenance of trust, friendship, and neighborly bonds
- Observation of oaths, agreements, and promises
- Guidance for youth in social and moral education

Her worship is deeply integrated into daily life. Shrines are common in kitchens, front halls, and communal gathering places. Rituals may be as simple as blessings before a meal, words of reassurance to a visiting friend, or quiet observances when drafting agreements.

Cyrrollalee differs from other halfling deities by her emphasis on moral and social vigilance over adventurous or agricultural pursuits. She is the ethical steward of domestic life, promoting cooperation and monitoring honesty.

#### Roleplaying Followers of Cyrrollalee

Followers of Cyrrollalee are diligent, conscientious, and attentive to community well-being. They are guardians of trust and social order, not necessarily champions of battle or adventure.

Common traits include:

- Vigilance over homes, property, and agreements
- Strong commitment to honesty and reliability
- Respect for neighbors, friendships, and communal bonds
- Tendency to intervene gently to resolve disputes or enforce agreements
- Dedication to raising youth with values of responsibility and trust

They are rarely flamboyant, preferring practical, measured action to dramatic gestures.

#### Priests of Cyrrollalee

Priests serve as custodians of domestic and social order. Their duties include:

- Protecting households and neighborhoods
- Overseeing contracts, oaths, and social agreements
- Educating young halflings in practical and moral disciplines
- Performing blessings, minor rituals, and community observances

Unlike other halfling clergy, Cyrrollalee’s priests rarely adventure, focusing instead on the continuity and stability of daily life.

### Sheela Peryroyl (Intermediate Goddess)
Sheela Peryroyl is the halfling goddess of nature, weather, and agriculture, embodying the lived relationship between cultivated land and the wider natural world. Where Yondalla represents the hearth and Cyrrollalee the home, Sheela governs the fields, hedgerows, orchards, and seasonal cycles that sustain halfling communities.

Her domain is defined by balance rather than separation. Wild growth and cultivated land are not opposing forces in her view, but parts of a continuous system that must be tended, respected, and understood. She is also associated with celebration, seasonal joy, and romance, reflecting the halfling recognition that sustenance and pleasure are closely linked.

Sheela is understood not only as a caretaker of crops and weather, but as a presence within living growth itself. Her influence is felt in flourishing plants, changing seasons, and the subtle responsiveness of nature to care and neglect.

#### Cultural and Religious Context
Sheela Peryroyl governs the intersection between halfling livelihood and the natural world. Her worship is deeply embedded in agricultural practice and seasonal rhythms.

Her concerns include:

- Cultivation of crops and orchards
- Stewardship of wild and semi-wild land
- Seasonal cycles, weather patterns, and rainfall
- Fertility of soil and ecological balance
- Communal feasts tied to planting and harvest
- Romantic and celebratory expressions tied to seasonal change

Temples are typically open-air shrines, gardens, or integrated farmland rather than constructed buildings. Ritual practice is inseparable from labor: planting, harvesting, pruning, and tending are all forms of devotion when performed with respect and awareness.

Sheela’s faith reinforces a key halfling principle: prosperity depends on attentiveness and restraint. Land is not to be dominated, but cooperated with.

#### Roleplaying Followers of Sheela Peryroyl
Followers of Sheela tend to be attentive, grounded, and attuned to cycles of change. They are observant of subtle environmental shifts and place strong value on sustainable practices.

Common traits include:

- Respect for seasonal rhythms and natural timing
- Practical knowledge of farming, weather, and ecology
- Preference for sustainable, long-term cultivation over short-term yield
- Appreciation of beauty in natural growth and change
- Social warmth expressed through food, festivals, and shared labor
- Aversion to wasteful or destructive use of land

They often serve as quiet regulators of environmental balance within halfling communities, advising on when to plant, harvest, rest fields, or allow areas to return to wilderness.

#### Priests of Sheela Peryroyl
Priests of Sheela function as stewards of land and seasonal order. They oversee the agricultural cycle not as owners of nature, but as participants within it.

Their duties include:

- Supervising planting, harvesting, and land rotation
- Maintaining ecological balance between wild and cultivated areas
- Leading seasonal festivals, feasts, and rites of renewal
- Advising on weather patterns and environmental changes
- Protecting land from blight, overuse, or magical corruption

They are among the most outwardly visible halfling clergy during key seasonal moments, but otherwise remain integrated into daily rural life.

### Urogalan (Demigod)
Urogalan is the halfling demigod of earth and death, representing the stillness beneath the soil and the continuity of life beyond mortal existence. His nature is dual but unified: earth as foundation, and death as return. He is not a destructive force, but a custodial one, concerned with what is preserved, remembered, and gently reclaimed by the ground.

Within halfling belief, Urogalan is less widely worshipped than he is acknowledged. He is present in burial customs, ancestral remembrance, and the quiet respect given to the dead, but his imagery and symbols evoke unease. This reflects not rejection, but the halfling discomfort with finality and the unknown aspects of what lies beneath both earth and life.

He is understood as a necessary figure within the divine order: the one who receives what Yondalla gives and Sheela nurtures, ensuring that nothing is lost to meaninglessness.

#### Cultural and Religious Context
Urogalan governs aspects of existence that are hidden, foundational, and final. His influence is most strongly felt in practices surrounding burial, construction, and ancestral continuity.

His domains include:

- Burial rites and funerary customs
- Protection and guidance of the dead
- Earth as foundation, soil, and subterranean space
- Genealogy, lineage, and remembrance of ancestors
- Consecration of foundations and excavations

Worship of Urogalan is quiet, formal, and often ritualized rather than devotional. Shrines are commonly found in burial grounds, beneath communal halls, or within the first carved chambers of burrow-dwellings. His priests ensure that both the dead and the spaces built over them are treated with respect and continuity.

He differs from other halfling deities in that he does not concern himself with daily life or communal harmony, but with what underlies and outlasts it. His presence reinforces the idea that even the most comfortable and stable life rests upon deeper, often unseen structures.

#### Roleplaying Followers of Urogalan
Followers of Urogalan tend to be reserved, deliberate, and attentive to continuity across generations. They are less concerned with immediate comfort than with long-term memory and stability.

Common traits include:

- Respect for tradition, ancestry, and lineage
- Calm acceptance of mortality as part of natural order
- Preference for quiet, structured ritual over emotional display
- Careful attention to physical foundations and underground spaces
- Strong sense of responsibility toward the dead and their memory
- Interest in history, genealogy, and long-term community continuity

They are often viewed as solemn by other halflings, but not alienated; rather, they fulfill a necessary role that complements the more outward-facing aspects of halfling life.

#### Priests of Urogalan
Priests of Urogalan serve as custodians of burial, remembrance, and subterranean sanctity. They oversee the transition between life, death, and the earth that receives both.

Their duties include:

- Conducting funerary rites and burial ceremonies
- Maintaining burial sites and ancestral records
- Blessing foundations of buildings and burrow settlements
- Advising on excavation, tunneling, and underground safety
- Preserving genealogies and communal memory

They are also occasionally called upon to interpret omens related to death or subterranean disturbance, serving as intermediaries between the living community and what lies beneath.
